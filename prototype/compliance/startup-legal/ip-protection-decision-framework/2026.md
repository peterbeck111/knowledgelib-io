# IP Protection Decision Framework

## Purpose

This recipe produces an IP Protection Strategy Matrix that maps each of a startup's innovations, brands, and creative works to the optimal protection type (patent, trade secret, trademark, or copyright), with cost estimates, filing timelines, and priority rankings. The output is an actionable filing plan that prevents common mistakes like patenting what should be a trade secret, or neglecting trademark registration until a competitor files first.

## Prerequisites

- [ ] **Innovation inventory** — list of all products, processes, algorithms, brands, logos, content, and proprietary data the startup has created or plans to create
- [ ] **Business model description** — how each innovation generates revenue (direct sales, licensing, competitive moat)
- [ ] **USPTO.gov account** — free account at [USPTO](https://account.uspto.gov/) for trademark searches and filings
- [ ] **Budget allocation** — IP budget approved by founders (minimum $500 for basic protections, $5K-$15K+ for patent prosecution)
- [ ] **Legal counsel access** — patent attorney or IP law firm identified for complex filings (provisional patents can be self-filed, utility patents should not be)

## Constraints

- USPTO fees are current as of March 2026 and change annually. Always verify at [uspto.gov/fees](https://www.uspto.gov/learning-and-resources/fees-and-payment/uspto-fee-schedule) before filing. [src1]
- Patent applications require full public disclosure. Once published (18 months after filing), the information cannot be re-classified as a trade secret. This is irreversible. [src4]
- Trade secret protection requires "reasonable measures" to maintain secrecy under the Defend Trade Secrets Act (DTSA). Without NDAs, access controls, and documentation, courts will not enforce trade secret claims. [src3]
- Trademark rights in the US arise from use in commerce, not registration. However, federal registration provides nationwide priority, presumption of validity, and access to federal courts. [src2]
- Most startups qualify for micro entity status (80% USPTO fee reduction) or small entity status (50% reduction). Verify eligibility before filing. [src1]

## Tool Selection Decision

```

├── Brand name, logo, slogan, or product name
│   └── PATH A: Trademark Registration
├── Novel invention, device, process, or composition
│   ├── Can competitors reverse-engineer it?
│   │   ├── Yes → PATH B: Patent (provisional → utility)
│   │   └── No → PATH C: Trade Secret
├── Software, writing, design, music, or creative work
│   └── PATH D: Copyright Registration
├── Internal process, formula, algorithm, or data set (not reverse-engineerable)
│   └── PATH C: Trade Secret
└── Multiple types
    └── Evaluate each innovation separately through this tree
```

|------|----------------|---------------|---------------|----------|----------|
| A: Trademark | Federal trademark | $350/class | $650-$2,500 | 8-14 months | 10 years (renewable) |

| C: Trade Secret | NDA + internal controls | $0 | $500-$2,000 setup | Immediate | Indefinite (if maintained) |
| D: Copyright | Federal registration | $45-$65 | $0-$500 | 3-6 months | Life + 70 years / 95 years |

## Execution Flow

### Step 1: Inventory and Classify All IP Assets

**Duration**: 30-60 minutes
**Tool**: Spreadsheet

Catalog every piece of intellectual property the startup has or is developing. Classify each into one or more protection categories.

```
IP Asset Inventory:
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Asset Name          | Type           | Reverse-Eng? | Revenue Link        | Priority
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
{Product name}      | Brand          | N/A          | Customer trust      | High
{Core algorithm}    | Process        | No           | Competitive moat    | Critical
{Mobile app}        | Software       | Partially    | Direct revenue      | High
{Product design}    | Device         | Yes          | Direct revenue      | High
{Training data set} | Data           | No           | ML model accuracy   | Medium
{Marketing copy}    | Creative work  | N/A          | Brand awareness     | Low

Classification rules:
- If it is a name, logo, or slogan → Trademark
- If it is a novel, non-obvious invention that competitors can see → Patent
- If it is an internal process, formula, or data that competitors cannot see → Trade Secret
- If it is original creative expression (code, text, design, media) → Copyright
- Many assets need MULTIPLE protections (e.g., software = copyright + trade secret + possibly patent)
```

**Verify**: Every revenue-generating or moat-creating asset is listed. Nothing is left unclassified.
**If failed**: Interview each founder and engineer separately — people forget IP they work with daily.

### Step 2: Trademark Search and Filing

**Duration**: 1-2 hours for search; 8-14 months for registration
**Tool**: USPTO TESS, filing service or attorney

For each brand asset, search for conflicts, then file.

```
Trademark Filing Checklist:
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. SEARCH for conflicts:
   - USPTO TESS: https://tmsearch.uspto.gov/
   - Search exact name AND phonetic equivalents
   - Search in all relevant Nice classes
   - Check state trademark databases
   - Check domain name availability

2. DETERMINE filing basis:
   - Section 1(a): Already using the mark in commerce
   - Section 1(b): Intent to use (not yet in commerce — additional $150/class filing fee later)

3. IDENTIFY Nice classification:
   - Class 9: Software, apps, electronics
   - Class 35: Business services, advertising
   - Class 42: SaaS, technology services, software design
   - Full list: https://www.uspto.gov/trademarks/trademark-updates-and-announcements/nice-agreement

4. FILE application:
   - USPTO base filing fee: $350/class [src2]
   - Custom identification surcharge: +$200/class (if not using pre-approved ID)
   - Total DIY cost: $350-$550/class
   - With attorney: $1,000-$2,850/class total [src2]
   - With LegalZoom: $649 + $350 USPTO fee = ~$999/class

5. RESPOND to Office Actions (if issued):
   - ~30-40% of applications receive an Office Action
   - Attorney response cost: $500-$1,500 per Office Action
   - Response deadline: 3 months (extendable to 6 months for $125)
```

**Verify**: TESS search returns no identical or confusingly similar marks in relevant classes. Application accepted by USPTO (serial number issued).
**If failed**: If conflicts found, consult trademark attorney before filing. A rejected application wastes fees and may create estoppel issues.

### Step 3: Patent Strategy — Provisional vs. Utility

**Duration**: 1-4 weeks for provisional; 2-4 years for full prosecution
**Tool**: Patent attorney (utility), optionally self-file provisional

For each patentable invention, decide between provisional and full utility filing.

```
Patent Decision Matrix:
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Factor                    | Provisional Patent        | Utility Patent
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
USPTO filing fee (micro)  | $65                       | $455 (filing+search+exam)
USPTO filing fee (small)  | $130                      | $910
USPTO filing fee (large)  | $325                      | $2,000
Attorney cost             | $2,000-$5,000             | $7,500-$15,000+
Total cost range          | $2,065-$5,325             | $10,000-$20,000+
Timeline to file          | Days to weeks             | Weeks to months
Examination               | None — not examined       | 18-36 months to first action
Protection granted        | None (establishes date)   | 20 years from filing date
Disclosure required       | Yes (enables description) | Yes (full claims required)

When to file provisional first:
- Need "patent pending" status quickly
- Product launching within 12 months
- Want to test market before committing $10K+
- Technology still evolving (file utility later with improvements)

When to skip provisional and file utility directly:
- Technology is mature and well-defined
- Competitors are close to market
- Budget allows full prosecution
- International filing timeline demands it (PCT has strict deadlines)
```

**Verify**: Provisional application describes the invention in sufficient detail to support all planned utility claims. Patent attorney has confirmed patentability opinion.
**If failed**: If prior art search reveals blocking patents, pivot to trade secret protection for non-public aspects.

### Step 4: Trade Secret Protection Program

**Duration**: 1-2 days for setup; ongoing maintenance
**Tool**: NDA templates, access control systems

Establish formal trade secret protection for all non-public IP. This is legally required to enforce trade secret rights under the DTSA. [src3]

```
Trade Secret Protection Checklist:
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. IDENTIFY trade secrets:
   - Algorithms not visible in product
   - Internal processes and methodologies
   - Customer lists and pricing models
   - Training data and ML model weights
   - Supplier terms and business strategies

2. IMPLEMENT reasonable measures:
   - [ ] NDA for ALL employees (must include DTSA whistleblower notice) [src3]
   - [ ] NDA for ALL contractors and vendors
   - [ ] NDA for investors and potential partners (mutual NDA)
   - [ ] Access controls (role-based, need-to-know basis)
   - [ ] Document marking (label confidential materials)
   - [ ] Exit interviews with departing employees
   - [ ] Non-compete/non-solicit where enforceable (state-dependent)

3. DTSA WHISTLEBLOWER NOTICE (required in all employee NDAs):
   "An individual shall not be held criminally or civilly liable
   under any Federal or State trade secret law for the disclosure
   of a trade secret that is made (i) in confidence to a Federal,
   State, or local government official, either directly or indirectly,
   or to an attorney; and (ii) solely for the purpose of reporting or
   investigating a suspected violation of law."

4. ONGOING MAINTENANCE:
   - Annual audit of trade secret inventory
   - Update access controls when employees change roles
   - Review NDAs when laws change
   - Document all protection measures (critical for litigation)

Cost:
  - NDA template (attorney-drafted): $500-$2,000 one-time
  - Access control software: $0-$50/month
  - Annual audit: 2-4 hours of attorney time ($400-$1,600)
  - Total Year 1: $500-$2,000
  - Total ongoing: $400-$1,600/year
```

**Verify**: Every person with access to trade secrets has a signed NDA with DTSA notice. Access controls are documented and tested.
**If failed**: If any employee or contractor lacks an NDA, prioritize immediately. Missing NDAs can destroy trade secret status for the entire category of information shared with that person.

### Step 5: Copyright Registration

**Duration**: 30-60 minutes to file; 3-6 months for certificate
**Tool**: US Copyright Office eCO system

Register copyright for key creative works. While copyright exists automatically upon creation, registration is required to file infringement suits and enables statutory damages.

```
Copyright Registration Priorities:
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
High Priority (register immediately):
  - Source code for core product
  - Website content and documentation
  - Original design assets and UI

Medium Priority:
  - Marketing materials and brand content
  - Training materials and presentations
  - Blog posts and whitepapers

Low Priority (automatic protection sufficient):
  - Internal emails and memos
  - Meeting notes
  - Draft documents

Filing Process:
  1. Go to https://www.copyright.gov/registration/
  2. Create eCO account
  3. Select work type (literary work for software/text, visual art for designs)
  4. Complete application form
  5. Upload deposit copy (the work itself)
  6. Pay fee: $45 (single author, not work-for-hire) or $65 (standard) [src6]
  7. Processing time: 3-6 months standard, 2-3 months for online

Cost per registration:
  - Single work, single author: $45
  - Standard application: $65
  - Group of unpublished works (up to 10): $85
  - No attorney needed for straightforward registrations
```

**Verify**: Registration confirmation received for all high-priority works. Certificate number recorded in IP asset inventory.
**If failed**: If deposit copy rejected, verify file format meets Copyright Office requirements and resubmit.

### Step 6: Compile IP Strategy Matrix and Filing Calendar

**Duration**: 30 minutes

Consolidate all decisions into a single strategy document with deadlines.

```
IP STRATEGY MATRIX — {Company Name}
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Asset              | Protection  | Cost Est.    | File By      | Status
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Company name       | Trademark   | $999-$2,850  | Immediate    | ________
Product name       | Trademark   | $999-$2,850  | Pre-launch   | ________
Core algorithm     | Trade Secret| $500-$2,000  | Immediate    | ________
Product invention  | Patent (P)  | $2,065-$5,325| Pre-launch   | ________
Product invention  | Patent (U)  | $10K-$20K    | Within 12mo  | ________
Source code        | Copyright   | $65          | This month   | ________
Source code        | Trade Secret| Included     | Immediate    | ________
UI/UX designs      | Copyright   | $65          | This month   | ________
Logo/brand assets  | Trademark   | Included     | With name    | ________
Logo/brand assets  | Copyright   | $65          | This month   | ________
Training data      | Trade Secret| Included     | Immediate    | ________

FILING CALENDAR:
  Week 1: Trade secret program (NDAs, access controls)
  Week 2: Trademark applications (name + logo)
  Week 3: Copyright registrations (code, designs, content)
  Week 4: Provisional patent application (if applicable)
  Month 12: Convert provisional to utility patent
  Ongoing: Maintain trade secret program, respond to Office Actions
```

**Verify**: Every IP asset has at least one protection type assigned. Filing calendar has specific deadlines and responsible parties.
**If failed**: If budget insufficient for all filings, prioritize: trade secrets first (lowest cost, immediate), trademarks second (moderate cost, critical for brand), copyrights third (low cost, easy), patents last (highest cost, longest timeline).

## Error Handling

| Error | Likely Cause | Recovery Action |
|-------|-------------|----------------|
| Trademark application rejected (likelihood of confusion) | Similar mark exists in same class | Consult trademark attorney for design-around options or negotiate coexistence agreement |
| Provisional patent expires without utility filing | Missed 12-month deadline | File utility immediately if within grace period, or refile provisional (loses original priority date) |
| Trade secret leaked by employee | Missing or unenforceable NDA | Document the leak, consult attorney on DTSA federal claim or state UTSA claim, strengthen NDA program |
| Copyright registration rejected | Insufficient originality or wrong work category | Reclassify work type, add original elements if derivative, refile with attorney assistance |
| Patent examiner issues prior art rejection | Invention not novel or obvious over prior art | Work with patent attorney on claim amendments, consider narrower claims, or abandon and protect as trade secret |
| Budget insufficient for all filings | Underestimated total IP costs | Prioritize: trade secret (free setup) → trademark ($999) → copyright ($65) → patent ($2K-$15K+) |

## Anti-Patterns

### Wrong: Filing a patent for something that should be a trade secret
Startups often reflexively pursue patents for algorithms or internal processes that competitors cannot see or reverse-engineer. Patent filing requires full public disclosure — once your patent application is published (typically 18 months after filing), the information is permanently public, even if the patent is never granted. [src4]

### Correct: Use the reverse-engineerability test
If competitors cannot discover the innovation by examining your product, trade secret protection is usually superior. It costs less, takes effect immediately, and lasts indefinitely. Reserve patents for inventions that are visible in the product or can be independently discovered. [src4]

### Wrong: Delaying trademark filing until "the brand is established"
Many startups wait to file trademarks until they have significant revenue or brand recognition. During this delay, a competitor or trademark squatter can register the same or confusingly similar mark, forcing a costly opposition proceeding or rebrand. [src7]

### Correct: File trademark applications before or at launch
File as soon as you commit to a name, even on an intent-to-use basis (Section 1(b)). The $350 filing fee is trivial compared to the cost of rebranding ($50K-$500K+ for an established brand). [src2]

### Wrong: Relying on copyright alone for software protection
Copyright protects the expression of code (literal copying), not the underlying functionality. A competitor can legally replicate your software's features as long as they write their own code. [src7]

### Correct: Layer protections for software
Combine copyright registration (protects code expression, $65), trade secret protection (protects proprietary algorithms and data), and potentially patent protection (protects novel functional methods, if applicable).

This recipe is most valuable pre-launch or within the first year of operations, when IP decisions have the highest leverage and lowest correction cost.
