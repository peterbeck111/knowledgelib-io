# Dodd-Frank Act Key Provisions: Volcker Rule, Derivatives Reform, and Resolution Planning

## Rule

The Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act (Pub. L. 111-203, enacted July 21, 2010) imposes a comprehensive regulatory framework on US financial institutions. Its core provisions prohibit banking entities from proprietary trading (Volcker Rule, Section 619), require registration and margin/clearing for over-the-counter swap dealers (Title VII), mandate resolution plans ("living wills") for systemically important financial institutions with $250 billion or more in consolidated assets (Title I, Section 165(d)), and established the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau for consumer financial product oversight (Title X). Entities must determine which provisions apply based on their charter type, asset size, and swap dealing activity before assessing specific obligations. [src1, src2]

## Evidence

Since the Volcker Rule's compliance deadline in July 2015, five federal agencies (OCC, Federal Reserve, FDIC, SEC, CFTC) jointly enforce the proprietary trading ban, with the 2019 final amendments streamlining compliance by tailoring obligations to three tiers based on trading activity. Community banks with under $10 billion in total consolidated assets and trading assets/liabilities below 5% of total assets are excluded entirely. Under Title VII, the CFTC requires swap dealer registration for entities exceeding a $3 billion de minimis threshold in aggregate gross notional swap dealing over 12 months; as of 2022, 76% of interest rate derivatives and 88% of credit default swaps are centrally cleared through DCOs. The 2018 Economic Growth, Regulatory Relief, and Consumer Protection Act raised the SIFI asset threshold from $50 billion to $250 billion, removing approximately two dozen firms from automatic enhanced prudential standards. The CFPB's statutory funding cap was halved from 12% to 6.5% of the Federal Reserve's 2009 operating expenses by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (signed July 4, 2025), and the administration proposed reducing staff from 1,689 to 207 positions, though courts have partially blocked the reductions. [src1, src2, src3, src4, src6]

## Key Properties

- **Statutory citation**: Pub. L. 111-203, 124 Stat. 1376 (July 21, 2010)
- **Volcker Rule scope**: Prohibits proprietary trading and covered fund activities by banking entities; exemptions for market-making, underwriting, hedging, and government obligations [src3]
- **Swap dealer de minimis threshold**: $3 billion aggregate gross notional over 12 months (below this, no registration required) [src2]
- **SIFI asset threshold**: $250 billion in total consolidated assets (raised from $50B by the 2018 Economic Growth Act); Fed retains discretion for $100B-$250B [src4]
- **Resolution plan filers**: Bank holding companies with $250B+ assets must submit living wills to FDIC and Federal Reserve under Section 165(d) [src8]
- **CFPB funding (post-July 2025)**: Capped at 6.5% of Federal Reserve 2009 operating expenses, approximately halving prior authorization [src6]
- **Enforcement agencies**: SEC, CFTC, FDIC, OCC, Federal Reserve, CFPB, FSOC

## Conditions

- **Applies when**: The entity is a US-chartered bank, bank holding company, savings association, US branch or agency of a foreign bank, or any person meeting the swap dealer or major swap participant definitions under CFTC/SEC rules; also applies to nonbank financial companies designated as systemically important by FSOC
- **Does NOT apply when**: Community banks under $10 billion in total consolidated assets (exempt from Volcker Rule); entities below the $3 billion de minimis swap dealing threshold (exempt from swap dealer registration); non-financial commercial end-users hedging commercial risk (exempt from clearing requirements under Title VII)
- **Confidence degrades when**: The current administration's deregulatory agenda produces further statutory changes or agency guidance reversals; CFPB litigation outcomes are unresolved; FSOC reconsiders its 2025 review of nonbank SIFI designation guidance

## Constraints

- US federal law only -- does not apply to purely non-US institutions with no US nexus; cross-border swap dealing triggers CFTC registration based on the de minimis calculation for US-person-facing activity [src2, src7]
- The CFPB's enforcement capacity is severely diminished as of early 2026: 16 of 34 pending enforcement actions dismissed, staffing proposed to be cut 88%; a December 2025 court order requires continued funding, but operational capability remains uncertain [src6]
- The Orderly Liquidation Authority (Title II) has never been invoked; its real-world effectiveness is untested; activation requires two-thirds vote of the Federal Reserve Board plus Treasury Secretary approval (consulting the President) [src5]
- FSOC announced in September 2025 its intention to review nonbank SIFI designation guidance, potentially narrowing the scope of designations [src4]
- Title VII jurisdiction is split: CFTC oversees swaps (interest rate, commodity, credit default); SEC oversees security-based swaps (single-name CDS, equity swaps); mixed swaps require joint regulation [src7]

## Rationale

The Dodd-Frank Act was enacted in direct response to the 2007-2008 financial crisis, which exposed systemic risks from unregulated OTC derivatives (particularly credit default swaps), excessive proprietary trading by deposit-taking banks, the absence of a non-bankruptcy resolution mechanism for failing SIFIs, and inadequate consumer financial protection. The law's architecture addresses each root cause: the Volcker Rule separates speculative risk from insured deposits, Title VII brings transparency and central clearing to $400+ trillion in notional OTC derivatives, Title I's resolution planning requirement forces pre-crisis contingency preparation, and Title X created the CFPB to consolidate fragmented consumer protection authority. [src1, src5]

## Application Checklist

### Step 1: Classify the entity and determine applicable titles
- **Inputs needed**: Entity charter type (national bank, state-chartered, BHC, savings association, foreign banking organization), total consolidated assets, swap dealing volume, whether designated as SIFI by FSOC
- **Output**: List of applicable Dodd-Frank titles and specific provisions
- **Constraint**: Do not apply Volcker Rule to community banks under $10B with trading assets below 5% of total assets -- they are excluded by the 2019 amendments [src3]

### Step 2: Assess Volcker Rule obligations (if applicable)
- **Inputs needed**: Trading desk inventory, proprietary trading activity, covered fund relationships (hedge funds, private equity funds), market-making and underwriting justifications
- **Output**: Compliance program classification (significant, moderate, or limited trading entity) and documentation of permitted activities
- **Constraint**: All proprietary trading is prohibited unless an exemption applies (market-making, underwriting, hedging, government obligations, insurance company activities); burden of proving exemption is on the entity [src3]

### Step 3: Assess Title VII swap obligations (if applicable)
- **Inputs needed**: Aggregate gross notional swap dealing volume over trailing 12 months, counterparty types, clearing eligibility of swap products
- **Output**: Determination of swap dealer or major swap participant status; if registered, compliance with capital, margin, reporting, business conduct, and clearing requirements
- **Constraint**: The $3 billion de minimis threshold is calculated on dealing activity only; hedging and proprietary positions are excluded; CFTC staff interpretive letters on cross-border calculation must be reviewed for non-US entities [src2, src7]

### Step 4: Assess resolution planning obligations (if applicable)
- **Inputs needed**: Total consolidated assets, firm complexity category (Category I-IV), most recent resolution plan submission and feedback
- **Output**: Resolution plan or living will demonstrating rapid and orderly resolution under the US Bankruptcy Code; public and confidential sections
- **Constraint**: Plans must be submitted to both the FDIC and Federal Reserve on a cycle determined by firm category; joint agency feedback letters may require remediation before the next submission; escalate to legal counsel if the plan receives a deficiency notice [src8]

### Step 5: Monitor CFPB and FSOC developments
- **Inputs needed**: Current enforcement posture, pending litigation (e.g., December 2025 court order on funding), proposed FSOC designation guidance changes
- **Output**: Updated compliance risk assessment reflecting operational status of CFPB and FSOC designation pipeline
- **Constraint**: CFPB enforcement capability is substantially reduced but not eliminated; do not assume zero enforcement risk; a court order requires continued funding and operations [src6]

## Anti-Patterns

### Wrong: Assuming community banks are fully exempt from Dodd-Frank
Small banks sometimes assume Dodd-Frank does not apply to them at all. While the Volcker Rule exempts banks under $10 billion in assets with limited trading, other provisions (consumer protection, Bank Secrecy Act amendments, mortgage reform under Titles X and XIV) still apply to institutions of all sizes. [src1]

### Correct: Conduct a title-by-title applicability analysis
Even community banks must assess compliance with applicable Dodd-Frank titles. The Volcker Rule exemption is specific to Section 619; other titles have different or no asset thresholds. [src3]

### Wrong: Treating the $250 billion SIFI threshold as a bright line with no obligations below it
Firms between $100 billion and $250 billion may assume they are entirely exempt from enhanced prudential standards. The Federal Reserve retains discretionary authority to apply enhanced standards to firms in the $100B-$250B range, and did so for several firms following the 2023 regional bank failures. [src4]

### Correct: Monitor Fed discretionary actions for the $100B-$250B range
Review Federal Reserve guidance and orders for your asset size category. The 2018 Economic Growth Act gave the Fed discretion, not a blanket exemption, for firms between $100B and $250B. [src1]

### Wrong: Registering as a swap dealer based on total swap notional without distinguishing dealing from hedging
Some entities over-count their swap activity by including hedging positions in the de minimis threshold calculation, triggering unnecessary registration and compliance costs. [src2]

### Correct: Calculate de minimis on dealing activity only
The $3 billion threshold counts only swap dealing activity (transactions entered into as an accommodation to counterparties). Exclude bona fide hedging and positions taken for investment purposes. Consult CFTC staff guidance on the dealing/hedging distinction. [src7]

## Counter-Arguments

- Critics argue the Volcker Rule is unnecessarily complex and reduces market liquidity, citing a 2017 Federal Reserve study showing decreased market-making capacity in corporate bonds since implementation. Proponents counter that the 2019 simplification amendments addressed the most burdensome requirements while preserving the core prohibition. [src3]
- The 2018 Economic Growth Act's increase of the SIFI threshold from $50B to $250B was criticized for removing prudential oversight of mid-size banks, a concern validated by the March 2023 failures of Silicon Valley Bank ($209B) and Signature Bank ($110B), both of which fell below the $250B threshold. [src4]
- Supporters of CFPB restructuring argue the Bureau was insufficiently accountable due to its unique self-funding mechanism; opponents cite $19 billion in estimated consumer harm from the enforcement pullback and note that courts have ordered continued operations. [src6]

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: The Volcker Rule bans all trading by banks.
  **Reality**: The Volcker Rule prohibits only proprietary trading -- trading for the bank's own profit. It explicitly permits market-making, underwriting, hedging, trading in government obligations, and insurance company portfolio activities. The 2019 amendments introduced a three-tier compliance framework that reduces the burden on entities with limited trading activity. [src3]

- **Misconception**: All OTC derivatives must be centrally cleared under Dodd-Frank.
  **Reality**: Only swaps that the CFTC or SEC has determined are required to be cleared must go through a derivatives clearing organization. Non-financial commercial end-users hedging commercial risk are exempt from mandatory clearing. Bespoke or illiquid swaps that no DCO will accept for clearing remain bilateral. [src2, src7]

- **Misconception**: The CFPB has been abolished.
  **Reality**: As of early 2026, the CFPB's funding has been halved and staffing drastically reduced, but the agency has not been abolished. A December 2025 federal court order requires the administration to continue seeking funding for the Bureau. The CFPB remains a Congressionally-created agency that cannot be eliminated without an act of Congress. [src6]

- **Misconception**: The Orderly Liquidation Authority means the government will bail out failing banks.
  **Reality**: The OLA is explicitly not a bailout mechanism. Under Title II, all losses are borne by the private sector -- shareholders are wiped out, creditors take losses, and if the resolution has a net cost, surviving large financial institutions are assessed a fee to recoup it. The Treasury provides temporary liquidity, not permanent capital. [src5]
