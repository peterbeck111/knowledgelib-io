Summary

The best laptop for video editing in 2026 is the Apple MacBook Pro 16-inch with M5 Max ($3,899+), now widely available since shipping March 11, 2026. The M5 Max features an 18-core CPU with six “super cores,” up to a 40-core GPU with Neural Accelerators, and up to 128 GB unified memory with 614 GB/s bandwidth. PetaPixel calls it “better than your desktop,” with DaVinci Resolve Studio video effects rendering up to 3x faster than the M4 Max and RAW video processing scores nearly double the previous best. SSDs reach 14.5 GB/s read/write. Tom’s Guide (updated March 2026) ranks the M5 Pro 16-inch as its #1 overall pick, scoring 105,580 on PugetBench Premiere Pro. [src1, src2, src7, src8]

On Windows, the ASUS ProArt P16 (H7606, 2025 refresh) remains the top creator laptop at $2,899 (street prices as low as $2,399 at Best Buy), equipped with an RTX 5070 (8 GB GDDR7) and a 2.8K 120 Hz OLED touchscreen. Creative Bloq rates it 9/10, though NotebookCheck notes the RTX 5070 delivers only 6-12% gains over the RTX 4070 in creator workloads. The Razer Blade 16 with RTX 5080 ($3,500) or RTX 5090 ($4,500) offers the highest VRAM on Windows but benchmarks show diminishing returns at the top end. Dell has rebranded its Precision workstation line to “Dell Pro Max” — the Pro Max 16 Premium replaces the Precision 5690 with RTX PRO Blackwell GPUs. [src4, src5, src6]

Key decision factors remain GPU VRAM (critical for DaVinci Resolve), unified memory (32 GB minimum for 4K, 64 GB for multicam/RAW), display color accuracy (100% DCI-P3 minimum), and SSD speed for scrubbing timelines. The M5 generation brings PCIe 5.0 SSDs, Wi-Fi 7, and Thunderbolt 5 across all Pro/Max models. NotebookCheck measured 20% CPU and 30-50% GPU gains for the M5 Pro over M4 Pro — substantial enough to justify upgrading from M3 or earlier, though M4 owners may find less urgency. M5 Pro street prices have dropped $100-$200 below MSRP as of April 2026, with the 48 GB model hitting a record low of $2,899. [src1, src3, src7]

Best for Each Use Case

Best Overall: MacBook Pro 16-inch (M5 Max) (~$3,899) — Check price

The new consensus top pick for video editing. The M5 Max’s 18-core CPU with six super cores and 40-core GPU with per-core Neural Accelerators delivers transformative performance: PetaPixel measured RAW video processing scores nearly double the previous best, and DaVinci Resolve video effects rendering runs up to 3x faster than M4 Max. The 16.2-inch Liquid Retina XDR display (1,000 nits sustained, 1,600 nits HDR peak, full DCI-P3) remains industry-leading. Up to 128 GB unified memory at 614 GB/s bandwidth handles 8K ProRes RAW without proxies. Three Thunderbolt 5 ports, Wi-Fi 7, and PCIe 5.0 SSDs reaching 14.5 GB/s round out the package. Battery life reaches 24 hours for general use. [src1, src2, src8]

Best Value macOS Pro: MacBook Pro 16-inch (M5 Pro) (~$2,699) — Check price

NotebookCheck rates it 92% (Editors’ Choice), calling it “even better” than the already excellent M4 Pro model. The M5 Pro delivers 20% CPU and 30-50% GPU gains over M4 Pro, with an 18-core CPU and 20-core GPU that handle 4K multicam editing with ease. Base configuration starts with 1 TB SSD and includes Thunderbolt 5 and Wi-Fi 7. PCIe 5.0 storage delivers dramatically faster import/export speeds. At $2,699 it undercuts the M5 Max by $1,200 while delivering roughly 70-80% of its performance for 4K workflows. [src1, src3]

Best macOS Portable: MacBook Pro 14-inch (M5 Pro) (~$2,199) — Check price

The same M5 Pro chip in a 3.5-lb chassis for editors who prioritize portability. At 0.61 inches thick, it fits in camera bags while delivering the full M5 Pro performance envelope. The 14.2-inch Liquid Retina XDR display provides the same P3 color accuracy as the 16-inch. Configuring with 48 or 64 GB unified memory is recommended for DaVinci Resolve Fusion nodes and After Effects compositions. The $500 savings over the 16-inch model buys a smaller screen but identical compute performance. [src1, src3]

Best Windows Laptop for Creators: ASUS ProArt P16 (H7606, 2025) (~$2,899) — Check price

Creative Bloq awards it 9/10, praising it as “extremely powerful” with a “brilliant touchscreen.” The 2025 refresh swaps the 4K 60 Hz OLED for a 2.8K 120 Hz OLED panel — smoother for timeline scrubbing but slightly lower resolution. The RTX 5070 (8 GB GDDR7) and AMD Ryzen AI 9 HX 370 with 50 TOPS NPU accelerate AI features in Premiere Pro (Scene Edit Detection, Auto Reframe) and DaVinci Resolve (Magic Mask, Super Scale). ASUS Dial provides intuitive parameter control. At 4.01 lbs with Pantone Validation, it remains the best Windows creator laptop. [src4, src5]

Best for DaVinci Resolve / GPU-Heavy Workflows: Razer Blade 16 (2025) (~$3,500) — Check price

DaVinci Resolve is GPU-bound, making the Razer Blade 16 with RTX 5080 (12 GB VRAM) at $3,500 the sweet spot for Windows Resolve users. The RTX 5090 model at $4,500 shows only up to 13% gains over the 5080 — NotebookCheck notes it “trails RTX 5080 laptops despite a higher price” in some scenarios. The 16-inch QHD+ 240 Hz OLED display covers full DCI-P3, and at 4.6 lbs it is remarkably thin for this performance class. Over 7 hours of web-browsing battery life is competitive. The RTX 5080 offers the best price-to-VRAM ratio for Resolve’s GPU-accelerated color grading. [src6, src7]

Best Enterprise Workstation: Dell Pro Max 16 Premium (~$2,500) — Check price

Dell has rebranded its Precision workstation line to “Dell Pro Max.” The Pro Max 16 Premium replaces the Precision 5690 with Intel Core Ultra 9 285H, NVIDIA RTX PRO 2000/3000 Blackwell GPUs (8-12 GB GDDR7), and a 4K 120 Hz OLED touchscreen. StorageReview measured 130 fps CPU and 184 fps OpenCL on Blackmagic RAW 8K playback. The magnesium build feels premium, CAMM2 RAM is user-upgradeable (rare for this class), and two M.2 SSD slots support RAID 0/1. ISV-certified with Intel vPro security and enterprise-grade support. [src7]

Best Budget Option: ASUS Vivobook Pro 15 OLED (~$1,299) — Check price

The most affordable OLED + dedicated GPU combination for video editing. The 3K 120 Hz OLED panel is Pantone Validated with 100% DCI-P3 — color accuracy rivaling laptops costing twice as much. The Intel Core Ultra 9 185H paired with RTX 4060 handles 4K timeline editing in Premiere Pro and DaVinci Resolve smoothly. Main limitations: 24 GB non-upgradeable RAM and shorter battery life. Ideal for freelancers and YouTubers who need professional color accuracy at an accessible price. [src4, src7]

Best Budget macOS: MacBook Air 15-inch (M4) (~$1,199) — Check price

The fanless M4 Air handles 1080p editing effortlessly and manages 4K timelines with proxy workflows. The 15.3-inch Liquid Retina display covers full P3, and silent operation is a genuine advantage for on-location editing near microphones. At 3.3 lbs with 18-24 hours battery life, it is the most portable option on this list. The limitation is no dedicated GPU: complex DaVinci Resolve color grading and multicam 4K with heavy effects will strain the 10-core GPU. Best for 1080p-4K editing without intensive effects. [src7]

Decision Logic

If budget < $1,500

→ MacBook Air 15-inch M4 (~$1,199) for macOS/Final Cut Pro, or ASUS Vivobook Pro 15 OLED (~$1,299) for Windows/Premiere Pro/DaVinci Resolve. Both offer P3-class displays. The MacBook Air trades GPU power for battery life and silence; the Vivobook trades battery for a dedicated RTX 4060. [src4, src7]

If primary software is Final Cut Pro

→ MacBook Pro is the only option — Final Cut Pro is macOS-exclusive. M5 Max 16-inch for professional 4K/8K work, M5 Pro 14-inch for portability, M4 Air for budget 1080p/4K. Apple Silicon’s hardware ProRes encoder gives a permanent speed advantage in FCP, and M5 Max AI tools (beat detection, smart montage) run dramatically faster. [src1, src2]

If primary software is DaVinci Resolve

→ Prioritize GPU VRAM over CPU cores. DaVinci Resolve is GPU-bound for color grading, Fusion, and rendering. Razer Blade 16 with RTX 5080 (12 GB) at $3,500 is the best Windows value. The M5 Max with 40-core GPU and 128 GB unified memory is the macOS pick, delivering up to 3x the Resolve performance of M4 Max. [src2, src6]

If primary software is Premiere Pro

→ Premiere Pro benefits from balanced CPU-GPU with strong NPU for AI features. The M5 Pro MacBook Pro ($2,199-$2,699) or ASUS ProArt P16 ($2,899) are the top picks. Enhance Speech runs 4x faster on M5 than M1, and the Ryzen AI 9’s 50 TOPS NPU accelerates Scene Edit Detection and Auto Reframe locally. [src1, src4, src5]

If user needs ISV certification for enterprise

→ Dell Pro Max 16 Premium or HP ZBook Studio G11. Both carry ISV certification for Premiere Pro, DaVinci Resolve, and After Effects. Dell offers vPro remote management and CAMM2 upgradeable RAM; HP offers DreamColor factory-calibrated displays. The HP ZBook Studio G12 launches August 2026 — wait if timing allows. [src7]

If portability is the top priority

→ MacBook Pro 14-inch M5 Pro (3.5 lbs, 0.61" thick) for professional performance, or MacBook Air 15-inch M4 (3.3 lbs, fanless) for lighter editing. Both deliver 18+ hours of battery life for non-rendering tasks and include Thunderbolt connectivity. [src1, src3]

Default recommendation

→ MacBook Pro 16-inch M5 Max (~$3,899). The new consensus top pick — PetaPixel, Tom’s Hardware (4.5/5), NotebookCheck, and Creative Bloq all recommend it as the best video editing laptop available. If Windows is required, the ASUS ProArt P16 (~$2,899) is the best alternative. If budget is tight, the M5 Pro 16-inch ($2,699) delivers 70-80% of the performance. [src1, src2, src3, src8]

  • M5 Pro/Max prices stabilizing: Apple’s M5 Max (March 2026) delivers up to 3x faster DaVinci Resolve rendering and nearly 2x RAW processing versus M4 Max. As of April 2026, M5 Pro street prices have dropped $100-$200 below MSRP — the 48 GB M5 Pro 16-inch hit $2,899 (record low). M5 Max supply has normalized. Tom’s Guide rates the M5 Pro 16-inch as #1 overall with 105,580 on PugetBench Premiere Pro. [src1, src2, src3, src7]
  • Dell rebrands Precision to Pro Max: Dell’s Precision mobile workstation line is now “Dell Pro Max.” The Pro Max 16 Premium replaces the Precision 5690 with RTX PRO Blackwell GPUs (RTX PRO 1000/2000/3000), CAMM2 upgradeable RAM, and a 4K 120 Hz OLED touchscreen. Enterprise buyers should transition to the new naming. [src7]
  • RTX 50-series shows modest gains for creators: NVIDIA’s Blackwell-architecture RTX 5070/5080/5090 laptop GPUs deliver only 6-12% performance gains over RTX 40-series in creative workloads, despite significant gaming improvements. The RTX 5080 at $3,500 offers the best value; the RTX 5090 at $4,500 shows diminishing returns. [src5, src6]
  • 32 GB RAM is the new baseline: 32 GB is the safe minimum for smooth 4K editing across all NLEs. For color grading, VFX compositing, or RAW workflows, 64 GB or more is strongly recommended. Apple’s unified memory architecture gives MacBook Pros an efficiency edge. [src3, src7]
  • AI-accelerated editing matures: NPUs in Apple M5, AMD Ryzen AI 9, and Intel Core Ultra processors now run AI editing features locally — Premiere Pro’s Enhance Speech (4x faster on M5 vs M1), DaVinci Resolve’s Magic Mask and Super Scale, and Final Cut Pro’s beat detection and smart montage. [src1, src4]
  • OLED displays dominate the creator segment: Nearly every premium video editing laptop offers OLED with 100% DCI-P3. The trend is toward higher refresh rates (120-240 Hz) over higher resolution — the ProArt P16 traded 4K 60 Hz for 2.8K 120 Hz. Self-emitting OLED pixels deliver infinite contrast critical for HDR grading. [src4, src5]

Important Caveats

  • Prices listed are US MSRP as of April 2026. Street prices are now $100-$200 below MSRP for M5 Pro models. Educational discounts (Apple offers $150-$300 savings) and regional pricing vary. The top M5 Max 16-inch config reaches ~$6,149.
  • Video editing performance varies dramatically by software. Final Cut Pro is optimized for Apple Silicon. DaVinci Resolve favors GPU VRAM and compute. Premiere Pro benefits from balanced CPU-GPU with NPU acceleration. Always benchmark with your primary application.
  • Display specifications matter for color-critical work, but no laptop display fully replaces a dedicated reference monitor for broadcast or cinema color grading. Factory calibration drifts over time — periodic recalibration with a hardware colorimeter is recommended.
  • Battery life figures are manufacturer-stated for general use. Active video editing, rendering, and export reduce battery life by 50-70%. Plan for power access during intensive editing sessions.
  • Storage is not expandable in most ultrabook-style laptops (MacBook Pro, Razer Blade). The Dell Pro Max 16 Premium is a notable exception with two M.2 SSD slots supporting RAID 0/1. External Thunderbolt 5 SSDs can supplement, but editing from external drives introduces latency versus internal NVMe.
  • NVIDIA Studio Drivers provide better stability for creative applications than Game Ready Drivers. Windows users should switch to Studio Drivers for production editing.
  • Dell has rebranded the Precision mobile workstation line to “Dell Pro Max.” The Precision 5690 is being replaced by the Dell Pro Max 16 Premium. Existing Precision 5690 units remain excellent if purchased at a discount.
  • HP ZBook Studio G12 launches August 2026. The G11 remains the current recommended ISV-certified HP workstation.
