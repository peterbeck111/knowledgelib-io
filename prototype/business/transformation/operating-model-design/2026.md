# Operating Model Design

## Definition

A target operating model (TOM) is the blueprint that describes how an organization delivers value -- connecting its strategy to its people, processes, technology, and governance structures. McKinsey defines an effective operating model as one designed to deliver four measurable outcomes: clarity (who does what), speed (how fast decisions move), skills (right capabilities in right roles), and commitment (employee engagement with the model). Designing a TOM involves activity analysis (what work must be done), organizational layering (where work is performed and by whom), and defined deliverables (what the model produces at each level). [src1]

## Key Properties

- **McKinsey's four outcomes**: Clarity, speed, skills, and commitment -- an effective TOM must deliver all four simultaneously [src1]
- **Design layers**: Strategy and design principles, value chain map, organizational structure, process architecture, technology platform, role design, KPIs, supplier contracts [src5]
- **McKinsey evolution**: From the classic 7-S framework (Strategy, Structure, Systems, Shared values, Style, Staff, Skills) to the "Organize to Value" system with 12 dynamic elements [src2]
- **Deloitte's six dimensions**: Process, people, service delivery, technology, data, and governance -- each must be explicitly designed and connected to strategy [src3]
- **Typical design timeline**: 8-16 weeks for design phase; 6-18 months for implementation; operating model redesigns occur every 3-5 years on average [src4]
- **Common triggers**: M&A integration, digital transformation, cost pressure, new strategy, regulatory change, or market disruption [src1]

## Constraints

- **Strategy-first requirement**: Operating model design fails when disconnected from strategy. The model must flow from strategic choices (where to compete, how to win), not from organizational convenience or historical structure. Organizations that design operating models without a clear strategy deliver 50% less value. [src1]
- **Implementation dominance**: Design accounts for only 20% of the effort in an operating model transformation. Implementation -- change management, leadership alignment, iterative refinement, and adoption -- is where most transformations fail. [src2]
- **Differentiation, not uniformity**: Different parts of the organization often need different operating models. Shared services, innovation labs, and customer-facing units have fundamentally different requirements. Forcing uniformity destroys value in diverse organizations. [src4]
- **Redesign frequency**: Operating model redesigns typically occur every 3-5 years. More frequent changes create "reorganization fatigue" with 15-25% productivity loss during each transition period. [src2]
- **Cross-functional ownership**: Operating model changes owned by a single function (IT, HR, finance) produce incomplete designs. Effective TOM design requires cross-functional design authority with executive sponsorship spanning all six Deloitte dimensions. [src3]

## Application Checklist

1. **Define strategic design principles** (Weeks 1-4)
   - Inputs: Corporate strategy, competitive analysis, customer value proposition, McKinsey's four outcomes (clarity, speed, skills, commitment)
   - Output: 5-8 design principles that translate strategy into operating model requirements (e.g., "customer-centric decision-making within 48 hours")
   - Constraint: Design principles must be approved by the executive committee before detailed design begins
   - Success metric: Design principles agreed and documented; baseline assessment of current model against all four McKinsey outcomes

2. **Map value chain and design target model** (Weeks 4-12)
   - Inputs: Design principles, Deloitte's six dimensions (process, people, service delivery, technology, data, governance), current-state assessment
   - Output: Target operating model blueprint covering all six dimensions with explicit design choices per dimension
   - Constraint: Each dimension must be explicitly designed -- "carry forward" is a valid choice but must be deliberate, not default
   - Success metric: TOM blueprint reviewed by all affected business unit leaders; gap analysis completed

3. **Design governance and decision rights** (Weeks 8-14)
   - Inputs: TOM blueprint, RACI matrices, decision rights framework, escalation protocols
   - Output: Governance model defining who decides what, at which level, with what authority and accountability
   - Constraint: Governance must address the top 20 most consequential decisions explicitly (McKinsey recommends defining decision rights for the highest-value decisions)
   - Success metric: Decision rights map validated through scenario testing; escalation paths clear

4. **Implement in waves with change management** (Months 3-18)
   - Inputs: Implementation plan, change management program (Kotter/ADKAR), training and communication materials, pilot plan
   - Output: Operating model changes rolled out in 2-3 waves with measured adoption
   - Constraint: First wave should be the most visible, highest-impact change to build momentum
   - Success metric: McKinsey's four outcomes improving in each wave; employee adoption metrics tracked

5. **Stabilize and refine** (Months 12-24)
   - Inputs: Adoption metrics, performance data, feedback from all levels, continuous improvement mechanisms
   - Output: Operating model fully operational with built-in adaptation mechanisms
   - Constraint: Plan for 6-12 months of stabilization before declaring the redesign complete
   - Success metric: All four McKinsey outcomes (clarity, speed, skills, commitment) measurably improved vs. baseline

## Anti-Patterns

- **Wrong**: Starting with the org chart (boxes and lines) and working outward to the operating model.
  **Right**: Start with strategy and design principles, then design processes and governance, then structure. Structure follows process, process follows strategy. [src1]

- **Wrong**: Designing the operating model in a consulting engagement without involving the people who will operate it.
  **Right**: Co-design with cross-functional teams from the operating level. McKinsey finds that operating models co-designed with frontline leaders have 2x higher adoption rates. [src2]

- **Wrong**: Designing a single, uniform operating model for a diversified organization.
  **Right**: Allow differentiated models for different business units while maintaining coherence at the enterprise level. The key question is "what must be common vs. what must be different." [src4]

- **Wrong**: Treating operating model design as a one-time project with a "go-live" date.
  **Right**: Build in adaptation mechanisms (quarterly reviews, continuous feedback, performance monitoring) so the model evolves with strategy and market conditions. [src2]

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: An operating model is the same as an org chart.
  **Reality**: An org chart shows reporting lines only. An operating model defines how work flows across the organization, where decisions are made, what technology enables processes, and how governance ensures quality. Structure is one of six or more dimensions. [src1]

- **Misconception**: Designing the model is the hard part; implementation follows naturally.
  **Reality**: McKinsey's research shows that implementation -- not design -- is where most operating model transformations fail. Change management, leadership alignment, and iterative refinement during rollout are the critical success factors. [src2]

- **Misconception**: One operating model design fits all business units.
  **Reality**: Different parts of the organization often need different operating models. A shared-services function requires a different model than an innovation lab or a customer-facing business unit. The key is coherence, not uniformity. [src4]
