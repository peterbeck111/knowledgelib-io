# Insurance Requirements for Startups

## Purpose

This recipe produces a stage-appropriate insurance portfolio for a startup — a prioritized list of policies with coverage limits, estimated annual premiums, broker recommendations, and a binding timeline. The output is a concrete insurance budget and action plan that satisfies legal requirements, investor covenants, and customer contract obligations, ready for broker engagement and policy binding.

## Prerequisites

- [ ] **Legal entity formed** — C-corp, LLC, or other entity registered with the state (insurance is bound to the entity, not founders personally)
- [ ] **EIN obtained** — federal tax ID required for all commercial policies
- [ ] **Employee classification confirmed** — clear distinction between W-2 employees and 1099 contractors (misclassification creates uninsured liability) [src3]
- [ ] **Funding stage and cap table** — determines D&O requirements and coverage limits
- [ ] **State of operations identified** — workers' comp and other requirements vary by state
- [ ] **Revenue and headcount projections** — insurers use these for underwriting and pricing

## Constraints

- Workers' compensation is required by law in 49 states once you hire your first W-2 employee. Texas is the only state without a mandate, but even there contractual requirements may apply. [src3]
- D&O insurance is a standard closing condition in VC term sheets — expect it as a requirement from seed stage onward. Investors view it as fundamental as reviewing the cap table. [src2]
- Policy limits must match contractual obligations. Enterprise customers commonly require vendors to carry $1M-$5M in general liability and professional liability coverage. [src6]
- Insurance costs are tax-deductible as a business expense, reducing effective cost by 20-30% depending on tax bracket.
- Bundled packages from startup-focused insurers save 15-25% versus buying individual policies. [src1]

## Execution Flow

### Step 1: Assess Legal Requirements by State

**Duration**: 30 minutes
**Tool**: State DOL website or OnPay state guide

Identify which policies are legally required in your state(s) of operation before considering optional coverage.

```
State Requirements Checklist:
────────────────────────────────────────────
State: _______________

Workers' Compensation:
  Required? [ ] Yes (most states) [ ] No (Texas only)
  Trigger:  First W-2 employee (full-time OR part-time)
  Penalty for non-compliance: $500-$100K+ fines, criminal charges in some states

Disability Insurance (state-mandated):
  Required? [ ] Yes (CA, HI, NJ, NY, RI, PR) [ ] No
  Type: Short-term disability / paid family leave

Commercial Auto:
  Required? [ ] Yes (company vehicles) [ ] No (personal vehicles only)

Professional License Bond:
  Required? [ ] Yes (licensed professions) [ ] No
────────────────────────────────────────────
```

**Verify**: Cross-reference requirements with your state's Department of Labor and Department of Insurance websites.
**If failed**: If operating in multiple states, check requirements for each state — the most restrictive state sets the floor.

**Duration**: 30 minutes
**Tool**: Term sheet, customer contracts, lease agreements

Identify insurance requirements imposed by investors, customers, and landlords. [src7]

```
Investor Requirements:
────────────────────────────────────────────
Term sheet D&O requirement?     [ ] Yes [ ] No [ ] Not yet raising
  Minimum coverage limit:       $_________ (typical: $1M-$3M at seed)
  Side A coverage required?     [ ] Yes [ ] No
  Key person insurance required? [ ] Yes [ ] No
  Coverage amount:              $_________ (typical: 5-10x salary)

Customer Contract Requirements:
────────────────────────────────────────────
GL minimum per occurrence:      $_________ (typical: $1M)
GL aggregate minimum:           $_________ (typical: $2M)
Professional liability / E&O:   $_________ (typical: $1M-$5M)
Cyber liability:                $_________ (typical: $1M-$5M)
Additional insured endorsement: [ ] Required [ ] Not required

Lease Requirements:
────────────────────────────────────────────
GL coverage:                    $_________ (typical: $1M)
Property insurance:             [ ] Required [ ] Not required
```

**Verify**: Review all active contracts, term sheets, and leases. Missing a contractual insurance requirement can void agreements.
**If failed**: If no contracts or term sheet yet, use industry-standard minimums from the table above.

### Step 3: Build the Policy Portfolio by Priority

**Duration**: 1 hour
**Tool**: Decision matrix below

Select policies based on your stage, industry, and requirements from Steps 1-2.

```
Insurance Priority Matrix:
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
PRIORITY 1 — LEGALLY REQUIRED OR CONTRACTUALLY MANDATORY
  [P1] General Liability (GL)
       When: Day 1 — before signing leases or customer contracts
       Coverage: $1M per occurrence / $2M aggregate
       Cost: $400-$750/year [src1]
       Industry note: Required for all startups

  [P1] Workers' Compensation
       When: First W-2 employee hired
       Coverage: State statutory limits
       Cost: $0.75-$2.50 per $100 of payroll [src3]
       Industry note: Required in 49 states

  [P1] D&O Insurance (if VC-backed)
       When: Term sheet stage — before board formation
       Coverage: $1M-$3M (seed); $3M-$5M (Series A)
       Cost: $5,000-$10,000/yr (seed); $10,000-$20,000/yr (Series A) [src2]
       Industry note: Fintech/healthtech premiums 20-40% higher

PRIORITY 2 — STRONGLY RECOMMENDED
  [P2] Professional Liability / E&O
       When: First paying customer (especially B2B SaaS)
       Coverage: $1M-$2M
       Cost: $600-$2,000/year [src1]
       Industry note: Critical for SaaS, consulting, professional services

  [P2] Cyber Liability
       When: Handling any customer data
       Coverage: $1M-$2M
       Cost: $1,200-$7,000/year [src5]
       Industry note: Fintech and healthtech need higher limits

  [P2] Key Person Insurance
       When: VC funding or critical single-founder dependency
       Coverage: $1M-$2M (5-10x founder salary)
       Cost: $600-$840/year per founder (age 35, healthy) [src4]
       Industry note: Almost always in VC term sheets

PRIORITY 3 — HIRE-TRIGGERED
  [P3] EPLI (Employment Practices Liability)
       When: 5+ employees or first HR complaint
       Coverage: $1M
       Cost: $2,000-$5,000/year for small teams
       Industry note: Highly recommended once interviewing candidates

PRIORITY 4 — INDUSTRY-SPECIFIC
  [P4] Product Liability
       When: Shipping physical products or hardware
       Coverage: $1M-$5M
       Cost: $750-$3,000/year [src6]
       Industry note: Required for hardware, IoT, DTC, food/bev

  [P4] Fiduciary Liability
       When: Offering 401(k) or other benefit plans
       Coverage: $1M
       Cost: $1,000-$3,000/year
       Industry note: Triggered by ERISA-covered benefit plans

  [P4] Umbrella / Excess Liability
       When: Series B+ or enterprise customers requiring higher limits
       Coverage: $5M-$10M excess
       Cost: $2,000-$8,000/year
       Industry note: Cost-effective way to raise limits across all policies
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

**Verify**: Every legally required and contractually mandated policy from Steps 1-2 is included in your portfolio.
**If failed**: If budget is constrained, cover P1 policies first, then add P2 policies within 90 days.

### Step 5: Bind Policies and Set Renewal Calendar

**Duration**: 1-3 days for binding; 15 minutes for calendar setup
**Tool**: Broker platform, calendar application

```
Policy Binding Checklist:
────────────────────────────────────────────
For each policy:
  [ ] Review policy declarations page (dec page)
  [ ] Confirm coverage limits match requirements
  [ ] Verify named insured is correct legal entity
  [ ] Check effective date and policy period
  [ ] Review exclusions — common startup exclusions:
      - Prior acts (E&O/D&O may exclude pre-policy events)
      - Known claims or circumstances
      - Crypto/blockchain activities (many policies exclude)
      - Cannabis-related activities
  [ ] Confirm additional insured endorsements (if customer requires)
  [ ] Save certificates of insurance (COIs)
  [ ] Set renewal reminder 60 days before expiration

Renewal Calendar:
  Policy: ____________  Renewal: ____/____/____  Reminder: ____/____/____
  Policy: ____________  Renewal: ____/____/____  Reminder: ____/____/____
  Policy: ____________  Renewal: ____/____/____  Reminder: ____/____/____
────────────────────────────────────────────
```

**Verify**: COIs are on file and can be produced within 24 hours for any customer or investor request. Renewal calendar is set.
**If failed**: If binding is delayed, ask broker for a binder (temporary proof of coverage) to satisfy immediate contractual deadlines.

| Error | Likely Cause | Recovery Action |
|-------|-------------|----------------|
| Quote declined or extremely high premium | Industry classified as high-risk (crypto, cannabis, weapons) | Verify NAICS code is correct; try surplus lines broker for hard-to-place risks |
| Workers' comp quote rejected | Startup in monopolistic state (OH, ND, WA, WY) | Apply through state fund directly, not private insurer [src3] |

| Customer requires higher limits than current policy | Enterprise contract requires $5M+ but policy is $1M | Add umbrella/excess policy ($2K-$8K/yr) to raise aggregate limits cost-effectively |
| Claim denied due to policy exclusion | Activity fell under standard exclusion (e.g., prior acts, known circumstances) | Review policy exclusions before binding; negotiate removal of critical exclusions or buy back coverage |

## Anti-Patterns

### Wrong: Waiting until after a VC term sheet to shop for D&O insurance
Founders who scramble to bind D&O after receiving a term sheet face higher premiums (rush underwriting) and risk delaying close. Investors view missing D&O as a sign of operational immaturity. [src2]

### Correct: Begin D&O shopping when fundraising starts
Start broker conversations 60-90 days before expected term sheet. This gives time for competitive quotes and avoids rush binding fees.

### Wrong: Skipping workers' comp for "just one part-time employee"
Most states require workers' comp from the first W-2 employee regardless of hours. Non-compliance penalties can reach $100K+ and founders may be personally liable for workplace injuries. [src3]

### Correct: Bind workers' comp before the first employee's start date
Even for part-time hires, bind coverage before their first day of work. Online platforms offer same-day binding for basic workers' comp.

### Wrong: Buying minimum coverage to save money, then facing a coverage gap
A startup with $500K in GL coverage signs an enterprise contract requiring $2M. When an incident occurs, the $1.5M gap comes out of company funds — potentially a fatal blow.

### Correct: Match coverage limits to your largest contractual obligation
Review all customer contracts and investor requirements. Set coverage at or above the highest required limit, using umbrella policies to bridge gaps cost-effectively.

### Wrong: Classifying employees as 1099 contractors to avoid workers' comp
Misclassification is one of the most common and most penalized compliance failures. States actively audit startups, and penalties include back premiums, fines, and personal liability. [src3]

### Correct: Use the IRS 20-factor test for worker classification
If you control how, when, and where work is done, the worker is likely an employee. When in doubt, classify as W-2 and carry workers' comp.
