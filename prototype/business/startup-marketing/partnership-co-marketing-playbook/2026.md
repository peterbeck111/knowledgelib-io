# Partnership & Co-Marketing Playbook

## Purpose

This recipe builds a startup's first 1-3 co-marketing partnerships from scratch: identifying potential partners, crafting a mutual value proposition, pitching and securing the partnership, and executing the first co-marketing campaign. The output is a partner identification matrix, a reusable partnership pitch template, and a completed first co-marketing campaign that generates measurable leads or brand awareness for both parties. [src2]

## Prerequisites

- [ ] **Active customer base** — minimum 100 users/customers to offer genuine audience value to partners
- [ ] **Marketing metrics** — email list size, social following, website traffic (partners evaluate these)
- [ ] **Product positioning** — clear one-pager explaining what you do, for whom, and differentiation
- [ ] **CRM configured** — ability to track lead sources and partner attribution ([HubSpot free](https://www.hubspot.com/products/crm))
- [ ] **Content creation capability** — ability to produce blog posts, webinars, or co-branded assets

## Constraints

- Do not propose partnerships before having at least 100 active users. Without an audience, you have nothing to offer a partner. [src1]
- Partnership proposals must include specific, measurable value for both parties. Vague proposals are ignored. [src3]
- Start with 1-2 partnership experiments before building a formal program. Validate the model first. [src1]
- Co-marketing content requires joint approval. Budget 2-3 weeks for creation including partner review cycles. [src5]
- Track partner-sourced leads separately from day one. Attribution without tracking is impossible.

## Tool Selection Decision

```
Which path?
├── Pre-revenue / early traction (< 1,000 users)
│   └── PATH A: Informal partnerships — content swaps, guest posts, newsletter mentions
├── Growth stage (1,000-10,000 users) + $0 budget
│   └── PATH B: Structured co-marketing — joint webinars, co-authored content, cross-promotions
├── Growth stage + budget available
│   └── PATH C: Co-marketing + tools — Crossbeam for overlap, structured campaigns
└── Scale stage (10,000+ users)
    └── PATH D: Partner program — PRM tools, tiered partners, co-sell motions
```

| Path | Approach | Cost | Timeline | Best For |
|------|----------|------|----------|----------|
| A: Informal | Content swaps, guest posts | $0 | 1-2 weeks per partnership | Early startups building credibility |
| B: Structured | Joint webinars, co-authored content | $0-200 | 4-6 weeks per campaign | Growth stage wanting pipeline |
| C: Co-marketing + Tools | Crossbeam overlap + campaigns | $200-2,000/mo | 6-8 weeks per campaign | Growth stage wanting measurable ROI |
| D: Partner Program | PRM platform, tiered partners | $2,000-10,000/mo | 3-6 months to build | Scale stage building ecosystem |

## Execution Flow

### Step 1: Identify and Score Potential Partners

**Duration**: 4-6 hours
**Tool**: Spreadsheet + manual research

Build a matrix of 30-50 potential partners scored on four criteria.

```
Partner Identification Framework:
──────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. Map your customer's adjacent needs:
   Your customer uses your product AND also needs:
   - [Complementary tool/service 1]
   - [Complementary tool/service 2]
   - [Information/education in related area]

2. Identify companies serving those needs:
   - Same ICP, different product category (non-competing)
   - Similar stage (±1 funding round — too large = you're a rounding error)
   - Active marketing presence (blog, newsletter, social media)

3. Score each potential partner (1-5 scale):
   Audience Overlap    — How much does their audience match your ICP?
   Brand Alignment     — Are they respected by your target market?
   Marketing Activity  — Do they actively create content and engage?
   Feasibility         — Can you reach the right person to propose?

Partner Scoring Spreadsheet:
──────────────────────────────────────────────────
Company | Product | ICP Overlap | Brand | Activity | Feasibility | Total | Contact
--------|---------|-------------|-------|----------|-------------|-------|--------
Ex: Notion | Wiki/docs | 4 | 5 | 5 | 3 | 17 | via LinkedIn
```

Where to find partners: product review sites (G2, Capterra), your customers' tech stacks, conference sponsor lists, companies your audience follows on social media, integration marketplace listings.

**Verify**: You have 30-50 companies scored, sorted by total score. Top 10 are realistic targets with clear audience overlap.
**If failed**: If you cannot identify 30 companies, broaden to adjacent markets. If your product is too niche, look at educational content creators (courses, newsletters, podcasters) who serve your ICP.

### Step 2: Craft the Partnership Value Proposition

**Duration**: 2-3 hours
**Tool**: Document editor

Create a reusable partnership pitch template with specific value metrics.

```
Partnership Value Proposition Template:
──────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. WHAT WE PROPOSE:
   [Specific campaign type: joint webinar / co-authored guide /
    newsletter swap / integration spotlight]

2. WHAT WE BRING:
   - Audience: [X email subscribers, Y social followers, Z monthly visitors]
   - Content: [capability to produce blog posts, webinars, videos]
   - Distribution: [newsletter, social channels, product in-app]
   - Brand: [credibility, customer testimonials, media mentions]

3. WHAT WE NEED FROM YOU:
   - Audience: [their distribution to their audience]
   - Content: [co-creation, review, approval]
   - Time: [estimated hours of partner time required]

4. MUTUAL BENEFIT:
   - Both parties get: [specific metrics — leads, impressions, signups]
   - Your audience gets: [value — education, tools, insights]
   - Their audience gets: [value — education, tools, insights]

5. PROPOSED TIMELINE:
   - Week 1-2: Content creation (shared ownership)
   - Week 3: Review and approval cycle
   - Week 4: Coordinated launch across both audiences
   - Week 5: Results sharing and next steps discussion

6. SUCCESS METRICS:
   - Leads generated for each party
   - Impressions / reach
   - Conversion rate
   - NPS / audience feedback
```

**Verify**: The pitch template includes specific numbers (your audience size, expected leads) and a clear ask. A neutral observer can understand the mutual benefit in 30 seconds.
**If failed**: If you cannot quantify your value, start with smaller asks: guest blog post swap or social media mention. Build toward larger campaigns as you accumulate metrics.

### Step 3: Outreach and Secure First Partner

**Duration**: 1-2 weeks (outreach cycles)
**Tool**: Email + LinkedIn

Reach out to your top 10 scored partners with personalized pitches.

```
Outreach Sequence:
──────────────────────────────────────────────────
Step 1: Find the right contact
  - Marketing lead, Head of Partnerships, or co-founder
  - LinkedIn is the primary channel for partnership outreach
  - Warm intros from mutual connections convert 3-5x better

Step 2: Warm-up (1-2 weeks before pitch)
  - Follow them on social media, engage with their content
  - Share their content with your audience (genuine, not forced)
  - Comment on their posts with valuable additions

Step 3: Send the pitch (email or LinkedIn DM)
  Subject: "[Your Company] x [Their Company] — co-marketing idea"

  Body (keep under 200 words):
  - Personal hook: reference something specific about their work
  - The proposal: one specific campaign idea (not "let's explore")
  - Your value: audience size, past campaign results
  - The ask: 15-minute call to discuss
  - Deadline: soft urgency ("planning Q2 co-marketing calendar")

Step 4: Follow-up
  - Wait 4-5 business days
  - One follow-up only (reply to original thread)
  - Add a new angle or data point

Expected response rates:
  - Cold outreach: 10-20%
  - Warm introduction: 40-60%
  - Existing relationship: 70-90%
```

**Verify**: At least 1 partner has agreed to a co-marketing campaign. You have a signed-off campaign brief with roles, timeline, and KPIs.
**If failed**: If response rate is under 10%, audit your pitch: is the value proposition specific? Is your audience large enough to be interesting? Consider starting with smaller companies (similar size) who have more to gain from the partnership.

### Step 4: Execute the First Co-Marketing Campaign

**Duration**: 4-6 weeks
**Tool**: Content tools + email + social media

Run the first campaign end-to-end. Start with one of these proven formats.

```
Co-Marketing Campaign Types (by effort/impact):
──────────────────────────────────────────────────
LOW EFFORT / MEDIUM IMPACT:
  Newsletter swap — each partner mentions the other in their newsletter
  Social media cross-promotion — coordinated posts featuring both brands
  Guest blog post exchange — write for each other's blogs

MEDIUM EFFORT / HIGH IMPACT:
  Co-authored guide or report — joint expertise in a shared PDF/blog
  Joint webinar — each party presents 20 minutes, shared Q&A
  Bundle promotion — offer both products at a discount for a limited time

HIGH EFFORT / HIGHEST IMPACT:
  Product integration spotlight — build + market a product integration
  Joint research report — co-branded original research with data
  Co-hosted event — virtual or in-person event with shared audience

Recommended First Campaign: Joint Webinar
──────────────────────────────────────────────────
Why: moderate effort, high visibility, generates leads for both parties
Timeline:
  Week 1: Agree on topic, format, and promotion plan
  Week 2: Create landing page, write promotional copy
  Week 3: Promote via email and social (both audiences)
  Week 4: Host webinar, share recording, follow up with attendees
  Week 5: Analyze results, discuss next campaign
```

**Verify**: Campaign is live, both parties are promoting, leads are being tracked with proper attribution (partner source tag in CRM).
**If failed**: If one partner is not promoting, address directly — co-marketing only works with mutual effort. If content creation is stalling, simplify the format (switch from webinar to newsletter swap).

### Step 5: Track Results and Build the Partnership Playbook

**Duration**: 1-2 hours post-campaign
**Tool**: CRM + spreadsheet

Measure results and create a reusable framework for future partnerships.

```
Campaign Results Template:
──────────────────────────────────────────────────
Campaign: [Campaign name]
Partner: [Partner company]
Type: [Webinar / newsletter swap / co-authored content]
Date: [Date range]

Metrics:
  Total reach (impressions): ___
  Landing page visits: ___
  Registrations / signups: ___
  Leads generated (your side): ___
  Leads generated (partner side): ___
  Conversion rate: ___%
  Revenue attributed (if measurable): $___

  CAC from partnership: $___
  CAC from paid channels (comparison): $___

Qualitative:
  Partner satisfaction (1-5): ___
  Content quality (1-5): ___
  Would repeat? Y/N

Next Steps:
  □ Schedule next campaign with this partner
  □ Apply learnings to next partner outreach
  □ Update partnership pitch template with results
```

**Verify**: Results documented, partner-sourced leads tagged in CRM, and a next campaign is proposed or scheduled with the partner.
**If failed**: If results are significantly below expectations, discuss with the partner what can be improved. Sometimes the topic was wrong, not the partnership. Try a different campaign type before abandoning the partnership.

## Quality Benchmarks

| Quality Metric | Minimum Acceptable | Good | Excellent |
|---------------|-------------------|------|-----------|
| Partners identified and scored | 20 | 30-50 | 50+ |
| Outreach response rate | > 10% | > 25% | > 40% |
| First campaign leads generated | 10+ | 50+ | 200+ |
| Partner satisfaction score | 3/5 | 4/5 | 5/5 |
| Partnership CAC vs paid CAC | Equal | 30% lower | 50%+ lower |
| Partners wanting to repeat | 50% | 75% | 90%+ |

**If below minimum**: If outreach response rate is under 10%, your value proposition is unclear or your audience is too small. Build more traction before partnership outreach. If campaign leads are low, the topic or format may not resonate — try a different approach with the same partner.

## Error Handling

| Error | Likely Cause | Recovery Action |
|-------|-------------|----------------|
| No responses to partnership pitches | Value proposition unclear or company too small | Strengthen metrics (grow audience), start with smaller companies, use warm introductions |
| Partner agreed but ghosting on execution | Low priority for them, unclear responsibilities | Send specific next-step email with deadlines, offer to handle 80% of the work |
| Co-marketing campaign generates leads for partner but not you | Asymmetric promotion or audience mismatch | Ensure equal promotion, add gated content requiring both-party registration |
| Partner publishes content without approval | Missing approval workflow | Establish joint review process upfront, use shared docs with comment access |
| Cannot attribute leads to partnership | CRM not configured for source tracking | Add UTM parameters to all partnership links, create partner source tag in CRM |
| Partner relationship deteriorates after first campaign | Misaligned expectations on results | Set expectations before campaign launch, celebrate wins openly, discuss improvements honestly |

## Anti-Patterns

### Wrong: Proposing vague "let's partner" without specific campaign ideas
Partnership pitches that say "we should collaborate" or "let's explore synergies" are deleted immediately. Without a specific proposal, the recipient has to do all the creative work to figure out how the partnership would work. [src3]

### Correct: Pitch one specific campaign with clear metrics
Propose a single, concrete campaign: "Let's co-host a webinar on [topic] targeting [audience]. We'll drive 200 registrations from our 5,000 email list, and we'd love your audience's participation too. Expected: 50+ leads each."

### Wrong: Pursuing partnerships with companies 10x your size
Large companies receive hundreds of partnership proposals. A startup with 500 users proposing a partnership with a company that has 500,000 users offers negligible value. The asymmetry is too large. [src1]

### Correct: Partner with companies at a similar stage (plus or minus one funding round)
The best early partnerships are between companies of similar size. Both parties bring meaningful value, both are motivated to promote, and both benefit roughly equally from the shared audience.

### Wrong: Running co-marketing campaigns without lead attribution tracking
If you cannot prove partnership ROI, you will abandon partnerships when they may actually be your most efficient channel. Without tracking, all partnership value is invisible. [src4]

### Correct: Set up UTM parameters and CRM source tags before the first campaign
Every partnership link should have UTM parameters (utm_source=partner_name, utm_medium=co-marketing, utm_campaign=campaign_name). Tag all leads in CRM with partner source before any campaign launches.

## When This Matters

Use this recipe when a startup has at least 100 active users and wants to grow through co-marketing partnerships instead of (or in addition to) paid advertising. Requires an existing audience to offer value to partners. This recipe handles partner identification, pitching, and first-campaign execution — not building a formal partner program with tiers and PRM software.
