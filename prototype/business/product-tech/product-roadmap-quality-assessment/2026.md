# Product Roadmap Quality Assessment

## Purpose

This assessment evaluates the quality of a product roadmap across six critical dimensions: strategy alignment, customer input quality, technical feasibility validation, resource balance, timeline realism, and stakeholder communication. It is designed for product leaders (VP Product, CPO, Group PM) who need to diagnose whether their roadmap is strategically sound, evidence-based, and executable before committing engineering resources. The output identifies specific roadmap weaknesses and routes to improvement playbooks for each dimension scoring below threshold. [src1]

## Constraints
<!-- Agents: read before running this assessment with a user. -->

- Requires access to the current roadmap artifact (tool, document, or presentation) plus the strategic plan or OKRs it should align to
- Most meaningful for teams with at least 3 months of roadmap history and one completed planning cycle — brand-new roadmaps lack sufficient signal
- Should involve the roadmap owner (PM lead) and ideally an engineering lead who can validate feasibility scores — solo PM assessment tends to over-score feasibility
- Does NOT assess product-market fit, team performance, or delivery velocity — those are separate assessments
- Re-run at every quarterly planning cycle; full reassessment after strategy pivots, leadership changes, or major market shifts

## Assessment Dimensions

<!-- Each dimension is scored independently. The structured format lets agents
     walk through this conversationally with a user, one dimension at a time. -->

### Dimension 1: Strategy Alignment

**What this measures**: How directly roadmap initiatives trace back to company strategy, OKRs, or stated business objectives — and whether that traceability is explicit, not assumed.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | Roadmap is a feature wish list with no connection to company strategy; items added based on loudest voice or latest request | No OKRs referenced; roadmap items lack strategic rationale; cannot explain why any item is on the roadmap vs not |
| 2 | Emerging | Some roadmap items reference strategic goals but the linkage is informal and inconsistent; strategy and roadmap are maintained separately | Verbal explanation of strategy connection exists but is not documented; roadmap tool has no strategy field |
| 3 | Defined | Every roadmap initiative explicitly maps to a strategic objective or OKR; mapping is documented and reviewable | Roadmap tool or document shows objective-to-initiative linkage; each item has a "why" statement tied to strategy [src2] |
| 4 | Managed | Strategy alignment is scored and weighted during prioritization; initiatives competing for the same objective are compared; orphan initiatives are flagged | Prioritization framework includes strategy alignment as a weighted factor; quarterly audit removes misaligned items [src3] |
| 5 | Optimized | Roadmap is derived from strategy, not mapped to it after the fact; strategy changes automatically trigger roadmap re-evaluation; alignment metrics tracked over time | Strategy-first planning process documented; alignment score tracked quarterly; <10% of roadmap items lack direct strategic linkage [src1] |

**Red flags**: PM cannot state which company objective each roadmap item serves; roadmap has not changed after a major strategy pivot; more than 30% of items are "carry-over" from previous cycles without re-validation. [src2]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "Pick any three items on your roadmap — can you name the specific company OKR or strategic objective each one serves?"

### Dimension 2: Customer Input Quality

**What this measures**: The rigor and breadth of customer evidence informing roadmap decisions — from no customer input to systematic, quantified customer signal.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | Roadmap driven entirely by internal opinions, competitor copying, or executive mandates; no structured customer input | No customer feedback repository; product decisions cite no customer data; "we think customers want this" is the standard justification |
| 2 | Emerging | Some customer input exists but is anecdotal — based on a few loud customers or recent support tickets rather than systematic research | Customer quotes appear in roadmap documents but are cherry-picked; feedback comes from <5% of customer base; no regular interview cadence |
| 3 | Defined | Structured customer feedback collection in place; roadmap items reference customer research, survey data, or usage analytics | Customer feedback tool (Productboard, Canny, UserVoice) in use; at least monthly customer interviews conducted; NPS or satisfaction data available [src5] |
| 4 | Managed | Customer input is quantified and weighted in prioritization; segment-specific needs are distinguished; feedback loop closed with customers | RICE or weighted scoring includes customer impact factor; feedback tagged by segment, MRR, and frequency; customers notified when their requests ship [src4] |
| 5 | Optimized | Predictive customer signal: usage data, churn indicators, and win/loss analysis proactively surface roadmap opportunities before customers articulate them | Product analytics drive discovery; churn prediction models inform roadmap; win/loss analysis directly feeds prioritization; evidence confidence scores assigned to each initiative [src5] |

**Red flags**: Roadmap has no customer-facing validation step; largest customer dictates 40%+ of roadmap; product team has not spoken to a customer in the last month; zero usage data referenced in prioritization.
**Quick diagnostic question**: "For your top roadmap initiative, what specific customer evidence supports building it — and how many customers have expressed this need?"

### Dimension 3: Technical Feasibility Validation

**What this measures**: Whether engineering has meaningfully validated the feasibility, complexity, and technical risk of roadmap initiatives before they are committed.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | No engineering input on roadmap feasibility; PM commits timelines without technical validation; surprises are frequent | Engineering learns about roadmap items when sprint planning begins; no technical spikes or feasibility reviews; 50%+ of estimates are wrong by 2x+ |
| 2 | Emerging | Engineering provides rough estimates on request but is not involved in roadmap construction; feasibility is assessed per-item, not holistically | T-shirt sizing exists but is done under time pressure; technical debt and dependencies are not factored into roadmap sequencing |
| 3 | Defined | Engineering participates in roadmap planning; feasibility reviews happen before commitment; technical spikes are budgeted for high-risk items | Formal feasibility review gate before items enter "committed" status; spike tickets created for uncertain items; engineering capacity is a roadmap input |
| 4 | Managed | Architecture review board or tech lead reviews roadmap for systemic risks; dependency mapping across initiatives is maintained; technical debt has explicit roadmap allocation | Cross-team dependency map exists; technical debt gets 15-25% of capacity allocation; architecture review catches conflicts before commitment [src4] |
| 5 | Optimized | Continuous technical feasibility: engineering proactively surfaces feasibility constraints and opportunities; prototyping validates critical assumptions before roadmap commitment | Proof-of-concept budget allocated for high-risk items; engineering contributes roadmap ideas based on technical possibilities; feasibility confidence scored per initiative |

**Red flags**: Engineering consistently calls roadmap items "impossible" or "way more complex than PM thinks" after commitment; no spike or prototype budget exists; technical debt is never on the roadmap; one architect leaving would invalidate 30%+ of the roadmap.
**Quick diagnostic question**: "When was the last time engineering vetoed or significantly changed a roadmap item's scope or timeline — and what happened as a result?"

### Dimension 4: Resource Balance

**What this measures**: How effectively the roadmap balances investment across new features, improvements to existing capabilities, technical debt, and innovation/exploration.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | 100% of capacity allocated to new features or reactive work; no deliberate balance; technical debt and platform health are ignored | All roadmap items are net-new features; no maintenance, debt, or exploration category exists; platform degrades over time |
| 2 | Emerging | Awareness that balance is needed but no explicit allocation; technical debt addressed only when it causes incidents | "We know we should pay down debt" but no capacity reserved; debt work happens only in firefighting mode; innovation is a side project |
| 3 | Defined | Explicit capacity allocation ratios documented (e.g., 70% features / 20% debt / 10% exploration); ratios reviewed quarterly | Roadmap visually shows investment by category; team can state their allocation split; ratios are aspirational but sometimes violated under pressure |
| 4 | Managed | Allocation ratios enforced and tracked; balance adjusted based on product lifecycle stage; near-term vs long-term investment is deliberately managed | Dashboard tracks actual vs planned allocation; ratio adjusts by quarter (e.g., more debt after a big launch); leadership reviews balance, not just features [src6] |
| 5 | Optimized | Dynamic allocation based on data: platform health metrics trigger debt investment, experimentation success rates inform exploration budget, market signals adjust feature investment | Automated platform health scores influence debt allocation; experimentation has measured ROI that informs budget; portfolio-level investment optimization across product lines |

**Red flags**: Cannot state the team's allocation split between new features, improvements, and debt; technical debt has no roadmap representation; 100% of roadmap is "what we'll build" with no "what we'll fix or explore"; team has not shipped an infrastructure improvement in two quarters.
**Quick diagnostic question**: "What percentage of your engineering capacity over the last quarter went to new features vs improvements vs technical debt vs exploration?"

### Dimension 5: Timeline Realism

**What this measures**: Whether roadmap timelines are credible based on historical delivery data, team capacity, and dependency management — or are aspirational targets disconnected from reality.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | Timelines are executive mandates or arbitrary dates with no connection to team capacity; roadmap rarely delivers on time | Deadlines set before scope is understood; no historical velocity data referenced; more than 60% of roadmap items miss their dates |
| 2 | Emerging | Some effort estimation exists but is unreliable; timelines are set by PM intuition rather than data; scope creep is common | Past estimates are off by 50%+ on average; buffer is not built in; dates shift every review cycle |
| 3 | Defined | Timelines informed by team velocity data and historical estimation accuracy; buffer built in for known risks; scope is explicitly bounded | Team tracks velocity or throughput; estimation accuracy is measured; roadmap uses ranges (Q2 vs specific dates) for far-horizon items |
| 4 | Managed | Probabilistic timeline management: confidence intervals on delivery dates; Monte Carlo or reference-class forecasting used; dependencies tracked across teams | 80% confidence intervals published alongside dates; cross-team dependency calendar maintained; estimation accuracy >70% within one sprint of actuals |
| 5 | Optimized | Predictive delivery modeling with continuous recalibration; roadmap timelines update automatically based on velocity changes; leading indicators surface timeline risks before they hit | Automated forecasting tools (e.g., Jira forecasting plugins, Linear estimates); timeline risk alerts triggered by velocity drops; historical estimation accuracy >85% |

**Red flags**: Every roadmap item has a specific date but no confidence interval; team has never measured estimation accuracy; "it'll be ready by [date]" is stated without referencing any data; roadmap has not changed dates despite missing the last three commitments.
**Quick diagnostic question**: "What percentage of your roadmap items from last quarter were delivered within one sprint of the originally committed date?"

### Dimension 6: Stakeholder Communication

**What this measures**: How effectively the roadmap is communicated to stakeholders (executives, sales, support, customers) and whether feedback flows back into roadmap decisions.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | Roadmap exists only in the PM's head or in a document no one else sees; stakeholders discover priorities through hallway conversations | No shared roadmap artifact; sales and support have no visibility into what is coming; "when is feature X shipping?" is answered differently by different people |
| 2 | Emerging | Roadmap is shared periodically but in a format stakeholders cannot easily consume; communication is one-way (broadcast, no feedback loop) | Quarterly roadmap presentation exists but is a dense spreadsheet or Jira board; stakeholders attend but rarely engage; no feedback mechanism |
| 3 | Defined | Audience-appropriate roadmap views exist (executive, sales, engineering); regular cadence of roadmap reviews with feedback incorporated | Executive summary view, customer-facing roadmap, and detailed engineering roadmap maintained; monthly or quarterly review meetings with action items [src1] |
| 4 | Managed | Two-way communication: stakeholder feedback is systematically captured and influences roadmap; stakeholder satisfaction with roadmap process is measured | Feedback from roadmap reviews tracked in backlog; stakeholder alignment survey conducted; sales and support input has a clear path into prioritization [src2] |
| 5 | Optimized | Continuous stakeholder alignment: real-time roadmap visibility with self-service access; proactive communication of changes; alignment score tracked and optimized | Self-service roadmap portal for stakeholders; automated change notifications; alignment score >80% in stakeholder surveys; cross-functional workshops proactively resolve misalignment [src1] |

**Red flags**: Sales team regularly promises features not on the roadmap; executives are surprised by what ships; no one outside product team can describe the next quarter's priorities; roadmap reviews are status updates, not alignment discussions.
**Quick diagnostic question**: "If I asked your head of sales and your CTO separately what the top 3 product priorities are for next quarter, would they give the same answer?"

## Scoring & Interpretation

### Overall Score Calculation

Equal-weighted average across all six dimensions. Each dimension represents a critical quality factor — weakness in any one can undermine the entire roadmap.

```
Overall Score = (Strategy Alignment + Customer Input Quality + Technical Feasibility + Resource Balance + Timeline Realism + Stakeholder Communication) / 6
```

### Score Interpretation

| Overall Score | Maturity Level | Interpretation | Recommended Next Step |
|---------------|---------------|----------------|----------------------|
| 1.0 - 1.9 | Critical | Roadmap is a feature wish list with no strategic foundation, no customer validation, and unreliable timelines; high risk of building the wrong things | Stop and rebuild: establish strategy-to-roadmap linkage, implement customer feedback collection, involve engineering in feasibility |
| 2.0 - 2.9 | Developing | Basic roadmap structure exists but significant gaps in evidence, feasibility validation, or stakeholder alignment; execution risk is high | Close the biggest gap first: run dimension-level routing below to identify and fix the weakest link |
| 3.0 - 3.9 | Competent | Solid roadmap process with defined practices across most dimensions; ready to optimize and move from process compliance to strategic leverage | Focus on moving weakest dimensions from "defined" to "managed"; introduce data-driven prioritization and probabilistic timelines |
| 4.0 - 4.5 | Advanced | Roadmap is well-structured, evidence-based, and effectively communicated; focus should shift to predictive capabilities and portfolio optimization | Implement predictive delivery modeling, dynamic resource allocation, and continuous stakeholder alignment measurement |
| 4.6 - 5.0 | Best-in-class | Roadmap is a strategic asset that drives organizational alignment; maintain through continuous improvement and share practices across the organization | Pioneer AI-assisted roadmap optimization, automated feasibility analysis, and cross-portfolio investment balancing |

## Common Pitfalls in Assessment

- **Strategy alignment theater**: Teams score high on alignment because every roadmap item has an OKR tag — but the tags are retrofitted after prioritization, not used to drive it. Verify by asking: "Which items did you deprioritize because they didn't align with strategy?" [src2]
- **Customer input bias**: High score on customer input may mask a small, unrepresentative sample. Scoring 4+ requires quantified feedback across segments, not just 10 conversations with power users. [src5]
- **Feasibility-by-assumption**: Teams claim engineering validated feasibility but validation was a 5-minute Slack message, not a structured review. Ask for the feasibility review artifact or spike results.
- **Aspirational timelines normalized**: Organizations that consistently miss deadlines may rate themselves a 3 because "everyone misses deadlines" — this is a 1-2 score. Calibrate against actual on-time delivery percentage.
- **Communication without feedback**: A polished roadmap presentation scores high on Dimension 6 but masks that communication is one-directional. True communication requires evidence that stakeholder feedback changed roadmap decisions.
