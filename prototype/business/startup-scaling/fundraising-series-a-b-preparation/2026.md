# Fundraising Series A/B Preparation

## Purpose

This recipe produces an investor-ready metrics dashboard, pitch deck, organized data room, and board update template — the four deliverables needed to run an efficient fundraising process. The output transforms scattered financial data, product metrics, and corporate documents into a coherent investor narrative backed by benchmarked numbers, reducing typical fundraising cycles from 6+ months to 3-4 months. [src1]

## Prerequisites
<!-- Agents: verify ALL prerequisites before executing. Missing prerequisites = failed execution. -->

- [ ] **Financial data** — 12-24 months of P&L, balance sheet, and cash flow statements from accounting software (QuickBooks/Xero)
- [ ] **Product metrics** — ARR/MRR trend, NRR, churn rates, CAC, LTV from analytics platform (Stripe, Mixpanel, or equivalent)
- [ ] **Cap table** — current ownership structure in [Carta](https://carta.com) or [Pulley](https://pulley.com) (not a stale spreadsheet)
- [ ] **Corporate documents** — articles of incorporation, board consents, IP assignments, key contracts
- [ ] **Clean accounting** — GAAP-compliant books, ideally reviewed by CPA (required for Series B, recommended for Series A)

## Constraints
<!-- Hard rules. Agents: enforce throughout execution. Violating these = broken output or legal risk. -->

- Start preparation 6-9 months before you need capital — rushed fundraising reduces leverage [src4]
- Series A baseline: $1.5-3M ARR, 2-3x YoY growth, demonstrated PMF [src1]
- Series B baseline: $5-10M ARR, 15-20% MoM growth, NRR > 100%, burn multiple < 2x [src2]
- Data room must be organized before first investor meeting — messy data rooms kill deals [src3]
- Financial model must show path to profitability within 24-36 months of the raise [src5]

## Tool Selection Decision

```
Which round?
├── Series A ($5M-$15M raise)
│   ├── First-time founder
│   │   └── PATH A: Full preparation (8 weeks) — build everything from scratch
│   └── Repeat founder with warm investor relationships
│       └── PATH B: Accelerated preparation (4 weeks) — polish existing materials
├── Series B ($15M-$50M raise)
│   ├── Existing board engaged in process
│   │   └── PATH C: Board-led process (6 weeks) — board members make intros
│   └── Founder-led process
│       └── PATH D: Full institutional process (8 weeks) — broader outreach
└── Bridge/Extension
    └── PATH E: Existing investor focused (2-3 weeks) — internal round dynamics
```

| Path | Duration | Investor Targets | Typical Outcome |
|------|----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| A: Full Series A | 8 weeks prep + 3-4 months | 40-60 investors | $7-12M at $30-60M valuation |
| B: Accelerated Series A | 4 weeks prep + 2-3 months | 15-25 warm targets | $7-15M at $35-80M valuation |
| C: Board-led Series B | 6 weeks prep + 2-3 months | 10-20 growth-stage funds | $20-50M at $100-200M valuation |
| D: Full Series B | 8 weeks prep + 3-4 months | 30-50 investors | $20-50M at $100-200M valuation |
| E: Bridge/Extension | 2-3 weeks prep + 2-4 weeks | Existing investors | 50-100% of last round |

## Execution Flow

### Step 1: Metrics Audit and Benchmarking

**Duration**: 3-5 days
**Tool**: Spreadsheet + analytics platforms (Stripe, Mixpanel, Amplitude)

Calculate all investor-grade metrics and benchmark against 2025-2026 standards. [src1] [src5]

```
SERIES A METRICS DASHBOARD:

| Metric                | Your Value | Median (2025) | Top Quartile | Investor Threshold |
|-----------------------|-----------|---------------|-------------|-------------------|
| ARR                   | $____     | $2.5M         | $4M+        | > $1.5M           |
| ARR Growth (YoY)     | ____%     | 100%          | 200%+       | > 100%            |
| MRR Growth (MoM)     | ____%     | 8-10%         | 15%+        | > 8%              |
| Net Revenue Retention | ____%     | 105%          | 120%+       | > 100%            |
| Gross Churn (monthly) | ____%     | 2-3%          | < 1.5%      | < 3%              |
| Gross Margin          | ____%     | 70%           | 80%+        | > 65%             |
| CAC Payback (months)  | ____      | 15            | < 8         | < 18              |
| LTV:CAC Ratio        | ____:1    | 3:1           | 5:1+        | > 3:1             |
| Burn Multiple         | ____x     | 2.5x          | < 1.5x      | < 2x              |
| Rule of 40            | ____%     | 25%           | 40%+        | > 25%             |
| Runway (months)       | ____      | 18            | 24+         | > 12              |

SERIES B ADDITIONS:
| Metric                | Your Value | Median (2025) | Top Quartile | Investor Threshold |
|-----------------------|-----------|---------------|-------------|-------------------|
| ARR                   | $____     | $8M           | $15M+       | > $5M             |
| Magic Number          | ____      | 0.7           | 1.0+        | > 0.5             |
| Average ACV           | $____     | $25K          | $50K+       | Depends on model  |
| Logo Retention        | ____%     | 85%           | 92%+        | > 85%             |
| CAC by Channel        | $____     | Varies        | Varies      | Breakdown needed  |
| Cohort Revenue Curves | Graph     | Flat/growing  | Growing     | Not declining      |
```

**Verify**: All metrics calculated from actual data (not estimates), benchmarked against relevant stage/model
**If failed**: If any metric is below investor threshold, flag as a risk factor and prepare a narrative for why it will improve post-raise

### Step 2: Build Investor Narrative and Pitch Deck

**Duration**: 5-7 days
**Tool**: Presentation software (Google Slides / Figma / PowerPoint)

Build a 10-15 slide deck that tells a story, not a data dump. [src4]

```
PITCH DECK STRUCTURE (10-15 slides):

Slide 1: Title + one-line description
Slide 2: Problem — what pain exists, who has it, why now
Slide 3: Solution — what you built, why it's better
Slide 4: Market — TAM/SAM/SOM with bottom-up calculation
Slide 5: Business model — how you make money, unit economics
Slide 6: Traction — ARR growth chart, key metrics (the "hockey stick")
Slide 7: Product — demo or screenshots, key differentiators
Slide 8: Go-to-market — customer acquisition channels, sales motion
Slide 9: Competition — differentiation matrix (not a 2x2 with you in top-right)
Slide 10: Team — founders + key hires, relevant experience
Slide 11: Financials — 3-year projection, path to profitability
Slide 12: The Ask — raise amount, use of funds, milestones

OPTIONAL (Series B):
Slide 13: Customer case studies — logos, metrics, quotes
Slide 14: Expansion strategy — new markets, products, segments
Slide 15: Appendix — detailed cohort analysis, unit economics deep-dive
```

**Investor narrative framework**:
1. **The Insight**: What non-obvious thing do you understand about the market that others don't?
2. **The Wedge**: How did you enter the market in a way that's hard to replicate?
3. **The Machine**: What repeatable growth engine have you built?
4. **The Vision**: What does the company look like at $100M+ ARR?

**Verify**: Deck reviewed by 2-3 friendly investors or advisors for feedback before going live
**If failed**: If narrative is unclear, run a mock pitch with advisors and iterate based on the questions they ask

### Step 3: Organize Data Room

**Duration**: 5-7 days
**Tool**: DocSend / Google Drive (organized folder structure)

Set up a structured data room that investors can navigate without guidance. [src3] [src6]

```
DATA ROOM STRUCTURE:

📁 1. Company Overview
├── Pitch deck (latest version)
├── Executive summary (1-2 pages)
├── Company fact sheet
└── Product demo video or walkthrough

📁 2. Financials
├── Historical P&L (monthly, last 24 months)
├── Balance sheet (last 3 quarters)
├── Cash flow statement
├── 3-year financial model with assumptions
├── Monthly burn rate and runway calculation
└── Revenue recognition policy

📁 3. Metrics & Analytics
├── ARR/MRR waterfall (expansion, contraction, churn, new)
├── Cohort analysis (revenue retention by monthly cohort)
├── Unit economics (CAC, LTV, payback by channel)
├── NRR/GRR trend (monthly, last 12-24 months)
├── Pipeline and conversion funnel metrics
└── Product usage metrics (DAU/MAU, feature adoption)

📁 4. Cap Table & Corporate
├── Cap table (export from Carta/Pulley)
├── Articles of incorporation
├── Board meeting minutes (last 12 months)
├── Option pool summary (granted, exercised, available)
├── Previous round term sheets and closing docs
└── Outstanding SAFEs or convertible notes

📁 5. Legal & IP
├── IP assignment agreements (all employees/contractors)
├── Patent portfolio (if applicable)
├── Key customer contracts (top 5 by ARR)
├── Material vendor agreements
├── Outstanding litigation (if any)
└── Insurance coverage summary

📁 6. Team & HR
├── Org chart (current + planned 12-month)
├── Key employee bios and LinkedIn profiles
├── Employee stock option plan (ESOP)
├── Compensation benchmarking data
└── Key person dependencies and succession plan

📁 7. Market & Competition
├── Market sizing analysis (TAM/SAM/SOM)
├── Competitive landscape analysis
├── Customer testimonials and case studies
└── Industry analyst mentions (if any)
```

**Verify**: All documents current (within 30 days), no broken links, folder structure navigable without explanation
**If failed**: If documents are missing, prioritize financials (#2) and metrics (#3) — these are requested first in 90% of processes

### Step 4: Build Financial Model

**Duration**: 5-10 days
**Tool**: Google Sheets / Excel / Causal

Create a bottoms-up 3-year financial model that supports the ask. [src5]

```
FINANCIAL MODEL STRUCTURE:

Tab 1: Summary Dashboard
├── Key metrics (ARR, growth, margins, runway)
├── Funding scenarios (raise amount → runway → milestones)
└── Sensitivity table (growth rate vs burn rate)

Tab 2: Revenue Model (bottoms-up)
├── New customer acquisition by channel
├── Average contract value by segment
├── Expansion revenue assumptions
├── Churn and contraction assumptions
├── Monthly MRR waterfall → annual ARR

Tab 3: Cost Model
├── Headcount plan by department (month-by-month)
├── Salary assumptions + annual increases
├── Non-headcount costs (infrastructure, tools, marketing spend)
├── One-time costs (office, equipment, legal)
└── Total OPEX build-up

Tab 4: Cash Flow
├── Operating cash flow
├── Capital expenditures
├── Financing activities (this raise)
├── Monthly cash balance
└── Runway calculation under different scenarios

Tab 5: Scenarios
├── Base case (likely outcome)
├── Bull case (everything goes right — 120% of base)
├── Bear case (underperformance — 70% of base)
└── Breakeven scenario (what it takes to reach profitability)
```

**Use of funds allocation (typical)**:
| Category | Series A (% of raise) | Series B (% of raise) |
|----------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Engineering / Product | 40-50% | 30-40% |
| Sales & Marketing | 25-35% | 35-45% |
| G&A / Operations | 10-15% | 10-15% |
| Buffer / Working capital | 10-15% | 10-15% |

**Verify**: Model produces 18-24 months of runway at base case burn, growth assumptions supported by historical data
**If failed**: If model shows < 18 months runway, either reduce burn assumptions or increase raise amount

### Step 5: Investor Targeting and Pipeline

**Duration**: 3-5 days
**Tool**: CRM (Affinity / HubSpot / Attio / Notion)

Build a targeted investor list with warm intros prioritized.

```
INVESTOR PIPELINE STRUCTURE:

Tier 1: Warm intros (existing investors, board, advisors) — 10-15 targets
├── Score each: sector fit (1-5) + stage fit (1-5) + relationship warmth (1-5)
├── Research recent investments in your space
├── Prepare personalized outreach for each
└── Schedule intros in 2-week batches

Tier 2: Targeted cold outreach — 20-30 targets
├── Investors who led competitive rounds
├── Investors with public thesis matching your space
├── Angel/scout network introductions
└── Conference and event connections

Tier 3: Broad funnel — 10-20 backup targets
├── Emerging fund managers (first-time funds often more accessible)
├── Corporate VCs with strategic fit
├── International investors with mandate in your geography
└── Crossover funds (for Series B)

PIPELINE TRACKING:
| Investor | Fund | Partner | Stage Fit | Intro Source | Status | Next Step | Notes |
|----------|------|---------|-----------|-------------|--------|-----------|-------|
| ...      | ...  | ...     | ...       | ...         | ...    | ...       | ...   |
```

**Process timeline**:
- Week 1-2: Tier 1 meetings (warm intros)
- Week 3-4: Tier 2 meetings + follow-ups from Tier 1
- Week 5-6: Partner meetings and term sheet negotiations
- Week 7-8: Due diligence and closing

**Verify**: 40-60 investors identified (Series A) or 30-50 (Series B), with at least 10 warm intros confirmed
**If failed**: If warm intro pipeline is thin, invest 2-4 weeks in relationship-building before starting formal process

### Step 6: Prepare for Due Diligence

**Duration**: 2-3 days (preparation) + 2-4 weeks (process)
**Tool**: Data room (DocSend / Google Drive)

Anticipate the top 20 due diligence questions and pre-write answers.

```
TOP DUE DILIGENCE QUESTIONS (have answers ready):

Financial:
1. Walk me through your unit economics by channel
2. What is your payback period and how has it trended?
3. Explain your biggest customer concentration risk
4. What does your cash conversion cycle look like?
5. What are the key assumptions in your 3-year model?

Product:
6. What is your product roadmap for the next 12 months?
7. How defensible is your technology? Any proprietary IP?
8. What is the switching cost for customers?
9. How does your NPS compare to competitors?

Market:
10. How do you calculate your TAM? Walk me through bottom-up
11. Who are your top 3 competitors and how do you differentiate?
12. What market trends are tailwinds for your business?

Team:
13. What key hires will you make with this raise?
14. What is founder vesting status?
15. Are there any co-founder or team dynamics risks?

Legal/Risk:
16. Any outstanding litigation or regulatory risk?
17. Are all IP assignments in place?
18. Any key-person dependencies?
19. What are the biggest risks to the business?
20. What would cause this company to fail?
```

**Verify**: Written answers for all 20 questions, reviewed by co-founders for consistency
**If failed**: If any question reveals a real gap, address it before starting the fundraise (not during)

## Anti-Patterns

### Wrong: Fundraising Without Metrics-Ready Data
Starting investor conversations before having clean, benchmarked metrics. Investors who see rough data in first meetings rarely give second chances — first impressions drive 80% of the decision to take a second meeting. [src1]

### Correct: Metrics-First Preparation
Calculate all metrics, benchmark against stage-appropriate standards, and identify gaps before the first investor conversation. If metrics are below threshold, improve them first or have a compelling narrative for why they will improve.

### Wrong: One-Size-Fits-All Pitch
Sending the same deck to every investor without customization. A growth fund with $500M AUM has different expectations than an early-stage fund with $50M AUM. [src4]

### Correct: Tailored Outreach
Research each investor's portfolio, thesis, and recent investments. Customize the intro and highlight why your company fits their specific investment criteria.

### Wrong: Data Room as Afterthought
Scrambling to organize documents after receiving a term sheet. Due diligence delays kill more deals than valuation disagreements. [src3]

### Correct: Data Room Ready Before First Meeting
Have the data room 90% complete before starting outreach. Share it proactively with serious investors after the second meeting to demonstrate operational maturity.

The output transforms scattered data into a professional fundraising package that compresses the raise timeline and maximizes valuation.
