# Customer Discovery Interview Playbook

## Purpose

This recipe produces a validated problem hypothesis backed by 20-40 structured customer discovery interviews, a synthesis report with pattern analysis, and a go/no-go scorecard based on a clear threshold: at least 8 out of 10 interviewees must describe the target problem unprompted. The output replaces founder intuition with evidence-based problem validation that feeds directly into solution design and MVP scoping. [src1]

## Prerequisites
<!-- Agents: verify ALL prerequisites before executing. Missing prerequisites = failed execution. -->

- [ ] **ICP definition** available from business/customer-research — with firmographic and behavioral criteria specific enough to screen recruits
- [ ] **Problem hypothesis** — 1-2 sentence statement of the problem you believe exists, including assumed severity and frequency
- [ ] **Scheduling tool** — Calendly ([calendly.com/signup](https://calendly.com/signup)) or Cal.com account (free tier: 1 event type)
- [ ] **Video conferencing** — Zoom ([zoom.us/signup](https://zoom.us/signup)) or Google Meet account (free tier: 40-min meetings)
- [ ] **Recording consent language** — one-sentence disclosure to read at interview start (legal requirement in many jurisdictions)
- [ ] **Synthesis workspace** — Dovetail account, Google Sheet, or Notion database set up with tagging taxonomy

## Constraints
<!-- Hard rules. Agents: enforce throughout execution. Violating these = broken output or legal risk. -->

- Never mention your solution or product idea during problem interviews. Introducing your idea turns the interview into a pitch and contaminates responses. Ask about their life, not your idea. [src1]
- Record every interview with explicit consent. Memory-based notes miss 40-60% of actionable detail and introduce confirmation bias. [src2]
- Recruit from target ICP segments only. Friends, family, and convenience samples produce false validation. [src1]
- Synthesize within 48 hours of each interview batch. Delayed synthesis loses emotional context and non-verbal signals that inform severity coding. [src3]
- Stop adding interviews only after reaching pattern saturation — when 3 consecutive interviews reveal no new problem themes. Minimum 20, target 30. [src6]
- Validation threshold: 8/10 interviewees must describe the problem unprompted (without you naming it) before the problem is considered validated. Below this threshold, pivot or reframe.

## Execution Flow

### Step 1: Recruit Interviewees

**Duration**: 5-10 days
**Tool**: Scheduling tool + outreach channels

Recruit 35-50 candidates to yield 25-40 completed interviews (expect 30-40% no-show/decline rate). Recruit only from your ICP — never from friends, family, or people who owe you favors. [src1]

**Recruitment channels ranked by signal quality**:

```
Channel                          Signal Quality    Response Rate
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. Warm intro via mutual contact    Highest           40-60%
2. LinkedIn personalized outreach   High              15-25%
3. Industry community/Slack/Reddit  High              10-20%
4. Cold email to ICP list           Medium            5-15%
5. Social media post (Twitter/X)    Medium            3-10%
6. Paid recruiting panel            Variable          80-95%
   (User Interviews, Respondent)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

**Outreach message template** (keep under 80 words):

```
Subject: Quick question about [their specific problem domain]

Hi [Name],

I'm researching how [ICP role] handles [problem domain].
I'm NOT selling anything — just learning.

Would you have 25 minutes for a conversation?
I'd love to hear about your experience with [topic].

[Calendly/scheduling link]

Happy to share what I learn across all conversations
as a thank-you.

[Your name]
```

**Verify**: 35+ candidates scheduled within 10 days. Track in a simple spreadsheet: name, company, role, ICP match score, scheduled date, status.
**If failed**: If under 20 scheduled after 7 days, add a second recruitment channel or offer a small incentive ($20-50 gift card).

### Step 2: Prepare Interview Script

**Duration**: 1-2 hours
**Tool**: Document editor

Build a semi-structured script following Mom Test principles: ask about their life, not your idea. Focus on past behavior, not future intentions. Never ask hypothetical "would you" questions. [src1]

**Interview script structure** (25 minutes total):

```
OPENING (2 min):
─────────────────
"Thanks for making time. I'm researching [problem domain]
to understand the real challenges. There are no right or
wrong answers — I just want to learn about your experience.
Mind if I record for my notes? Everything stays confidential."

CONTEXT (3 min):
─────────────────
1. "Tell me about your role. What does a typical week look like?"
2. "Where does [problem domain] fit into your priorities?"

PROBLEM EXPLORATION (15 min):
─────────────────────────────
3. "Walk me through the last time you dealt with [broad topic area]."
   → Follow the story. Ask: "What happened next?"
   → Probe emotions: "How did that make you feel?"
   → Probe severity: "What was the impact on [their goal]?"

4. "What's the hardest part about [the process they described]?"
   → Let them name the problem. Do NOT name it for them.
   → If they name YOUR hypothesized problem unprompted: STRONG SIGNAL
   → If they name a different problem: note it — this is data

5. "How are you solving that today?"
   → Current solution reveals willingness to invest effort
   → "Cobbled-together" solutions = high pain signal

6. "What have you tried that didn't work?"
   → Failed attempts = evidence of active problem-seeking

7. "If you could wave a magic wand, what would change?"
   → Captures desired outcome (JTBD "so that I can...")

COMMITMENT TESTING (3 min):
───────────────────────────
8. "Who else on your team deals with this?"
   → Tests organizational scope + gets referral recruits

9. "Would you be open to a follow-up conversation
    when I have something more concrete to show?"
   → Measures genuine interest (time commitment signal)

CLOSE (2 min):
──────────────
"This was incredibly helpful. Is there anything I should
have asked but didn't?"
→ Often surfaces the most important insight of the interview
```

**Key rules for the interviewer**: [src1]
- Talk less than 30% of the time. Listen more than 70%.
- Never say your product idea. If they ask what you're building, deflect: "I'm still figuring that out — I want to understand the problem better first."
- When they give a compliment ("great idea!"), deflect and return to specifics: "Thanks — but tell me more about the last time this happened."
- Write down exact quotes, not paraphrases.

**Verify**: Script reviewed by at least one other person. Run one pilot interview with a non-ICP contact to test flow and timing.
**If failed**: If pilot runs over 30 minutes, cut questions. If conversation feels interrogative, add more "tell me about..." open-ended prompts.

### Step 3: Conduct Interviews

**Duration**: 2-4 weeks (2-3 interviews per day maximum)
**Tool**: Video conferencing + transcription
**Rate limit**: Maximum 3 interviews per day to avoid interviewer fatigue and ensure same-day note capture.

Run interviews following the script from Step 2. Adapt based on what you learn — if early interviews reveal a problem you did not hypothesize, add probing questions about it in subsequent interviews.

```
Per-interview checklist:
─────────────────────────
□ Recording consent given and acknowledged
□ Recording started (video + audio)
□ Note-taker present (or solo with recording)
□ Script available but NOT read verbatim
□ Timer visible — stay within 25-30 minutes
□ Exact quotes captured in notes (not paraphrases)
□ Commitment ask completed at end
□ 5-minute debrief immediately after:
  - What surprised me?
  - What problem did they describe unprompted?
  - Severity rating (1-5)
  - Would they be a design partner? (yes/no/maybe)
```

**After each interview, log in your tracking spreadsheet**:

```
Interview Tracking Log:
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
| # | Name | Date | ICP Match | Problem Mentioned | Unprompted? | Severity | Current Solution | Commitment | Notes |
|---|------|------|-----------|-------------------|-------------|----------|-----------------|------------|-------|
| 1 | ...  | ...  | Y/N       | [exact quote]     | Y/N         | 1-5      | [what they use]  | Y/N        | ...   |
```

**Expected output**: After 10 interviews, you should see 2-3 problem themes emerging. After 20, the top problem should be clear. After 30, you should have reached saturation (no new themes in 3 consecutive interviews). [src6]
**Verify**: At least 80% of scheduled interviews completed. Tracking log fully populated for every interview.
**If failed**: If no-show rate exceeds 40%, send reminder messages 24 hours and 1 hour before. Consider rescheduling via the same booking link.

### Step 4: Synthesize Interview Data

**Duration**: 4-8 hours (batch after every 8-10 interviews)
**Tool**: Dovetail, Notion, or Google Sheets

Synthesis converts raw interview transcripts into structured patterns. Do this in batches of 8-10 interviews, not all at once at the end. [src2] [src7]

**Synthesis process**:

```
Step 4a: Tag and Code (per interview)
───────────────────────────────────────
1. Read/listen to full transcript
2. Highlight and tag every:
   - Problem statement (what hurts)
   - Severity indicator (how much it hurts)
   - Frequency indicator (how often it hurts)
   - Current solution (how they cope today)
   - Desired outcome (what "fixed" looks like)
   - Emotional signal (frustration, excitement, resignation)
3. Use consistent tag taxonomy across all interviews

Step 4b: Cluster into Themes (per batch)
─────────────────────────────────────────
1. Group tagged excerpts by problem theme
2. Name each cluster with a descriptive label
3. Count frequency: how many interviews mention this theme?
4. Rate average severity across interviews (1-5 scale)
5. Note whether mentions were prompted or unprompted

Step 4c: Build Evidence Table (running)
───────────────────────────────────────
| Problem Theme | Frequency (n/total) | Avg Severity | Unprompted Rate | Key Quotes | Current Solutions |
```

**Verify**: Every interview transcript has been fully tagged. No interview is unprocessed. Theme clusters are mutually exclusive (each excerpt belongs to exactly one cluster).
**If failed**: If themes overlap significantly, your taxonomy is too broad. Split overlapping themes into sub-themes and re-tag.

### Step 5: Score and Decide (Go/No-Go)

**Duration**: 1-2 hours
**Tool**: Spreadsheet or synthesis tool

Apply the validation scorecard to determine if the problem is validated, partially validated, or invalidated.

```
PROBLEM VALIDATION SCORECARD
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Primary Problem Hypothesis: [state it]
Total Interviews Completed: ___

METRIC 1: Unprompted Problem Mention Rate
─────────────────────────────────────────
Interviewees who described this problem
without being asked about it specifically:  ___/___ = ___%

Threshold:  >= 80% (8/10) = VALIDATED
            60-79%        = PARTIALLY VALIDATED (needs reframing)
            < 60%         = NOT VALIDATED (pivot)

METRIC 2: Severity Score
────────────────────────
Average severity across interviews:  ___/5.0

Threshold:  >= 4.0 = High severity (strong signal)
            3.0-3.9 = Medium (viable but not urgent)
            < 3.0   = Low (nice-to-have, risky to build for)

METRIC 3: Active Solution Seeking
──────────────────────────────────
Interviewees currently using a workaround
or paying for a partial solution:  ___/___ = ___%

Threshold:  >= 60% = Strong (people invest effort to solve this)
            30-59% = Moderate (problem exists but not acute)
            < 30%  = Weak (they tolerate the problem)

METRIC 4: Commitment Signal
───────────────────────────
Interviewees who agreed to a follow-up
or offered to introduce colleagues:  ___/___ = ___%

Threshold:  >= 50% = Strong buying signal
            25-49% = Moderate interest
            < 25%  = Low engagement

OVERALL DECISION:
─────────────────
All 4 metrics at "validated/strong" level  → GO
Metric 1 validated + 2 of 3 others strong  → CONDITIONAL GO
Metric 1 not validated                      → NO GO (pivot or reframe)
Any single metric below minimum             → INVESTIGATE further
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

**Verify**: Scorecard uses actual tallied data from the tracking log, not impressions. Cross-check counts against raw log.
**If failed**: If Metric 1 is between 60-79%, conduct 10 more interviews with reframed problem language before making a final decision.

### Step 6: Produce Deliverables

**Duration**: 2-4 hours

Generate the final synthesis report and problem validation scorecard for downstream consumption.

```
Final Deliverables:
────────────────────
1. Problem Validation Scorecard (JSON or spreadsheet)
   - All 4 metrics with raw numbers
   - Go/no-go recommendation with reasoning

2. Synthesis Report (Markdown or Google Doc)
   - Executive summary (1 paragraph)
   - Top 3 validated problems ranked by frequency × severity
   - Per-problem evidence: frequency, severity, quotes,
     current solutions, desired outcomes
   - Unexpected findings (problems you didn't hypothesize)
   - Recommended next steps

3. Raw Data Archive
   - All transcripts (tagged)
   - Interview tracking log (complete)
   - Recruitment log with conversion rates

4. Design Partner List
   - Interviewees who agreed to follow-up
   - Sorted by engagement level and ICP fit
```

**Output files**:
- `interview-synthesis-report.md` — Primary synthesis document with evidence-backed findings
- `problem-validation-scorecard.json` — Machine-readable scorecard for pipeline consumption
- `interview-tracking-log.csv` — Complete log of all interviews with coded data
- `design-partners.csv` — Qualified follow-up candidates for solution interviews

## Anti-Patterns

### Wrong: Asking "Would you use a product that does X?"
Hypothetical questions produce hypothetical answers. People are terrible at predicting their own future behavior. Asking "would you buy this?" yields compliments and false positives that lead founders to build products nobody actually uses. This is the core insight of the Mom Test — even your mother will lie to you to be supportive. [src1]

### Correct: Ask about past behavior and current pain
Ask "Tell me about the last time you dealt with [problem domain]" and listen for the problem to emerge naturally. Past behavior is the best predictor of future behavior. If they describe your hypothesized problem without you naming it, that is a validated signal.

### Wrong: Stopping at 10-12 interviews because patterns "seem clear"
Early pattern recognition is often confirmation bias. The first 10 interviews tend to confirm your hypothesis because you unconsciously seek confirmation. True saturation requires hearing the same patterns from people you did not expect to agree. [src6]

### Correct: Continue until 3 consecutive interviews yield no new themes
The saturation rule: conduct at least 20 interviews, and continue until 3 in a row surface zero new problem themes. This typically happens between interview 25 and 35.

### Wrong: Synthesizing all interviews in one marathon session at the end
Batch synthesis at the end produces shallow analysis. You lose the contextual memory of tone, hesitation, and emphasis that informs severity coding. You also miss the opportunity to adapt your script based on emerging themes. [src2]

### Correct: Synthesize in batches of 8-10, within 48 hours
Process each batch while the conversations are fresh. Update your theme clusters and tracking log. Adjust questions for the next batch based on emerging patterns.

## When This Matters

Use this recipe when a founder or product team needs to validate that a real, severe, frequent problem exists before investing in building a solution. It produces structured evidence that replaces gut feeling with data. The output feeds directly into solution interview design, MVP scoping, and investor pitch evidence. Requires an ICP definition as input — without knowing who to interview, the process produces noise instead of signal.
