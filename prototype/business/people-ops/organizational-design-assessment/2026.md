# Organizational Design Assessment

## Purpose

This assessment evaluates the effectiveness of an organization's structural design across five critical dimensions: span of control optimization, management layer efficiency, role clarity and job architecture, decision rights allocation, and structural agility. The output is a composite maturity score (1-5) that identifies where structural dysfunction is creating drag on execution speed, employee confusion, or excessive management overhead. Use this when diagnosing slow decision-making, unclear accountability, excessive management layers, or preparing for a reorganization. [src4]

## Constraints
<!-- Agents: read before running this assessment with a user. -->

- Requires access to org chart and headcount data with reporting relationships for reliable scoring
- Not meaningful for companies under 25 employees where informal structures are expected
- Optimal spans and layers vary by function — do not apply engineering norms to sales or operations
- Assessment evaluates structure, not culture — a perfectly designed org with toxic leadership still fails
- Re-run after any major reorganization, M&A event, or 50%+ headcount change

## Assessment Dimensions

<!-- Each dimension is scored independently. The structured format lets agents
     walk through this conversationally with a user, one dimension at a time. -->

### Dimension 1: Span of Control

**What this measures**: Whether managers have an appropriate number of direct reports for their function and level — neither too few (expensive, micromanagement risk) nor too many (burnout, neglect risk).

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | No awareness of span metrics; some managers have 1-2 reports while others have 20+; no rationale for variation | Wild variation in spans with no correlation to role complexity; managers with 1-2 reports doing IC work |
| 2 | Emerging | Spans are tracked but not managed; known imbalances exist but not addressed; generic target applied across functions | Spans tracked in HRIS but no action taken; same span target for engineering and call center managers |
| 3 | Defined | Function-specific span targets established; most managers within target range; exceptions documented with rationale | Written span-of-control policy by function; 70%+ of managers within target; exception review process |
| 4 | Managed | Spans actively optimized during reorgs and hiring; data-driven span analysis; manager capability considered in span decisions | Span analysis in every reorg business case; manager effectiveness correlated with span; quarterly span reporting |
| 5 | Optimized | Dynamic span management with real-time monitoring; AI-assisted workload analysis; span adjustments tied to manager development and team performance data | Real-time span dashboards; predictive overload alerts; spans adjust based on team complexity scores |

**Red flags**: Any manager with fewer than 3 direct reports (unless leading other managers); any individual contributor manager with more than 15 reports; average span below 4 organization-wide. [src1]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "What is the average number of direct reports per manager, and how much does it vary across functions?"

### Dimension 2: Management Layer Efficiency

**What this measures**: Whether the organization has the right number of management layers between the CEO and frontline — too many layers slow decisions and increase costs; too few create bottlenecks.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | No awareness of layer count; layers added organically as people were promoted; some paths from CEO to IC have 10+ layers | Cannot answer "how many layers exist"; promotion-driven layer creation; skip-level meetings non-existent |
| 2 | Emerging | Layer count known but not managed; acknowledged as too many or too few but no action taken; layers vary by function without rationale | Layer count documented but static; acknowledged management bloat; no delayering initiatives |
| 3 | Defined | Target layer count established by function and size; recent delayering or restructuring to align; manager-to-IC ratio tracked | Written policy on max layers; recent reorg reduced layers; M:IC ratio reported quarterly |
| 4 | Managed | Layer efficiency actively managed; every new layer requires business justification; regular layer audits; cost of management calculated | Layer addition requires VP approval with ROI; annual management overhead audit; cost-per-layer calculated |
| 5 | Optimized | Minimal viable layers with dynamic team structures; project-based overlay teams; real-time layer impact analysis | Flat structure with functional matrix; team leads vs. permanent managers; layer count benchmarked against peers |

**Red flags**: More than 8 layers from CEO to frontline in a company under 5,000 employees; manager-to-IC ratio above 1:3 (more than 1 manager for every 3 ICs); "manager of one" roles that exist only for title progression. [src2]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "How many management layers exist between your CEO and frontline individual contributors?"

### Dimension 3: Role Clarity & Job Architecture

**What this measures**: Whether roles are clearly defined with documented responsibilities, career levels, and competency expectations — and whether employees understand their scope and how to advance.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | No job descriptions; titles are arbitrary and inconsistent; employees unclear on responsibilities; no career levels defined | No written job descriptions; same title means different things across teams; frequent scope conflicts |
| 2 | Emerging | Some job descriptions exist but outdated; ad hoc titles; career levels informal ("junior, senior, lead" without criteria); role overlap common | Partial JD coverage; title inconsistency across functions; employees cannot articulate next career step |
| 3 | Defined | Comprehensive job architecture with levels, titles, and competency expectations; 80%+ roles have current descriptions; career ladders documented | Formal job families and levels; current JDs for most roles; career ladder visible to employees |
| 4 | Managed | Job architecture actively maintained and calibrated; role clarity verified through employee surveys; scope boundaries enforced; skills-based role design | Annual JD review process; role clarity survey scores >80%; cross-functional RACI documented |
| 5 | Optimized | Dynamic role architecture that adapts to business needs; skills-based job design; internal talent marketplace; AI-assisted role recommendations | Skills taxonomy drives role design; internal mobility enabled by role clarity; continuous role evolution |

**Red flags**: More than 30% of employees cannot describe their top 3 responsibilities; title inflation (Director-level with no direct reports in a company over 100 people); frequent escalation due to unclear ownership. [src4]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "If I asked 10 random employees what their top 3 responsibilities are and who they escalate to, would they give consistent answers?"

### Dimension 4: Decision Rights Allocation

**What this measures**: Whether it is clear who can make which decisions, at what level, and with what authority — and whether decision-making speed matches business needs.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | No documented decision rights; most decisions escalated to founder/CEO; employees afraid to decide without approval; constant bottlenecks | Every non-trivial decision requires CEO input; decisions take weeks; learned helplessness in middle management |
| 2 | Emerging | Some decisions delegated informally; inconsistent authority levels; "shadow decision-makers" who have influence without formal authority | Some managers empowered but inconsistently; key decisions still bottleneck at top; no DACI or RACI framework |
| 3 | Defined | Decision rights documented for key processes; RACI/DACI frameworks exist; spending authority limits defined; most operational decisions made at appropriate level | Written DACI for major decisions; spending authority tiers; 70%+ of decisions made without CEO involvement |
| 4 | Managed | Decision rights actively managed and reviewed; decision speed tracked; decision quality reviewed; empowerment measured in engagement surveys | Decision rights audited annually; decision velocity metrics; empowerment scores tracked |
| 5 | Optimized | Distributed decision-making with clear guardrails; real-time decision tracking; AI-assisted decision routing; decision rights adapt to context | Decision logged and analyzed; AI routes decisions to appropriate level; near-zero decision bottleneck escalation |

**Red flags**: CEO personally approving expenses under $5,000 in a company over 100 people; decisions taking more than 2x industry-average cycle time; employees who say "I need to check with my manager" for routine operational decisions. [src4]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "What's the largest purchase or commitment an individual manager can approve without escalation?"

### Dimension 5: Structural Agility

**What this measures**: How quickly and effectively the organization can restructure, create cross-functional teams, and adapt its design to changing business needs without major disruption.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | Reorganizations are traumatic, infrequent (every 3-5 years), and disruptive; no change management process; org design is static and fragile | Last reorg was chaotic; employees fear reorgs; cross-functional projects require executive intervention to staff |
| 2 | Emerging | Awareness that org design needs to evolve but restructuring is still disruptive; some cross-functional teams exist but are exceptions | Reorgs happen but are poorly managed; cross-functional projects exist but staffing is political |
| 3 | Defined | Regular org design reviews (annual); change management process for reorgs; cross-functional teams can form without executive intervention | Annual org review process; reorg playbook exists; project teams form within 2 weeks |
| 4 | Managed | Continuous org design evolution; reorgs are incremental rather than big-bang; matrix or pod structures enable rapid team formation | Quarterly micro-adjustments; pod/squad model; teams form within days; reorg impact measured |
| 5 | Optimized | Org design is dynamic and fluid; team structures adapt in real-time; internal talent marketplace enables self-selection into roles; minimal reorg disruption | Continuous team evolution; internal gig marketplace; near-zero productivity loss during restructuring |

**Red flags**: No org design changes in 3+ years despite significant growth; every cross-functional initiative requires a VP sponsor to get resources; employees describe the org structure as "confusing" or "outdated." [src5]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "When was the last time your org structure was meaningfully updated, and how disruptive was the process?"

## Scoring & Interpretation

### Overall Score Calculation

All five dimensions are weighted equally, as each represents a critical structural capability.

```
Overall Score = (Span of Control + Layer Efficiency + Role Clarity + Decision Rights + Structural Agility) / 5
```

### Score Interpretation

| Overall Score | Maturity Level | Interpretation | Recommended Next Step |
|---------------|---------------|----------------|----------------------|
| 1.0 - 1.9 | Critical | Org design is creating significant drag — unclear accountabilities, decision bottlenecks, and structural bloat are actively impeding execution | Engage org design consultant; conduct span-and-layer analysis; establish job architecture as foundation |
| 2.0 - 2.9 | Developing | Structure exists but is organic and unmanaged. Growing pains are visible — confusion, slow decisions, duplicated efforts | Define span targets by function; document decision rights; create job architecture; establish annual org review |
| 3.0 - 3.9 | Competent | Sound structural foundation. Core processes work, but optimization opportunities exist in agility and advanced role design | Optimize spans and layers; implement skills-based role design; accelerate decision delegation |
| 4.0 - 4.5 | Advanced | Structure enables rather than constrains. High clarity, efficient layers, and effective decision distribution. Minor gaps in agility | Focus on dynamic teaming; implement talent marketplace; automate org analytics |
| 4.6 - 5.0 | Best-in-class | Org design is a competitive advantage — fluid, adaptive, and data-driven. Rare outside of tech-forward organizations | Maintain and innovate. Pilot AI-assisted org design. Share best practices externally |

### Dimension-Level Action Routing

| Weak Dimension (Score < 3) | Fetch This Card |
|----------------------------|-----------------|
| Span of Control | Conduct function-specific span analysis using benchmarks: 3-6 for complex, 8-12 for standardized roles |
| Management Layers | Run delayering analysis — target 5-7 layers for companies under 5,000 employees |
| Role Clarity | Build job architecture: define job families, levels, competencies, and career ladders |
| Decision Rights | Implement DACI framework; define spending authority tiers; measure decision velocity |
| Structural Agility | Establish annual org review cycle; create reorg playbook; pilot cross-functional pod model |

## Common Pitfalls in Assessment

- **Confusing org chart accuracy with org design quality**: Having an up-to-date org chart does not mean the design is effective. Many companies have accurate charts documenting a dysfunctional structure. [src4]
- **Applying uniform span targets across functions**: A 1:10 span is excellent for a sales manager with experienced reps but dangerous for an engineering manager with junior developers. Function-specific targets are essential. [src1]
- **Optimizing structure without addressing decision rights**: Delayering without delegating decision authority simply creates bottlenecks at fewer levels. Structure and decision rights must evolve together. [src2]
- **Treating reorganization as the solution**: Reorgs address structural problems but not cultural, process, or leadership problems. If the issue is management quality, restructuring around bad managers does not fix it.
