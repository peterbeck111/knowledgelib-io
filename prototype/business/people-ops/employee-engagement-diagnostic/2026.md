# Employee Engagement Diagnostic

## Purpose

This assessment evaluates the maturity of an organization's employee engagement measurement and response system across five critical dimensions: survey methodology and measurement, driver analysis and insights, action planning and follow-through, manager enablement, and retention risk identification. The output is a composite maturity score (1-5) that identifies where the engagement system is failing — not whether employees are engaged, but whether the organization has the capability to measure, understand, and act on engagement. Use this when diagnosing why engagement initiatives are not moving the needle, evaluating whether the organization is ready for a major engagement transformation, or assessing the engagement function after a leadership change. [src1]

## Constraints
<!-- Agents: read before running this assessment with a user. -->

- Requires at least one completed engagement or pulse survey for evidence-based scoring
- Not meaningful for companies under 20 employees where direct conversation is more effective
- Engagement scores are context-dependent — post-layoff or post-raise surveys are not representative baselines
- This assessment evaluates the system and process, not the engagement level itself — a mature system with low scores is better than no system
- Re-run annually; supplement with quarterly pulse checks

## Assessment Dimensions

### Dimension 1: Survey Methodology & Measurement

**What this measures**: Whether the organization has a scientifically valid, well-executed approach to measuring employee engagement — covering survey design, cadence, participation, and data quality.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | No engagement measurement; leadership relies on gut feel; no surveys conducted; attrition is the only signal | No survey data; leadership says "our culture is great" without evidence; turnover surprises |
| 2 | Emerging | Occasional surveys (annual or less); simple satisfaction questions; low participation; no benchmarking | Annual survey with <50% participation; generic questions not tied to engagement framework; results delayed months |
| 3 | Defined | Regular engagement surveys (annual + pulse); validated question framework; 70%+ participation; results within 2-4 weeks; benchmarked against industry | Validated engagement index (Cronbach alpha >0.80); quarterly pulse surveys; participation tracked; benchmarks available |
| 4 | Managed | Continuous listening strategy with multiple channels (surveys, pulse, lifecycle, exit); 80%+ participation; real-time results; longitudinal trend analysis | Multi-channel feedback system; real-time dashboards; 3+ years of trend data; survey fatigue monitored |
| 5 | Optimized | AI-powered continuous listening with natural language analysis; sentiment detection from multiple signals; predictive engagement modeling; personalized survey experience | AI analyzes open-ended responses; passive signal detection (meeting patterns, collaboration tools); predictive engagement scores |

**Red flags**: No engagement survey in the past 18 months; participation below 50%; results take more than 6 weeks to share with teams; questions not validated against engagement outcomes. [src1]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "When was your last engagement survey, what was the participation rate, and how long did it take to share results?"

### Dimension 2: Driver Analysis & Insights

**What this measures**: Whether the organization can identify what specifically drives (or erodes) engagement — moving beyond top-line scores to understand causal factors and priority areas.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | No analysis beyond top-line scores; leadership interprets results based on personal experience; no driver identification | Overall score reported with no decomposition; "let's focus on communication" without data support |
| 2 | Emerging | Basic breakdowns by department/tenure; some open-ended question themes identified; no statistical driver analysis | Results cut by department; manual theme coding of comments; no regression or correlation analysis |
| 3 | Defined | Statistical driver analysis identifying top engagement drivers; results segmented by manager, team, demographic, and tenure; heatmaps for targeted action | Regression-based driver ranking; manager-level heatmaps; demographic cuts; comparison to prior periods |
| 4 | Managed | Real-time driver analytics with dynamic updates; cross-survey trend analysis; predictive driver modeling; inclusion and equity lens applied to driver analysis | Real-time driver engine; driver shifts detected automatically; equity analysis identifies differential engagement drivers |
| 5 | Optimized | AI-driven driver analysis with natural language processing of open-ended responses; causal inference modeling; individual-level driver profiles; prescriptive recommendations | AI identifies emerging themes from comments; causal models distinguish correlation from causation; personalized manager insights |

**Red flags**: Leadership cannot name the top 3 engagement drivers for the organization; driver analysis has not changed in 3+ years despite business changes; open-ended comments ignored or unanalyzed. [src3]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "What are the top 3 drivers of engagement in your organization, and how do you know?"

### Dimension 3: Action Planning & Follow-Through

**What this measures**: Whether engagement insights actually lead to meaningful action — the gap between "we surveyed" and "we changed something" is where most engagement programs fail.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | Survey results collected but no action taken; employees see no change; survey fatigue and cynicism growing | Results filed away; no action plans created; employees say "nothing changes after the survey" |
| 2 | Emerging | Some actions taken by individual managers but no systematic follow-through; no accountability for action plans; company-wide themes acknowledged but not addressed | Sporadic manager actions; no tracking of action plan completion; annual "we heard you" communication |
| 3 | Defined | Formal action planning process with team-level and company-level plans; accountability for completion; progress communicated to employees; 60%+ of action plans completed | Manager action plan template; quarterly progress tracking; employee communication of actions taken; HR reviews completion |
| 4 | Managed | Action plans tied to business objectives; impact measured (did engagement score improve?); closed-loop communication (survey -> action -> resurvey); managers coached on action planning | Impact measurement on each action; closed-loop process validated; manager coaching; 80%+ plan completion |
| 5 | Optimized | AI-suggested actions based on driver analysis; real-time impact tracking; continuous improvement cycle; employees contribute to and co-create solutions | AI recommends targeted actions; real-time impact dashboards; employee co-creation forums; action fatigue monitoring |

**Red flags**: Engagement scores flat or declining despite surveys being conducted; 39% of HR teams cite engagement as a top priority but less than 50% have formal action planning processes; employees express survey cynicism. [src2]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "After your last engagement survey, what specific actions were taken, and did you communicate those actions back to employees?"

### Dimension 4: Manager Enablement

**What this measures**: Whether managers are equipped, trained, and supported to understand and act on engagement data for their teams — managers are the primary lever for team-level engagement.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | Managers receive no engagement data; no training on people leadership; engagement is "HR's job"; manager quality varies wildly | Managers never see survey results; no people management training; HR owns all engagement initiatives |
| 2 | Emerging | Managers receive aggregate team results but no guidance on interpretation or action; minimal management training; some managers act, most do not | Managers get PDF reports; no training on action planning; engagement action is optional for managers |
| 3 | Defined | Managers receive team-level results with context and benchmarks; manager effectiveness training includes engagement; HR provides action planning templates and support | Manager-accessible dashboards; annual manager training; HR supports action planning; manager accountability established |
| 4 | Managed | Managers receive real-time engagement indicators; trained to identify and address disengagement; manager impact on engagement measured; coaching available for struggling managers | Real-time team health indicators; manager engagement impact scored; coaching for low-engagement managers; manager NPS tracked |
| 5 | Optimized | AI-powered manager insights with personalized recommendations; predictive alerts for team disengagement; manager development linked to engagement outcomes; manager-as-coach culture embedded | AI nudges managers to take action; predictive team health alerts; manager development driven by engagement impact data |

**Red flags**: Managers say "I never see the survey results"; no manager training on engagement; engagement is positioned as an "HR initiative" rather than a leadership responsibility. [src2]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "Do managers receive their team's engagement data, and are they trained on how to act on it?"

### Dimension 5: Retention Risk Identification

**What this measures**: Whether the organization can identify employees at risk of leaving before they resign — and whether engagement data feeds into proactive retention interventions.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | No retention risk identification; turnover is a surprise; exit interviews are the only data source; reactive retention (counter-offers only) | Every resignation is unexpected; no stay interviews; counter-offer is the only tool; no retention analytics |
| 2 | Emerging | Basic attrition tracking by department and tenure; exit interview themes identified but not systematized; some awareness of flight risk factors | Turnover tracked quarterly; exit interview data collected but analysis sporadic; no predictive indicators |
| 3 | Defined | Retention risk framework identifies common flight risk factors; stay interviews conducted for critical roles; engagement survey data linked to turnover predictions; proactive retention plans for key talent | Flight risk factors documented; stay interviews for top 20% talent; engagement-turnover correlation analyzed; retention plans for critical roles |
| 4 | Managed | Predictive retention models using engagement, tenure, and performance data; real-time flight risk dashboards; proactive intervention triggers; retention cost modeling | Predictive flight risk scores; automated alerts for at-risk employees; retention intervention ROI tracked; manager-accessible dashboards |
| 5 | Optimized | AI-powered retention intelligence integrating engagement, compensation, career trajectory, and external market signals; personalized retention strategies; near-zero regretted attrition | AI predicts flight risk 3-6 months out; personalized retention interventions; regretted attrition below 3%; continuous retention modeling |

**Red flags**: More than 50% of voluntary exits were "surprises" to the manager; no stay interview program; exit interview data collected but never analyzed or acted on; retention is reactive (counter-offer only). [src5]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "What percentage of voluntary resignations in the past year were genuinely unexpected by the direct manager?"

## Scoring & Interpretation

### Overall Score Calculation

All five dimensions are weighted equally.

```
Overall Score = (Measurement + Driver Analysis + Action Planning + Manager Enablement + Retention Risk) / 5
```

### Score Interpretation

| Overall Score | Maturity Level | Interpretation | Recommended Next Step |
|---------------|---------------|----------------|----------------------|
| 1.0 - 1.9 | Critical | No engagement measurement system — flying blind on workforce sentiment. Attrition risks are invisible until resignations arrive. | Deploy first engagement survey within 30 days; implement eNPS as immediate pulse metric; conduct stay interviews for top performers |
| 2.0 - 2.9 | Developing | Basic measurement exists but insights are shallow and actions are sporadic. The organization surveys but does not systematically respond. | Implement driver analysis; create formal action planning process; train managers on engagement data interpretation |
| 3.0 - 3.9 | Competent | Solid engagement measurement and action system. The organization can diagnose, plan, and act on engagement. Ready for optimization. | Add continuous listening channels; implement predictive retention models; strengthen manager enablement |
| 4.0 - 4.5 | Advanced | Engagement system is a competitive advantage. Data-driven, action-oriented, with strong manager enablement. Minor gaps in predictive capability. | Invest in AI-powered analytics; build causal inference models; personalize the engagement experience |
| 4.6 - 5.0 | Best-in-class | World-class engagement system with predictive capabilities, personalized interventions, and measurable business impact. Rare outside of best-employer organizations. | Maintain leadership; publish thought leadership; mentor other organizations; innovate with emerging technology |

## Common Pitfalls in Assessment

- **Surveying without acting**: The most common and destructive pattern. Conducting engagement surveys without visible follow-through creates cynicism that is harder to reverse than never surveying at all. 39% of HR teams cite engagement as a priority but fewer than half have formal action processes. [src2]
- **Over-relying on eNPS**: eNPS is a useful single-number indicator but is insufficient as a standalone engagement measure. It does not identify drivers, segments, or actionable insights. Always pair with multi-dimension surveys. [src4]
- **Ignoring manager variance**: Organization-wide engagement scores mask massive manager-level variance. A company with 72% engagement may have teams at 95% and teams at 35%. Manager-level analysis is essential. [src3]
- **Confusing satisfaction with engagement**: Satisfied employees may not be productive or committed. Engaged employees are emotionally invested in outcomes. Survey questions must measure engagement (effort, advocacy, intent to stay), not just satisfaction (happiness, comfort). [src1]
