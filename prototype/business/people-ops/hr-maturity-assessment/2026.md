# HR Maturity Assessment

## Purpose

This assessment evaluates the maturity of a company's HR function across six critical dimensions: talent acquisition, onboarding and employee experience, performance management, compensation and benefits, learning and development, and HR compliance and analytics. The output is a composite maturity score (1-5) that identifies the weakest links in the people function and routes to specific improvement areas. SHRM research shows that only 1 in 8 HR functions perform at a high maturity level, with the average score at 3.85 out of 6 across nearly 1,300 organizations — indicating significant room for improvement across the industry. [src1]

## Constraints
<!-- Agents: read before running this assessment with a user. -->

- Requires access to HRIS data, recruiting metrics, turnover data, and compensation benchmarks for reliable scoring
- Not meaningful for companies with fewer than 20 employees — at that stage, HR is typically a founder responsibility
- HR maturity expectations differ by industry and region — tech companies versus manufacturing have different baselines
- Assessment is diagnostic — identifies HR capability gaps but does not prescribe specific policies or programs
- Re-run annually or after major organizational changes (mergers, layoffs, rapid scaling, leadership transitions)

## Assessment Dimensions

<!-- Each dimension is scored independently. The structured format lets agents
     walk through this conversationally with a user, one dimension at a time. -->

### Dimension 1: Talent Acquisition

**What this measures**: The effectiveness and maturity of the recruiting function — from sourcing through offer acceptance — including process consistency, employer brand, and hiring quality.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | No structured recruiting process; hiring is reactive and manager-driven; no ATS; no employer brand effort | Managers post to job boards ad hoc; no interview scorecard; time-to-fill not tracked; no career page; hiring based on urgency not planning |
| 2 | Emerging | ATS in use; basic structured interviews for some roles; employer brand exists but is not actively managed; reactive recruiting | ATS adopted but underutilized; some roles have structured interviews; career page exists but is generic; time-to-fill tracked but not optimized |
| 3 | Defined | Structured interview process for all roles with scorecards; proactive sourcing pipeline for key roles; employer brand actively managed; time-to-fill under 45 days for most roles | Interview scorecards standard; sourcing pipeline for top 5 roles; career page showcases culture; recruiting metrics dashboard; diversity of candidate sources tracked |
| 4 | Managed | Data-driven recruiting with quality-of-hire tracking; predictive sourcing; talent pipeline for critical roles; DEI embedded in recruiting; time-to-fill under 35 days | Quality-of-hire measured at 6 and 12 months; predictive talent mapping; DEI targets set and tracked; recruiter productivity benchmarked; candidate NPS tracked |
| 5 | Optimized | AI-enhanced talent acquisition; predictive hiring models; talent marketplace and internal mobility platform; recruiting is a strategic function with board-level visibility | AI screens and matches candidates; internal mobility fills 25%+ of roles; recruiting influences headcount planning; employer brand is a measurable competitive advantage |

**Red flags**: No ATS or ATS used only as a resume repository; no structured interview process; time-to-fill unknown; offer acceptance rate below 70%; no sourcing beyond job board postings. [src2]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "Do you have a structured interview process with scorecards for every role, and do you track time-to-fill and quality-of-hire?"

### Dimension 2: Onboarding & Employee Experience

**What this measures**: How effectively the company integrates new hires and manages the overall employee experience from day one through their tenure.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | No structured onboarding; new hires figure things out on their own; no employee experience measurement | First day is "here's your laptop, good luck"; no onboarding checklist; no 30/60/90-day plan; no employee survey; no exit interviews |
| 2 | Emerging | Basic onboarding checklist (IT setup, paperwork); 1-2 day orientation; some manager-level 30-day plans; annual employee survey | Onboarding checklist exists but varies by manager; orientation is HR presentation + IT setup; annual engagement survey with low participation |
| 3 | Defined | Structured 90-day onboarding program with milestones; buddy/mentor assignment; quarterly pulse surveys; new hire feedback at 30/60/90 days | 90-day onboarding with clear milestones; mentor assigned; new hire surveys at 30/60/90; quarterly pulse surveys with 70%+ participation; basic action plans |
| 4 | Managed | Personalized onboarding by role and seniority; continuous employee listening (pulse + lifecycle surveys); employee experience team or owner; eNPS tracked | Role-specific onboarding paths; lifecycle surveys (onboarding, anniversary, exit); eNPS above 30; dedicated employee experience function; action plans from survey data |
| 5 | Optimized | AI-personalized onboarding; continuous real-time employee sentiment; employee experience is a strategic priority with executive sponsorship; onboarding-to-productivity time measured | AI-recommended onboarding content; real-time sentiment analysis; time-to-productivity tracked and optimized; employee experience metrics reported to board; below-market voluntary turnover |

**Red flags**: No onboarding process beyond paperwork; new hire turnover above 20% in first 90 days; no employee engagement survey; exit interviews not conducted or not analyzed. [src4]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "What does your onboarding program look like for the first 90 days, and do you survey new hires about their onboarding experience?"

### Dimension 3: Performance Management

**What this measures**: How the company sets expectations, provides feedback, evaluates performance, and develops talent through its performance management system.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | No formal performance reviews; feedback is informal and inconsistent; no goal-setting process; performance not documented | No review cadence; goals not written down; promotions based on tenure or advocacy; no performance improvement process; no manager training on feedback |
| 2 | Emerging | Annual performance reviews exist but are a compliance exercise; goals set annually but not tracked; basic competency framework for some roles | Annual review form exists; goals set in January and revisited in December; reviews are check-the-box; no calibration; manager quality of reviews varies wildly |
| 3 | Defined | Semi-annual or quarterly review cycles; OKRs or goals cascade from company objectives; calibration sessions standardize ratings; manager training on feedback | Quarterly goal check-ins; calibration sessions at least annually; performance ratings tied to clear criteria; managers trained on feedback delivery; PIPs documented |
| 4 | Managed | Continuous feedback culture with real-time recognition; 360-degree feedback for leadership; performance data integrated with development plans and succession planning | Real-time feedback tools (Lattice, Culture Amp, etc.); 360 reviews for managers; performance linked to development plans; succession planning for key roles; manager effectiveness tracked |
| 5 | Optimized | AI-supported performance management; continuous performance signals from multiple sources; performance management drives strategic talent decisions; high-performance culture is measurable | AI aggregates performance signals; continuous calibration; performance data drives compensation, development, and succession simultaneously; performance management is a competitive advantage |

**Red flags**: No formal review process; annual reviews that are 100% manager narrative with no criteria; no calibration across managers; no connection between performance ratings and compensation or development; no PIP process. [src3]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "How often do formal performance reviews occur, and is there a calibration process to ensure consistent ratings across managers?"

### Dimension 4: Compensation & Benefits

**What this measures**: How well the company's compensation and benefits strategy attracts, retains, and motivates talent relative to market and internal equity.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | No compensation philosophy; pay set ad hoc by negotiation; no market data; benefits are statutory minimums | Every offer is a negotiation; no salary bands; pay equity unknown; benefits are basic health insurance only; no comp review process |
| 2 | Emerging | Basic salary ranges exist for some roles; some market data used (Glassdoor/Levels.fyi); annual comp review for all employees; standard benefits package | Informal salary ranges; market data checked sporadically; annual raise cycle but inconsistent methodology; benefits package competitive but not differentiated |
| 3 | Defined | Compensation philosophy documented; salary bands for all roles benchmarked to market data (Radford, Mercer, Pave); pay equity audit completed; benefits competitive with peer group | Written comp philosophy (e.g., "target 75th percentile"); salary bands published internally; annual pay equity analysis; benefits benchmarked against peers; equity/RSU program structured |
| 4 | Managed | Total rewards strategy including base, variable, equity, and benefits; real-time market data for comp decisions; transparent leveling framework; proactive retention compensation for flight risks | Total rewards philosophy; real-time comp data (Pave, Carta); transparent levels and bands; proactive retention reviews; geographic pay strategy defined; benefits personalization offered |
| 5 | Optimized | AI-driven compensation optimization; predictive retention compensation; personalized total rewards packages; compensation is a strategic tool for talent strategy | AI recommends compensation actions; predictive attrition model triggers retention offers; cafeteria-style benefits; comp strategy aligned with workforce planning; board-approved comp philosophy |

**Red flags**: No salary bands; compensation set purely by negotiation; no market data used; pay equity never audited; significant gender or racial pay gaps; benefits below market. [src5]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "Do you have documented salary bands for every role, and when was your last pay equity audit?"

### Dimension 5: Learning & Development

**What this measures**: How systematically the company develops employee skills, supports career growth, and builds organizational capability.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | No L&D budget or program; learning is entirely self-directed; no career pathing; no manager development | No L&D budget; no training beyond compliance; career paths not documented; managers promoted without management training |
| 2 | Emerging | Basic L&D budget (conference attendance, course stipends); compliance training exists; some ad hoc management training | Annual learning stipend; compliance training automated; occasional management workshop; no career framework; no skills gap analysis |
| 3 | Defined | Structured L&D program with learning paths by role; manager development program; career ladders documented for key roles; annual skills assessment | Career ladders for IC and management tracks; new manager training program; L&D catalog with required and optional courses; annual development plans linked to performance |
| 4 | Managed | Learning ecosystem with internal and external content; mentorship program; leadership development pipeline; skills taxonomy linked to workforce planning | LMS or learning platform adopted; mentorship program active; leadership pipeline identified; skills gaps inform hiring priorities; L&D ROI tracked |
| 5 | Optimized | AI-personalized learning recommendations; internal talent marketplace; continuous skill development integrated into daily work; L&D is a strategic capability builder | AI recommends learning based on role and goals; internal mobility marketplace; learning in the flow of work; L&D directly linked to business outcomes; upskilling addresses strategic capability gaps |

**Red flags**: No L&D budget; managers promoted without any management training; no career ladders or progression framework; compliance training is the only structured learning; turnover driven by lack of development opportunities. [src6]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "Do you have documented career ladders and a management development program, and what is your annual L&D spend per employee?"

### Dimension 6: HR Compliance & Analytics

**What this measures**: How well HR manages regulatory compliance and uses data and analytics to inform strategic decisions.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | Compliance managed reactively (fix problems after they occur); no HR dashboards or metrics; employee records disorganized | No compliance calendar; employee files incomplete; no HR metrics tracked; payroll errors frequent; no handbook or outdated handbook |
| 2 | Emerging | Basic compliance processes (handbook, I-9s, required postings); HRIS in place for records; basic metrics tracked (headcount, turnover) | Employee handbook current; HRIS manages records; headcount and turnover reported quarterly; compliance training exists but inconsistent |
| 3 | Defined | Proactive compliance management with calendar and audits; HR dashboard with key metrics; turnover analyzed by segment; employee relations cases documented | Compliance calendar managed; quarterly HR dashboard; turnover analyzed by department/tenure/demographics; employee relations tracking system; annual compliance audit |
| 4 | Managed | Advanced HR analytics — predictive turnover models, workforce planning, DEI analytics; compliance automated where possible; HR data informs business strategy | Predictive turnover model; workforce planning 12-18 months ahead; DEI dashboard with goals; HR presents data-driven insights to leadership; automated compliance monitoring |
| 5 | Optimized | AI-powered people analytics; real-time organizational health dashboards; HR data fully integrated with business data; compliance-by-design architecture | AI identifies attrition risks 60 days ahead; organizational network analysis; HR analytics team or function; real-time compliance monitoring; people data drives business decisions at board level |

**Red flags**: No employee handbook or handbook not updated in 2+ years; no HRIS; turnover rate unknown; no compliance audit in 12+ months; employee records kept in spreadsheets; no HR metrics reported to leadership. [src1]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "What HR metrics do you report to leadership, and when was your last compliance audit?"

## Scoring & Interpretation

### Overall Score Calculation

All dimensions weighted equally for a general assessment. For companies experiencing high turnover, weight Talent Acquisition and Compensation more heavily (1.5x).

```
Overall Score = (Talent Acquisition + Onboarding + Performance Mgmt + Compensation + L&D + Compliance & Analytics) / 6
```

### Score Interpretation

| Overall Score | Maturity Level | Interpretation | Recommended Next Step |
|---------------|---------------|----------------|----------------------|
| 1.0 - 1.9 | Critical | HR is a compliance risk and talent liability; high turnover likely; significant legal exposure | Implement HRIS, create employee handbook, establish structured hiring process, conduct pay equity audit |
| 2.0 - 2.9 | Developing | Basic HR operations functional but reactive; processes inconsistent; limited strategic value | Standardize interview process, build 90-day onboarding, implement quarterly reviews, benchmark compensation |
| 3.0 - 3.9 | Competent | Solid HR foundation with structured processes; ready for strategic evolution | Build predictive analytics, develop leadership pipeline, implement total rewards strategy, launch DEI program |
| 4.0 - 4.5 | Advanced | HR is a strategic partner driving business outcomes through people decisions | Deploy AI-powered tools, build talent marketplace, implement workforce planning, develop people analytics capability |
| 4.6 - 5.0 | Best-in-class | HR is a competitive advantage; people strategy drives business strategy | Maintain excellence; innovate with AI; develop organizational agility; external employer brand recognition |

## Common Pitfalls in Assessment

- **Compliance-first myopia**: Many HR teams focus exclusively on compliance and administrative tasks, neglecting strategic capabilities like talent development and workforce planning. A compliant HR function that does not develop talent is underperforming. [src3]
- **Process-maturity confusion**: Having sophisticated HR technology does not equal process maturity. A company with Workday and no structured performance management process is less mature than one with simple tools and strong processes.
- **Self-assessment inflation**: HR teams consistently over-score on "soft" dimensions (culture, employee experience) and under-score on measurable dimensions (analytics, compensation). Require evidence-based scoring — dashboards, documented processes, and metric histories.
- **One-size-fits-all benchmarks**: A 50-person startup should not be compared to a 5,000-person enterprise on HR maturity. Use segment-appropriate benchmarks and focus on dimensions that are critical for the company's current stage.

## When This Matters

Fetch when a user asks to evaluate their HR function, is building an HR team from scratch, preparing for rapid scaling (doubling headcount), experiencing high turnover, onboarding a new CHRO, or preparing for due diligence in M&A or fundraising.
