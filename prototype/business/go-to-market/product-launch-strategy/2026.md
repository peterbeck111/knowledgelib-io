# Product Launch Strategy Decision Framework

## Summary

This framework helps choose between four launch strategies: big bang (simultaneous full-scale launch), soft launch (limited release to test), beta (invite-only feedback period), and early access (exclusive preview for advocates). 95% of product launches fail, and launch strategy significantly impacts success probability. Soft launches improve outcomes for 70% of startups by allowing iteration before full commitment. Big bang launches maximize initial impact but are irreversible — negative first impressions compound through reviews and social media. The default recommendation for most products is beta followed by soft launch, transitioning to full launch only after validation. [src1]

## Constraints

- 95% of product launches fail due to poor planning or wrong strategy — the stakes are high
- Big bang launches are one-way doors — a failed big bang poisons brand perception and is expensive to recover from
- Beta and early access require 4-8 weeks minimum to gather meaningful feedback
- Launch marketing budgets for new products average 8.1% of revenue — must be pre-allocated before launch date
- Launch strategy must match product maturity — shipping a buggy product via big bang destroys credibility

## Decision Inputs

| Input | Why It Matters | How to Assess |
|-------|---------------|---------------|
| Product readiness | Buggy products need soft/beta; polished products can big bang | Run QA audit; count P0/P1 bugs; test core user flows |
| Market awareness | New categories need education (soft launch); known categories can big bang | Check search volume for product category terms |
| Risk tolerance | Low tolerance demands phased approach; high tolerance enables big bang | Ask: can we survive a failed launch? |
| Existing audience | Audience enables early access/beta; no audience limits to soft launch | Count email list, waitlist, community members |
| Competitive timing | Competitor launches may force faster timeline | Monitor competitor release schedules and announcements |

## Decision Logic

### If product is battle-tested AND known category AND budget > $50K
→ **Big bang launch**. Maximize initial impact with simultaneous media, paid, and event activation. 80% of successful launches align with audience preferences — big bang works when you know your audience well. [src1]

### If product has rough edges AND have access to beta testers
→ **Beta (4-8 weeks) then soft launch**. 70% of startups improve after a soft launch. Use beta to validate core flows and fix critical bugs. Transition to soft launch when NPS > 30 and core metrics stabilize. [src3]

### If new category AND team has existing audience or waitlist
→ **Early access then phased launch**. Use exclusivity to create urgency and turn early users into advocates. Early access users become brand ambassadors with higher LTV. [src2]

### If product is MVP AND need market validation
→ **Closed beta with iteration loops**. Ship to 50-200 users, gather feedback, iterate, expand beta. Only move to soft launch after product-market fit signals (retention > 40% at day 30). [src4]

### Default recommendation
→ **Beta then soft launch then full launch**. This phased approach works for most products because it reduces risk while building momentum and social proof. 75% of startups using soft launches manage resources better. [src3]

## Anti-Patterns

### Wrong: Big bang launch with unvalidated product
Companies spend $100K+ on a big bang launch for a product that has never been used by real customers. Launch day reveals critical bugs, negative reviews accumulate, and the brand takes months to recover. 65% of failed launches are attributed to lack of readiness. [src4]

### Correct: Validate before amplifying
Run a minimum 4-week beta or soft launch with 50-500 real users. Fix P0/P1 issues. Verify core value metrics (activation rate, retention, NPS). Only proceed to full launch when product proves it delivers value. [src3]

### Wrong: Perpetual beta without committing to launch
Teams hide behind "beta" indefinitely, avoiding the marketing commitment of a full launch. The product never gains momentum because there is no event to rally around or press to generate. [src2]

### Correct: Set beta exit criteria upfront
Define measurable launch criteria before starting beta: NPS score, bug count, activation rate, day-30 retention. When criteria are met, commit to a launch date within 30 days. [src6]

### Wrong: Soft launch without a plan to transition to full launch
Companies soft-launch and then never scale marketing because early traction feels comfortable. The soft launch becomes the permanent state, and growth stalls. [src1]

### Correct: Build the full launch plan before soft launching
Create the full launch marketing plan (budget, channels, content, PR) during the soft launch period. Use soft launch data to refine messaging and targeting. Execute full launch within 4-8 weeks of soft launch completion.
