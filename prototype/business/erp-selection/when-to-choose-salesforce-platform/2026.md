# When to Choose Salesforce Platform

## Definition

Salesforce Platform refers to using Salesforce beyond its CRM origins as a comprehensive business application platform — encompassing Sales Cloud, Service Cloud, Marketing Cloud, Commerce Cloud, Data Cloud, Industry Clouds, and custom application development via the Lightning Platform (formerly Force.com). [src1] With 20.7% global CRM market share as of 2025 and adoption by 50% of Fortune 100 companies for its Data Cloud and AI offerings, Salesforce has evolved from a sales tool into strategic infrastructure that some organizations use to replace or augment traditional ERP functions through its AppExchange ecosystem and native platform capabilities. [src2] However, it remains fundamentally a CRM-first platform — not an ERP — and misusing it as a full ERP replacement creates significant technical and financial risks.

## Key Properties

- **Market position**: 20.7% global CRM market share for 12th consecutive year; 150,000+ customers worldwide; $900M ARR from Data Cloud and AI offerings alone [src2]
- **Platform scope**: Sales Cloud, Service Cloud, Marketing Cloud, Commerce Cloud, Data Cloud, Experience Cloud, Industry Clouds (Financial Services, Health, Manufacturing, etc.), plus AppExchange (7,000+ apps) [src5]
- **AI capabilities**: Einstein AI, Agentforce (autonomous AI agents), Prompt Studio, and Data Cloud — positioned as an AI-first platform since 2024 [src4]
- **What it is NOT**: Salesforce has no native general ledger, no manufacturing execution, no warehouse management, and no supply chain planning — these require AppExchange partners (e.g., Accounting Seed, Certinia, Rootstock) or integration with traditional ERP [src3]
- **Cost structure**: Per-user subscription starting at $25/user/month (Essentials) to $500+/user/month (Unlimited+); costs escalate with additional Clouds, API calls, storage, and AI features [src2]

## Constraints

- Salesforce governor limits cap API calls (100K-1M/day depending on edition), SOQL queries (50K/transaction), and data storage (10GB base + per-user allocation) — high-volume transactional workloads will hit these walls [src3]
- AppExchange ERP add-ons (Certinia, Rootstock, Accounting Seed) are not Salesforce products — they are ISV applications with separate pricing, support contracts, and upgrade cycles, adding vendor management complexity [src3]
- Heavily customized Salesforce orgs (extensive Apex code, custom objects, complex flows) become progressively harder to maintain and upgrade — the "customization tax" grows non-linearly [src1]
- Salesforce platform costs compound: a mid-market company starting with Sales Cloud ($150/user/month) often reaches $400-600/user/month once Service Cloud, Marketing Cloud, Data Cloud, and premium AI are added [src4]
- The Salesforce talent market is well-established but expensive — certified administrators earn $95K-$130K, developers $120K-$170K, and architects $160K-$220K in the US [src2]

## Application Checklist

### Step 1: Classify workloads as CRM-native vs ERP-native
- **Inputs needed**: Complete business process inventory categorized by function (sales, service, marketing, finance, operations, HR, supply chain)
- **Output**: A workload map showing which processes Salesforce handles natively, which require AppExchange, and which require a separate system
- **Constraint**: If more than 40% of critical processes are ERP-native (finance, manufacturing, supply chain), Salesforce-as-platform will create more integration complexity than a purpose-built ERP [src3]

### Step 2: Evaluate governor limit impact
- **Inputs needed**: Peak daily transaction volumes, API call patterns, data storage requirements, batch processing needs
- **Output**: A capacity assessment showing whether Salesforce editions and limits can support the workload
- **Constraint**: If any single process exceeds 50% of governor limits at current volume, growth will create bottlenecks — design for 3x current volume to avoid hitting walls within 2 years [src1]

### Step 3: Build total platform cost model
- **Inputs needed**: User count by Cloud/role, API call projections, storage needs, AppExchange subscription costs, integration costs, talent costs
- **Output**: 3-year total cost model including all Salesforce subscriptions, ISV apps, integration middleware, and dedicated Salesforce talent
- **Constraint**: If total per-user cost exceeds $500/month for more than 50% of users, compare TCO against an integrated ERP suite that includes those functions natively [src4]

### Step 4: Assess vendor dependency risk
- **Inputs needed**: Number of AppExchange dependencies, percentage of business processes on Salesforce platform, data portability assessment
- **Output**: Vendor lock-in score and migration cost estimate
- **Constraint**: If more than 80% of business-critical processes run on Salesforce (including ISV apps), the organization has single-vendor dependency equivalent to a monolithic ERP — without the integration benefits [src1]

## Anti-Patterns

### Wrong: Using Salesforce as a full ERP replacement
Organizations attempt to replicate manufacturing, warehouse management, or complex supply chain functions using custom Salesforce objects and Apex code, creating a fragile, expensive, unmaintainable system that neither CRM nor ERP teams can support effectively. [src3]

### Correct: Using Salesforce for CRM-adjacent functions, integrating with purpose-built ERP
Deploy Salesforce for customer-facing processes (sales, service, marketing, commerce) and integrate with a dedicated ERP for back-office operations. Use MuleSoft or another iPaaS for the integration layer. [src1]

### Wrong: Ignoring governor limits during architecture design
Teams design solutions without accounting for API call limits, SOQL query limits, or storage constraints, then hit production walls when transaction volumes grow. Governor limits are not suggestions — they are hard platform boundaries. [src1]

### Correct: Designing for governor limits from day one
Perform a capacity assessment during architecture phase. Use Platform Events for async processing, External Objects for large datasets, and Big Objects for archival — never store high-volume transactional data in standard objects. [src1]

### Wrong: Building custom when AppExchange has a mature solution
Development teams build custom Apex applications for functions (e.g., document generation, e-signatures, CPQ) where mature AppExchange products exist. Custom builds take longer, cost more to maintain, and miss ongoing ISV innovation. [src5]

### Correct: Evaluating AppExchange before custom development
Check AppExchange first. If an ISV product covers 80%+ of requirements with configuration, it will be cheaper and more maintainable than custom development. Reserve custom Apex for truly unique business logic. [src5]

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: Salesforce is an ERP system.
  **Reality**: Salesforce is a CRM platform with extensibility. It lacks native general ledger, manufacturing, warehouse management, and supply chain modules. Organizations can build ERP-like capabilities using AppExchange partners (Certinia, Rootstock, Accounting Seed), but these are ISV products, not Salesforce modules. [src3]

- **Misconception**: Salesforce is only for large enterprises.
  **Reality**: Salesforce offers editions from $25/user/month (Essentials, up to 10 users) to enterprise pricing. However, the total cost of ownership — including implementation, customization, and integration — means mid-market companies typically spend $100K-$500K in year one. Small businesses under 20 users should evaluate HubSpot, Zoho, or Pipedrive first. [src2]

- **Misconception**: Once you are on Salesforce, you should put everything on Salesforce.
  **Reality**: Platform monoculture creates the same risks as monolithic ERP — vendor lock-in, escalating costs, and capability limitations. The Salesforce ecosystem works best as the CRM hub in a composable architecture, not as the only platform in the stack. [src1]
