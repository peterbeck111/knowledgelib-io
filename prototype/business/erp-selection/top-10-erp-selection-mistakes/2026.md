# Top 10 ERP Selection Mistakes

## Definition

ERP selection mistakes are systematic errors made during the vendor evaluation and selection phase of an ERP project that increase the probability of implementation failure, cost overruns, or functional misfit. These mistakes occur before a single line of configuration is written, yet they account for an outsized share of downstream project failures. With 79% of ERP projects experiencing challenges or outright failure — often costing $1.5M+ and dragging on for years — selection-phase errors are the most cost-effective point of intervention. [src4] The 10 mistakes cataloged here are drawn from consulting firm pattern analysis across hundreds of enterprise ERP selections. [src1]

## Key Properties

- **Failure rate context**: Only 23% of ERP implementations are considered successful; many failures trace back to selection-phase decisions [src4]
- **Cost amplification**: A selection mistake that costs $50K to fix during evaluation costs $500K to fix during implementation and $5M+ to fix post-go-live
- **Compounding effect**: Selection mistakes rarely occur in isolation — skipping requirements analysis leads to demo-driven decisions, which leads to post-go-live customization spirals [src1]
- **Industry invariance**: The same 10 mistakes appear across manufacturing, services, retail, and healthcare — the consequences vary but the patterns are constant [src4]
- **Recoverable window**: Most selection mistakes are recoverable if caught before contract signing — after signing, remediation cost increases 10x [src2]

## Constraints

- This unit covers selection-phase errors only — implementation-phase failures (change management, data migration, testing) are covered in a separate unit [src4]
- Mistake severity is context-dependent: a mid-market company selecting its first ERP faces different risk magnitudes than an enterprise replacing a legacy system
- Some mistakes are rational in specific contexts (e.g., demo-driven selection may be acceptable for a 10-person company with simple needs) [src1]
- The ordering reflects frequency, not impact — Mistake #7 (ignoring TCO) may have higher financial impact than Mistake #1 in some organizations
- Avoiding all 10 mistakes requires 3-6 months of selection effort — organizations with shorter timelines must prioritize the top 5 [src2]

## Application Checklist

### Step 1: Validate that requirements exist before vendor contact
- **Inputs needed**: Business process documentation, pain point inventory, compliance requirements
- **Output**: A prioritized requirements document with must-have, should-have, and nice-to-have tiers
- **Constraint**: If the organization cannot articulate 20+ specific requirements before contacting vendors, they are not ready for selection — Mistake #1 in action [src1]

### Step 2: Assemble a cross-functional selection team
- **Inputs needed**: Stakeholder map spanning finance, operations, IT, and end users
- **Output**: A named selection team with executive sponsor, decision-making authority, and allocated time
- **Constraint**: If the selection team is entirely IT-driven with no business process owners, Mistake #4 is guaranteed [src4]

### Step 3: Evaluate vendors against requirements, not demos
- **Inputs needed**: Requirements document, vendor responses, scripted demo scenarios based on real business processes
- **Output**: A weighted scoring matrix evaluated by the cross-functional team
- **Constraint**: If demo scenarios are vendor-designed rather than customer-designed, the evaluation is testing vendor presentation skill, not fit [src1]

### Step 4: Model total cost of ownership over 5-7 years
- **Inputs needed**: Licensing, implementation, customization, integration, training, ongoing support, and exit costs
- **Output**: Full lifecycle TCO comparison across shortlisted vendors
- **Constraint**: If the TCO model excludes post-go-live costs (training, upgrades, support, customization maintenance), it understates true cost by 40-60% [src2]

## Anti-Patterns

### Wrong: Letting the vendor control the demo script
The vendor showcases pre-built scenarios that highlight their strengths and avoid their weaknesses. The selection team is impressed by polished presentations that bear no resemblance to their actual business processes. [src1]

### Correct: Providing vendors with your business scenarios
Write demo scripts based on your most complex, most frequent, and most compliance-critical business processes. Require all vendors to demonstrate against the same scenarios. Compare apples to apples. [src1]

### Wrong: Selecting based on brand reputation alone
An organization selects SAP because "nobody gets fired for buying SAP," without validating fit for their specific size, industry, and process complexity. The implementation requires extensive customization to fit processes the platform was not designed for. [src5]

### Correct: Selecting based on fit for your specific context
Evaluate vendors against your documented requirements, reference customers in your industry and size bracket, and total cost of ownership. Brand reputation is a tiebreaker, not a decision criterion. [src4]

### Wrong: Excluding the CFO from selection and only discovering TCO at contract signing
The IT team selects the best technical fit, but when the contract reaches the CFO, the 5-year TCO is 3x the original budget estimate because implementation, customization, and ongoing costs were not modeled. [src2]

### Correct: Involving finance in TCO modeling from day one
Include a finance representative on the selection team. Model full lifecycle costs before shortlisting vendors. Eliminate vendors that exceed budget before investing in deep evaluation. [src2]

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: The RFP process ensures a fair and thorough evaluation.
  **Reality**: Most ERP RFPs are checkbox exercises that vendors have pre-built responses for. RFPs measure a vendor's proposal-writing ability, not their software's fit. Scripted demos and reference calls are far more revealing than RFP responses. [src1]

- **Misconception**: The most expensive ERP is the best ERP.
  **Reality**: Price correlates with vendor size and market positioning, not with fit for your organization. A $200K NetSuite implementation may outperform a $2M SAP deployment for a 200-person services firm. [src4]

- **Misconception**: ERP selection is primarily a technology decision.
  **Reality**: ERP selection is primarily a business process decision. The technology must fit the processes, not the other way around. Organizations that treat ERP selection as an IT project make mistakes #4, #5, and #8 simultaneously. [src1]

## The 10 Mistakes

| # | Mistake | Consequence | Prevention |
|---|---------|-------------|------------|
| 1 | No documented requirements before vendor contact | Vendor-led evaluation; buying what's sold, not what's needed | Complete requirements before contacting any vendor [src1] |
| 2 | No executive sponsor or passive sponsorship | Project lacks authority to resolve cross-departmental conflicts | Name an active exec sponsor with decision authority [src4] |
| 3 | Demo-driven selection (vendor controls the script) | Evaluating presentation skill, not software fit | Customer-designed demo scenarios from real processes [src1] |
| 4 | IT-only selection team without business process owners | Technical fit without business fit; user adoption failure | Cross-functional team with ops, finance, and end users [src4] |
| 5 | Evaluating features instead of process fit | Checkbox exercise that misses workflow gaps | Map end-to-end processes and test against them [src1] |
| 6 | Ignoring change management requirements in selection | Selected system requires massive behavioral change not budgeted | Assess change impact per vendor during selection [src4] |
| 7 | Ignoring total cost of ownership (licensing only) | 40-60% cost underestimation; budget crisis mid-project | Full 5-7 year TCO model including post-go-live [src2] |
| 8 | Not checking references in your industry and size | Vendor references from dissimilar orgs paint misleading picture | Demand references matching your industry, size, and complexity [src1] |
| 9 | Rushing selection to meet an arbitrary deadline | Compressed evaluation leads to insufficient due diligence | Allow 3-6 months for selection; compress implementation instead [src2] |
| 10 | Failing to negotiate exit terms and data portability | Vendor lock-in with no practical exit path | Negotiate data export rights and exit clauses before signing [src3] |
