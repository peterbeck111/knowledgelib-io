# ESG Reporting

## Definition

ESG (Environmental, Social, and Governance) reporting is the structured disclosure of a company's performance on sustainability and governance metrics to investors, regulators, and stakeholders. The landscape is anchored by several frameworks: GRI (stakeholder-focused impact reporting), SASB/ISSB (investor-focused financial materiality), TCFD (climate risk disclosure, now absorbed into IFRS S2), and CSRD/ESRS (EU mandatory double-materiality reporting). [src1] The central challenge is materiality assessment — determining which ESG topics are significant enough to warrant measurement and disclosure. [src3]

## Key Properties

- **GRI Standards**: Stakeholder-focused; reports on the organization's impact on economy, environment, and people (impact materiality); applicable to any organization globally [src4]
- **SASB/ISSB (IFRS S1/S2)**: Investor-focused; reports on sustainability matters that affect enterprise value (financial materiality); industry-specific standards for 77 sectors [src1]
- **CSRD/ESRS**: EU mandatory reporting from 2024; requires double materiality (both impact and financial); EFRAG simplified ESRS in July 2025, reducing mandatory data points by 57% [src2]
- **TCFD**: Climate risk framework (governance, strategy, risk management, metrics/targets); recommendations now embedded in IFRS S2, so new adopters should go directly to ISSB [src1]
- **Double materiality**: Reports both how sustainability issues affect the company (financial) AND how the company affects society/environment (impact) — required by CSRD, optional under ISSB [src3]

## Constraints

- The ESG framework landscape is converging rapidly — TCFD has been absorbed into ISSB, SASB is part of IFRS Foundation, and EFRAG revised ESRS in 2025. Advice based on pre-2025 framework structures may be outdated. [src2]
- CSRD scope is complex — applies to large EU companies, listed SMEs, and non-EU companies with EUR 150M+ EU net turnover. Scope determination requires legal analysis, not a simple checklist. [src5]
- Double materiality and financial materiality are fundamentally different assessment methodologies — using the wrong one produces non-compliant disclosures. [src3]
- Data collection is the primary operational bottleneck — most companies lack the internal systems to capture Scope 3 emissions, supply chain labor practices, or biodiversity metrics reliably. [src4]
- Greenwashing risk is high — disclosures must be backed by verifiable data and increasingly require third-party assurance (limited assurance initially, reasonable assurance by 2028 under CSRD). [src2]

## Framework Selection Decision Tree

```
START — User needs ESG reporting guidance
├── Is reporting mandatory or voluntary?
│   ├── Mandatory (EU CSRD scope)
│   │   └── CSRD/ESRS with double materiality assessment
│   ├── Mandatory (ISSB jurisdiction — UK, Canada, etc.)
│   │   └── IFRS S1/S2 with financial materiality
│   └── Voluntary
│       ├── Primary audience?
│       │   ├── Investors → SASB/ISSB (financial materiality)
│       │   ├── Broad stakeholders → GRI (impact materiality)
│       │   └── Both → GRI + SASB combined reporting
├── Climate risk specifically?
│   ├── YES → IFRS S2 (replaces TCFD) ← YOU ARE HERE for climate
│   └── NO → Full ESG frameworks above
└── Company operates in the EU with >250 employees or listed?
    ├── YES → CSRD is likely mandatory — confirm scope
    └── NO → Voluntary framework selection applies
```

## Application Checklist

### Step 1: Determine scope and regulatory obligations
- **Inputs needed**: Company size, jurisdictions of operation, listing status, EU revenue
- **Output**: Mapping of mandatory vs. voluntary reporting obligations
- **Constraint**: CSRD scope is not optional — if the company meets the thresholds, disclosure is a legal requirement [src5]

### Step 2: Conduct materiality assessment
- **Inputs needed**: Stakeholder interviews, peer benchmarking, ESG risk assessment, industry-specific topics
- **Output**: Material topics matrix (double materiality if CSRD; financial materiality if ISSB)
- **Constraint**: Desktop analysis alone does not satisfy requirements — stakeholder engagement is mandatory under both GRI and CSRD [src3]

### Step 3: Select frameworks and establish data infrastructure
- **Inputs needed**: Material topics, reporting obligations, existing data systems
- **Output**: Framework selection, data collection plan, gap analysis for missing metrics
- **Constraint**: Scope 3 emissions and supply chain data are the most common gaps — budget 6-12 months for initial data collection [src4]

### Step 4: Report, assure, and iterate
- **Inputs needed**: Collected data, narrative disclosures, assurance provider selection
- **Output**: Published ESG report with third-party assurance (limited initially)
- **Constraint**: CSRD requires limited assurance from 2024 and reasonable assurance by 2028 — plan assurance readiness early [src2]

## Anti-Patterns

### Wrong: Treating ESG reporting as a marketing exercise
Company publishes a glossy sustainability report with cherry-picked positive metrics and no standardized framework. This invites greenwashing accusations and regulatory scrutiny. [src2]

### Correct: Report against recognized frameworks with verifiable data
Select GRI, ISSB, or CSRD/ESRS as appropriate, disclose both positive and negative performance, and engage third-party assurance. [src3]

### Wrong: Conflating GRI materiality with ISSB materiality
Company uses GRI's impact materiality methodology to satisfy investor-oriented ISSB requirements, producing disclosures that miss financially material topics. [src1]

### Correct: Match materiality methodology to framework
Use impact materiality for GRI/CSRD stakeholder reporting and financial materiality for ISSB/SASB investor reporting. For CSRD, conduct double materiality covering both. [src4]

### Wrong: Starting with Scope 3 emissions
Company attempts to calculate full value-chain emissions before establishing Scope 1 and 2 measurement, resulting in unreliable data and project paralysis. [src4]

### Correct: Build measurement from Scope 1 outward
Establish accurate Scope 1 (direct) and Scope 2 (purchased energy) emissions first, then expand to material Scope 3 categories based on industry-specific guidance. [src5]

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: TCFD is still a separate framework companies should adopt.
  **Reality**: TCFD recommendations have been fully incorporated into IFRS S2 (ISSB Climate Standard). New adopters should implement IFRS S2 directly; existing TCFD reporters can leverage their work as a foundation. [src1]

- **Misconception**: ESG reporting is only for large public companies.
  **Reality**: CSRD extends to large private companies and listed SMEs. Additionally, supply chain pressure means even small companies face ESG data requests from customers and investors. [src5]

- **Misconception**: One framework covers everything.
  **Reality**: Most companies need multiple frameworks — e.g., CSRD for EU compliance plus GRI for stakeholder reporting plus ISSB for investor communication. The frameworks are designed to be interoperable but serve different audiences. [src3]

## Comparison with Similar Concepts

| Concept | Key Difference | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| GRI Standards | Impact materiality — how the company affects the world | Broad stakeholder reporting, voluntary or GRI-aligned mandates |
| ISSB (IFRS S1/S2) | Financial materiality — how ESG affects enterprise value | Investor-focused reporting, capital markets disclosure |
| CSRD/ESRS | Double materiality — both impact and financial | EU mandatory reporting for in-scope companies |
| TCFD | Climate-specific risk disclosure (now in IFRS S2) | Legacy reference — new adopters should use IFRS S2 |
