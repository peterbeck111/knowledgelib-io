# Market Sizing Methodology (TAM/SAM/SOM)

## Purpose

This recipe produces a complete market sizing analysis with TAM, SAM, and SOM calculated via three independent methodologies (top-down, bottom-up, value-theory), cross-validated against each other. The output includes confidence intervals, growth projections, and investor-ready formatting. This is the quantitative foundation for financial models, fundraising decks, and go-to-market strategy.

## Prerequisites

- [ ] **Idea Classification Report** from [Idea Classification Framework](/business/startup-planning/idea-classification-framework/2026) — determines sizing approach
- [ ] **Feasibility check passed** from [Feasibility Quick Check](/business/startup-planning/initial-feasibility-quick-check/2026) — market dimension scored >= 3
- [ ] **Target customer definition** — who exactly the product serves (industry, size, geography, role)
- [ ] **Pricing hypothesis** — approximate price point or range for the product
- [ ] **Web access** for market data research

## Constraints

- TAM is a strategic planning input, not a vanity metric. Inflated TAMs destroy investor credibility. If your TAM is "$500B" for a niche B2B tool, you are doing it wrong. [src1]
- Bottom-up estimates always required. Top-down alone is insufficient because it relies on analyst assumptions that may not apply to your specific segment. [src3]
- SOM must be calculated from actual go-to-market capacity: sales team size, marketing budget, distribution reach, conversion rates. Saying "we'll get 1% of TAM" is meaningless without operational backing. [src6]
- Market size data older than 2 years is unreliable for sectors growing >15% CAGR. Always check report publication dates. [src5]
- Cross-validation is mandatory. If top-down and bottom-up estimates differ by >3x, one methodology has a flawed assumption. Find and fix it. [src2]

## Execution Flow

### Step 1: Define Market Boundaries

**Duration**: 20 minutes
**Tool**: Text editor

Before calculating anything, define what you are measuring:

```json
{
  "market_definition": {
    "product_category": "{What you sell — e.g., 'project management software'}",
    "customer_segment": "{Who buys — e.g., 'software development teams at companies with 50-500 employees'}",
    "geography": "{Where — e.g., 'United States'}",
    "price_range": "{Expected price — e.g., '$20-100/user/month'}",
    "time_horizon": "{Year — e.g., '2026'}",
    "inclusion_criteria": ["{What counts — e.g., 'standalone PM tools'}"],
    "exclusion_criteria": ["{What does not count — e.g., 'PM features in general productivity suites'}"]
  }
}
```

**Verify**: Can you explain the market boundary in one sentence? If not, it is too broad.
**If failed**: Narrow the definition until you can state: "We are sizing the market for [product] sold to [customer] in [geography] at [price range]."

### Step 2: Top-Down TAM Calculation

**Duration**: 30 minutes
**Tool**: Web search + spreadsheet

Start with the broadest credible market number and narrow down:

```
TOP-DOWN FORMULA:
TAM = Industry Total Revenue × Segment Percentage

Steps:
1. Find total industry revenue from a research report
   Search: "{industry} market size 2025 2026 report"
   Sources: Statista, Grand View Research, Allied Market Research, IBISWorld

2. Identify your specific segment's share
   If industry = "project management software" = $8B
   Your segment = "for software dev teams" = ~25% of PM market
   TAM (top-down) = $8B × 0.25 = $2B

3. Apply geographic filter
   If US-only: multiply by US share of global market
   US share of PM software = ~40%
   TAM (top-down, US) = $2B × 0.40 = $800M
```

Record the source, year, and page number for every data point.

**Verify**: TAM source is from a recognized research firm or government agency. Publication year >= 2024.
**If failed**: If no industry report exists, skip to bottom-up and use it as primary methodology.

### Step 3: Bottom-Up TAM Calculation

**Duration**: 45 minutes
**Tool**: Web search + spreadsheet

Build from individual customer counts up:

```
BOTTOM-UP FORMULA:
TAM = Number of Potential Customers × Average Revenue Per Customer

Steps:
1. Count potential customers
   Search: "how many {customer type} in {geography}"
   Example: "how many software companies with 50-500 employees in the US"
   Source: US Census Bureau (SUSB), BLS, LinkedIn data

2. Estimate average revenue per customer
   Annual contract value = price × users per company × 12 months
   Example: $50/user/month × 15 users × 12 = $9,000/year

3. Calculate
   TAM (bottom-up) = 45,000 companies × $9,000/year = $405M
```

The bottom-up TAM will typically be smaller than top-down. This is normal and expected — it means your market definition is specific enough to be actionable. [src1]

**Verify**: Bottom-up TAM is within 2-5x of top-down TAM. If bottom-up is >5x different, check assumptions.
**If failed**: If bottom-up is much smaller, check customer count. If much larger, check if top-down source excluded your segment.

### Step 4: Value-Theory TAM Calculation

**Duration**: 20 minutes
**Tool**: Spreadsheet

Calculate what customers would pay based on value delivered, not existing market prices:

```
VALUE-THEORY FORMULA:
TAM = Potential Customers × Value-Based Price

Steps:
1. Estimate value created by your product
   Example: PM tool saves 5 hours/week per developer
   Developer hourly rate = $75
   Value created = 5 × $75 × 52 = $19,500/year/developer

2. Apply willingness-to-pay ratio (typically 10-30% of value)
   Value-based price = $19,500 × 0.15 = $2,925/developer/year

3. Calculate
   TAM (value-theory) = 45,000 companies × 15 devs × $2,925 = $1.97B
```

This method is most useful for novel products where no existing market data exists. [src2]

**Verify**: Value-based price is 10-30% of quantifiable value created. Higher capture rates are unrealistic for most markets.
**If failed**: If value is hard to quantify, use willingness-to-pay surveys or competitor pricing as proxies.

### Step 5: Calculate SAM and SOM

**Duration**: 20 minutes
**Tool**: Spreadsheet

```
SAM CALCULATION:
SAM = TAM × Segment Accessibility Filters

Filters to apply:
- Geographic reach (where you can actually sell)
- Customer size fit (which customers match your product)
- Technology requirements (customers who can adopt your solution)
- Language/localization (markets you can serve)

Example:
  TAM = $800M (US PM software for dev teams)
  Geographic: US only = 100% → still $800M
  Company size: 50-500 employees = 60% of market → $480M
  Tech stack: Uses modern dev tools = 70% → $336M
  SAM = $336M

SOM CALCULATION:
SOM = SAM × Realistic Capture Rate (Year 1-3)

Capture rate factors:
- Current sales capacity (reps × quota)
- Marketing reach and budget
- Competitive intensity
- Sales cycle length
- Average win rate

Example:
  SAM = $336M
  Year 1: 2 salespeople × $500K quota × 60% attainment = $600K
  Year 2: 5 salespeople × $600K quota × 70% attainment = $2.1M
  Year 3: 10 salespeople + inbound → $5-8M
  SOM (Year 3) = $5-8M (1.5-2.4% of SAM)
```

**Verify**: SOM Year 1 is achievable with current resources. SOM Year 3 is <5% of SAM (for most startups).
**If failed**: If SOM exceeds 5% of SAM in Year 3, check if SAM is underestimated or growth assumptions are unrealistic.

### Step 6: Cross-Validate and Generate Report

**Duration**: 15 minutes

Compare all three TAM methodologies:

```json
{
  "market_sizing_report": {
    "market_definition": "{one-sentence boundary}",
    "tam": {
      "top_down": {"value": "$800M", "source": "{report name}", "year": 2025, "confidence": "medium"},
      "bottom_up": {"value": "$405M", "source": "Census SUSB + pricing model", "year": 2026, "confidence": "high"},
      "value_theory": {"value": "$1.97B", "source": "Value calculation", "year": 2026, "confidence": "low"},
      "selected_tam": "$405M-800M",
      "rationale": "Bottom-up as primary (more specific), top-down as ceiling"
    },
    "sam": {"value": "$336M", "filters_applied": ["company size", "tech stack"], "confidence": "medium-high"},
    "som": {
      "year_1": "$600K",
      "year_2": "$2.1M",
      "year_3": "$5-8M",
      "basis": "Sales capacity model"
    },
    "growth_rate": {"cagr": "15%", "source": "{report}", "confidence": "medium"},
    "cross_validation": {
      "top_down_vs_bottom_up_ratio": 1.98,
      "assessment": "Within acceptable 2-5x range — consistent"
    }
  }
}
```

**Output files**:
- `market_sizing_report.json` — Complete TAM/SAM/SOM with methodology
- `market_sizing_summary.md` — Human-readable summary for pitch decks

| Top-down/bottom-up ratio | Within 5x | Within 3x | Within 2x |

**If below minimum**: Do not proceed until at least top-down and bottom-up are completed. Single-method market sizing is not credible.

## Error Handling

| Error | Likely Cause | Recovery Action |
|-------|-------------|----------------|
| No industry reports found | Market too new or niche | Lead with bottom-up; use adjacent market reports for growth rates |
| Top-down and bottom-up differ >5x | Different market definitions | Align definitions, check if top-down includes segments you excluded |
| TAM seems too small (<$100M) | May be a lifestyle business, not VC-scale | Valid outcome — may need different funding strategy |
| TAM seems too large (>$100B) | Market definition too broad | Narrow customer segment and geographic scope |
| Customer count data unavailable | Niche B2B market | Use LinkedIn Sales Navigator to count companies matching criteria |

## Anti-Patterns

### Wrong: Starting with "the global X market is $500B"
Top-down only, starting from the broadest possible number, is the most common market sizing error. Investors see through it immediately. If your TAM slide says "$500B" for a developer tool, you have lost credibility. [src6]

### Correct: Lead with bottom-up, use top-down as ceiling
Start by counting actual potential customers and multiplying by your price. Then use top-down industry reports to validate the ceiling. Present both numbers to show rigor.

### Wrong: SOM = "1% of TAM"
Claiming 1% of a large market sounds modest but is actually arbitrary. It tells investors nothing about your actual go-to-market plan or growth capacity. [src1]

### Correct: SOM from operational capacity
Calculate SOM from: sales team size x quota x win rate, plus inbound pipeline from marketing budget. This is credible because it is verifiable.

## When This Matters

Use after an idea passes initial feasibility screening and before building financial models or fundraising materials. Market sizing is the quantitative backbone of every investor pitch, and getting it wrong — either too high (not credible) or too low (not interesting) — derails fundraising conversations. Every financial projection traces back to this analysis.
