# Competitive Landscape Mapping

## Purpose

This recipe produces a categorized inventory of all competitive forces in your market — direct competitors, indirect competitors, substitutes, and potential entrants — positioned on a strategic matrix with threat-level assessments. The output feeds directly into positioning strategy, differentiation decisions, and investor competitive slides. A complete landscape map prevents the fatal startup error of building a product that ignores how the market actually works.

## Prerequisites

- [ ] **Market definition** from [Market Sizing Methodology](/business/market-research/market-sizing-methodology/2026) — product category, customer segment, geography
- [ ] **Business model classification** from [Idea Classification Framework](/business/startup-planning/idea-classification-framework/2026) — determines competitor categories
- [ ] **Known competitor list** (even if incomplete — starting with 3-5 known competitors accelerates discovery)
- [ ] **Web access** for competitor research

## Constraints

- A landscape map is a snapshot that decays within 90 days. Schedule quarterly updates, especially in fast-moving tech markets. [src1]
- Zero direct competitors almost always means no proven market demand, not a "blue ocean opportunity." Validate demand before celebrating lack of competition. [src2]
- Competitive intelligence must use public sources only: websites, press releases, job postings, SEC filings, app stores, review sites. Do not misrepresent identity to access competitor materials. [src3]
- Landscape maps are only as good as their discovery process. The competitors you miss are more dangerous than the ones you find. Budget 60% of time on discovery, 40% on analysis. [src4]
- Include "do nothing" as a competitor. For many B2B products, the biggest competitor is the customer's existing manual process or spreadsheet, not another software vendor. [src1]

## Execution Flow

### Step 1: Competitor Discovery

**Duration**: 60-90 minutes
**Tool**: Web search + Crunchbase

Use 6 discovery methods to build a comprehensive competitor list:

```
METHOD 1: Direct Search
Search: "{your product category} software/tool/platform"
Search: "best {category} for {customer type} 2025 2026"
Search: "alternatives to {known competitor}"
→ Captures: Direct competitors

METHOD 2: Review Site Mining
Sites: G2.com, Capterra, TrustRadius, Product Hunt
Search: Browse category listings, "compare" pages
→ Captures: Direct competitors, emerging players

METHOD 3: Crunchbase/LinkedIn Category Browse
Search: Filter by industry, funding stage, employee count
→ Captures: Funded competitors, company size estimates

METHOD 4: Customer Perspective
Search: "{customer problem} solution/how to"
Search: Reddit, Quora, industry forums for recommendations
→ Captures: Indirect competitors, DIY alternatives, substitutes

METHOD 5: Investor Perspective
Search: "venture capital {industry} portfolio" or "{VC firm} portfolio"
→ Captures: Well-funded competitors, potential entrants

METHOD 6: Adjacent Market Scan
Search: Companies in related categories that could expand into your market
→ Captures: Potential entrants, platform threats
```

For each competitor found, record:
```json
{
  "name": "",
  "url": "",
  "category": "direct|indirect|substitute|potential_entrant|do_nothing",
  "discovery_method": "",
  "one_line_description": ""
}
```

**Verify**: Minimum 15 entries found across at least 3 categories
**If failed**: If fewer than 15, expand search terms. Use competitor's "alternatives" pages to find more.

### Step 2: Categorize by Competitive Relationship

**Duration**: 20 minutes
**Tool**: Spreadsheet

Classify every discovered competitor into one of 5 categories:

```
CATEGORY DEFINITIONS:

1. DIRECT COMPETITORS (same product, same customer)
   - Offer essentially the same product/service
   - Target the same customer segment
   - Customer would choose between you or them
   Threat Level: HIGH

2. INDIRECT COMPETITORS (different product, same need)
   - Solve the same customer problem differently
   - May target overlapping customer segments
   - Customer might use instead of your solution
   Threat Level: MEDIUM-HIGH

3. SUBSTITUTES (different approach entirely)
   - Customer achieves the same outcome via a completely different method
   - Often includes manual processes, spreadsheets, or hiring
   - Example: Hiring a bookkeeper vs. using QuickBooks
   Threat Level: MEDIUM

4. POTENTIAL ENTRANTS (not competing yet, but could)
   - Adjacent market players who could expand
   - Large platforms that could add your feature
   - Well-funded startups in related spaces
   Threat Level: VARIABLE (low now, potentially high)

5. "DO NOTHING" (status quo)
   - Customer's current workaround or manual process
   - Often the biggest competitor for new categories
   Threat Level: HIGH for new categories, LOW for established ones
```

**Verify**: Each competitor assigned exactly one category
**If failed**: If a competitor fits multiple categories, assign the one representing the greatest competitive threat

### Step 3: Gather Positioning Data

**Duration**: 60-90 minutes
**Tool**: Web search + SimilarWeb + Crunchbase

For the top 15-20 competitors (all direct + top indirect), gather:

| Data Point | Source | Priority |
|------------|--------|----------|
| Pricing (tiers, price points) | Website pricing page | Critical |
| Target customer (from messaging) | Website homepage, about page | Critical |
| Key features (top 5) | Features page, G2 profile | Critical |
| Founded year | Crunchbase, LinkedIn | High |
| Employee count | LinkedIn, Crunchbase | High |
| Funding total | Crunchbase | High |
| Website traffic (monthly) | SimilarWeb free | Medium |
| Customer count (if public) | Website, press releases | Medium |
| Key integrations | Integrations page | Medium |
| Recent news/launches | Google News search | Medium |

**Verify**: Pricing, target customer, and key features collected for all direct competitors
**If failed**: If pricing is hidden ("Contact us"), note as "enterprise/custom pricing" — this itself is useful competitive intelligence.

### Step 4: Build the Positioning Matrix

**Duration**: 30 minutes
**Tool**: Spreadsheet

Plot competitors on a 2D matrix using the two dimensions most relevant to your market:

Common dimension pairs:
- **Price vs. Feature Breadth** (most common for SaaS)
- **Enterprise vs. SMB Focus** (for B2B tools)
- **Ease of Use vs. Customizability** (for developer tools)
- **Speed to Value vs. Depth of Solution** (for consulting/services)

```json
{
  "positioning_matrix": {
    "x_axis": {"label": "Price", "low": "Free / low-cost", "high": "Enterprise premium"},
    "y_axis": {"label": "Feature breadth", "low": "Point solution", "high": "All-in-one platform"},
    "competitors": [
      {"name": "Competitor A", "x": 0.8, "y": 0.9, "category": "direct", "bubble_size": "large"},
      {"name": "Competitor B", "x": 0.3, "y": 0.4, "category": "direct", "bubble_size": "medium"},
      {"name": "Your Position", "x": 0.4, "y": 0.6, "category": "self", "bubble_size": "small"}
    ],
    "white_space": "Lower-right quadrant (premium point solution) underserved"
  }
}
```

Bubble size represents estimated market share or funding level.

**Verify**: All direct competitors and top 5 indirect plotted. At least one "white space" identified.
**If failed**: If no white space exists, the market is mature. Differentiation must come from execution, not positioning.

### Step 5: Assess Threat Levels and Generate Report

**Duration**: 30 minutes
**Tool**: Spreadsheet

Score each competitor on a 1-5 threat scale across 4 dimensions:

| Dimension | Weight | Description |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Market overlap | 0.35 | How much does their target market overlap with yours? |
| Resource advantage | 0.25 | Funding, team size, technology advantages |
| Momentum | 0.25 | Growth rate, recent launches, hiring pace |
| Switching cost from them | 0.15 | How hard is it for their customers to switch to you? |

```json
{
  "competitive_landscape_report": {
    "market_definition": "{market boundary}",
    "total_competitors_found": 28,
    "by_category": {
      "direct": 8,
      "indirect": 7,
      "substitutes": 5,
      "potential_entrants": 6,
      "do_nothing": 2
    },
    "top_threats": [
      {"name": "Competitor A", "threat_score": 4.2, "category": "direct", "key_risk": "Well-funded, same target customer"},
      {"name": "Competitor B", "threat_score": 3.8, "category": "direct", "key_risk": "Growing fast, strong brand"}
    ],
    "white_space_opportunities": ["{description of underserved position}"],
    "positioning_recommendation": "{Where to position and why}",
    "monitoring_priorities": ["Watch for: {competitor} expanding into {segment}"]
  }
}
```

**Output files**:
- `competitive_landscape_report.json` — Full categorized competitor inventory with threat scores
- `positioning_matrix.json` — 2D positioning data for visualization
- `competitive_landscape_summary.md` — Executive summary for pitch decks

## Quality Benchmarks

| Quality Metric | Minimum Acceptable | Good | Excellent |
|---------------|-------------------|------|-----------|
| Competitors discovered | >= 15 | >= 25 | >= 40 |
| Categories represented | >= 3 | All 5 | All 5 with examples |
| Data completeness (top 10) | Pricing + features | + funding + traffic | + customer count + growth |
| Discovery methods used | >= 3 | >= 5 | All 6 |

**If below minimum**: Expand search terms. Use competitor "alternatives" pages. Check G2 and Capterra category listings.

## Error Handling

| Error | Likely Cause | Recovery Action |
|-------|-------------|----------------|
| Zero direct competitors found | Market may not exist yet | Check for substitute solutions and manual workarounds. Validate demand separately. |
| 50+ direct competitors found | Market definition too broad | Narrow by customer segment, geography, or use case |
| Cannot find pricing for key competitors | Enterprise sales model | Note "custom pricing" and estimate from employee count and customer base |
| Competitor recently acquired or shut down | Market consolidation | Note in report; check acquirer's competitive position |
| AI tool lists competitors that don't exist | LLM hallucination | Verify every competitor with a live URL check |

## Anti-Patterns

### Wrong: Listing only direct competitors
Ignoring indirect competitors, substitutes, and potential entrants creates blind spots. Uber's real competition was not just other rideshare apps — it was public transit, bike sharing, and car ownership. [src1]

### Correct: Map all 5 competitive categories
Include direct, indirect, substitutes, potential entrants, and "do nothing." The most dangerous competitors are often the ones you never considered because they approach the problem differently.

### Wrong: Static one-time analysis
Building a competitive map once and never updating it is like navigating with a year-old GPS. Markets move. Competitors launch features. Funding rounds shift power dynamics. [src2]

### Correct: Schedule quarterly updates with monitoring alerts
Set Google Alerts for all top-10 competitors. Use Crunchbase alerts for funding rounds. Review the landscape map every 90 days.

## When This Matters

Use after market sizing is complete and before developing go-to-market strategy or positioning. The landscape map determines differentiation strategy, pricing strategy, and which competitors to watch most closely. Essential for investor pitches — investors will ask about competitors, and "we have no competition" is the wrong answer.
