# Sales-Marketing Alignment Diagnostic

## Purpose

This assessment evaluates the alignment maturity between sales and marketing teams across five dimensions — shared definitions, lead handoff process, SLA compliance, attribution and measurement, and strategic planning. Only 8% of companies report strong alignment, yet aligned organizations achieve 32% higher year-over-year revenue growth and 38% higher win rates. This diagnostic identifies specific misalignment gaps and routes to targeted improvement actions. [src1]

## Constraints
<!-- Agents: read before running this assessment with a user. -->

- Requires input from both sales and marketing leaders — single-side assessments produce biased results skewed by 1.0-1.5 points
- Most relevant for B2B companies with dedicated sales and marketing teams (10+ people combined)
- Should be facilitated by RevOps, an executive sponsor, or an external party to ensure neutrality
- Diagnoses alignment between functions, not individual team performance — pair with function-specific assessments for deeper diagnosis
- Re-run quarterly; alignment degrades with personnel changes, strategy shifts, and market pivots

## Assessment Dimensions

<!-- Each dimension is scored independently. The structured format lets agents
     walk through this conversationally with a user, one dimension at a time. -->

### Dimension 1: Shared Definitions and Taxonomy

**What this measures**: Whether sales and marketing agree on fundamental terms — lead stages, qualification criteria, ICP, and target account definitions.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | No shared definitions; sales and marketing use different terms for the same concepts; 49% of organizations report differing lead definitions | Ask sales and marketing separately "what is a qualified lead?" — answers diverge significantly |
| 2 | Emerging | Some informal agreements but not documented; ICP defined by marketing but not validated by sales | Marketing has buyer personas; sales uses different criteria to prioritize accounts; no written glossary |
| 3 | Defined | Documented shared glossary with MQL, SQL, SAL definitions; ICP jointly developed; lead scoring criteria agreed upon | Written SLA document includes shared definitions; both teams can articulate the same qualification criteria |
| 4 | Managed | Definitions reviewed and recalibrated quarterly based on conversion data; buying group/committee definitions incorporated; lifecycle stages mapped end-to-end | Quarterly alignment meeting reviews whether definitions still match actual buyer behavior; data drives adjustments |
| 5 | Optimized | Dynamic, data-driven definitions that auto-adjust based on conversion signals; account-level (not just lead-level) qualification; unified revenue language across all GTM functions | Definitions evolve continuously based on machine learning signals; sales, marketing, and CS share identical taxonomy |

**Red flags**: Marketing celebrates MQL volume while sales complains about lead quality; sales cannot articulate what makes a lead "qualified" from marketing; no written definition exists anywhere. [src2]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "If I asked your top sales rep and your demand gen lead to separately define a 'qualified lead,' would they give me the same answer?"

### Dimension 2: Lead Handoff Process

**What this measures**: The mechanics and effectiveness of how leads transfer from marketing to sales — speed, quality, information completeness, and follow-up compliance.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | No formal handoff process; leads sent via email or spreadsheet; follow-up timing varies from hours to weeks | Leads pile up in a shared inbox; no assignment rules; some leads never contacted |
| 2 | Emerging | Automated lead routing exists but without enrichment or context; speed-to-lead inconsistent; SAL rate below 50% | CRM assigns leads but sales receives only name/email; no lead intelligence or engagement history shared |
| 3 | Defined | Structured handoff with lead score, engagement history, and recommended talk track; speed-to-lead SLA defined (typically < 4 hours); SAL rate 60-75% | Handoff includes last 5 touchpoints, content consumed, and score; sales acknowledges receipt within SLA |
| 4 | Managed | Bi-directional feedback loop — sales provides lead quality feedback within 48 hours; rejected leads returned to marketing with reason codes; SAL rate 75-85% | Lead feedback loop actively managed; marketing adjusts scoring based on sales feedback; recycle process defined |
| 5 | Optimized | Real-time handoff with full buyer context including intent signals and buying committee mapping; SAL rate > 85%; behavioral triggers initiate sales engagement at optimal moment | Handoff timing driven by buying signals not arbitrary score thresholds; multi-threaded with buying committee context |

**Red flags**: Sales says "marketing leads are garbage" while marketing says "sales doesn't follow up"; no data on speed-to-lead; leads older than 48 hours in the handoff queue. [src4]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "What happens in the first 60 minutes after marketing passes a lead to sales — can you describe the exact steps?"

### Dimension 3: SLA Compliance and Accountability

**What this measures**: Whether formal service level agreements exist between sales and marketing, and whether they are measured, reported, and enforced.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | No SLA exists; commitments are verbal and informal; no accountability mechanism | "We should follow up faster" discussions happen but no targets are set or tracked |
| 2 | Emerging | Informal agreements on lead volume and follow-up timing but not documented or measured; compliance unknown | Marketing commits to "more leads" and sales commits to "faster follow-up" but neither is quantified |
| 3 | Defined | Written SLA specifying marketing lead volume/quality commitments and sales follow-up timing/attempt commitments; monthly compliance reporting | SLA document exists; monthly report shows MQL delivery vs. target and sales follow-up compliance rate |
| 4 | Managed | SLA integrated into CRM workflows with automated tracking; real-time compliance dashboards; SLA breach alerts; quarterly SLA reviews with adjustments | Dashboard shows live SLA performance; breaches trigger automated escalation; SLA terms updated based on data |
| 5 | Optimized | SLA evolved into shared revenue targets with joint accountability; leading indicators tracked alongside lagging; SLA compliance tied to compensation | Both teams measured on pipeline contribution and revenue; SLA is bidirectional with consequences for non-compliance |

**Red flags**: SLA exists on paper but nobody knows the numbers; marketing measures itself on MQLs while sales measures on revenue with no connection between them; no escalation process for SLA breaches. [src6]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "Show me your SLA dashboard — what were marketing and sales compliance rates last month?"

### Dimension 4: Attribution and Measurement

**What this measures**: The maturity of how marketing and sales jointly measure performance, attribute revenue, and use data to optimize the funnel.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | No attribution model; marketing measures campaigns in isolation; sales measures revenue without connecting to source | Marketing reports click-through rates; sales reports bookings; nobody connects the two |
| 2 | Emerging | First-touch or last-touch attribution implemented but disputed; marketing and sales present different numbers in leadership meetings | Marketing claims credit for leads; sales claims credit for relationships; conflicting reports create distrust |
| 3 | Defined | Multi-touch attribution model implemented and accepted by both teams; shared funnel metrics (MQL → SQL → opportunity → close) tracked end-to-end | Single source of truth for funnel metrics; both teams reference the same conversion rates and pipeline attribution |
| 4 | Managed | Revenue attribution model with influence tracking; marketing-influenced pipeline and revenue reported alongside marketing-sourced; joint pipeline reviews | Marketing reports both sourced and influenced revenue; joint weekly pipeline review analyzes conversion bottlenecks |
| 5 | Optimized | Probabilistic attribution with machine learning; account-level attribution across buying committees; shared revenue model eliminates credit disputes | Attribution is a tool for optimization, not credit allocation; both teams optimize the same revenue model |

**Red flags**: Marketing and sales present different pipeline numbers to the board; attribution model changes every quarter; neither team trusts the other's data. [src3]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "If I asked marketing 'how much revenue did you influence last quarter' and asked sales the same question, would the numbers match?"

### Dimension 5: Strategic Planning and Communication

**What this measures**: Whether sales and marketing plan together, share objectives, and communicate regularly enough to stay aligned on priorities.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | No joint planning; marketing plans campaigns without sales input; sales sets targets without marketing visibility | Marketing launches campaigns sales didn't know about; sales pursues segments marketing isn't targeting |
| 2 | Emerging | Annual planning includes some coordination but execution diverges quickly; ad hoc meetings when problems arise | Joint annual kickoff but by Q2, teams are operating independently; meetings happen reactively after conflicts |
| 3 | Defined | Quarterly joint planning sessions; shared target account list; monthly alignment meetings with structured agenda; joint campaign calendar | Both teams work from the same target account list; monthly meeting reviews pipeline, campaigns, and priorities |
| 4 | Managed | Integrated GTM planning with shared OKRs; weekly cross-functional standup; joint account strategy for top opportunities; shared Slack/Teams channels active daily | Teams share OKRs; weekly standup surfaces blockers; cross-functional war rooms for strategic deals |
| 5 | Optimized | Unified revenue team operating model (RevOps-led); real-time signal sharing; joint account-based strategies; shared technology stack with unified dashboards | Functionally distinct but operationally unified; single revenue plan owned collectively; strategy adjustments made jointly |

**Red flags**: Sales learns about new marketing campaigns from customers; marketing learns about product changes from the sales deck; leadership meetings are finger-pointing sessions. [src5]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "When was the last time sales and marketing leaders sat in a room together to plan — and what came out of it?"

## Scoring & Interpretation

### Overall Score Calculation

Equal weighting across all five dimensions — alignment is holistic and weakness in any single area cascades across the entire revenue process.

```
Overall Score = (Shared Definitions + Lead Handoff + SLA Compliance + Attribution + Strategic Planning) / 5
```

### Score Interpretation

| Overall Score | Maturity Level | Interpretation | Recommended Next Step |
|---------------|---------------|----------------|----------------------|
| 1.0 - 1.9 | Critical | Sales and marketing operate as separate organizations; misalignment costs 10-15% of potential revenue annually | Start with shared definitions and basic SLA — foundational alignment before process optimization |
| 2.0 - 2.9 | Developing | Some alignment exists but is fragile and informal; significant revenue leakage from handoff gaps and attribution disputes | Formalize SLA, implement structured handoff process, and establish shared funnel metrics |
| 3.0 - 3.9 | Competent | Solid alignment foundation with documented processes and measured performance; revenue teams working from shared playbook | Optimize lead handoff with bi-directional feedback; implement multi-touch attribution; joint pipeline reviews |
| 4.0 - 4.5 | Advanced | Strong alignment with shared accountability and integrated planning; revenue predictability improving significantly | Move toward unified revenue operations model; implement account-based alignment; shared compensation elements |
| 4.6 - 5.0 | Best-in-class | Unified revenue team with shared goals, real-time collaboration, and data-driven optimization; competitive advantage in GTM execution | Maintain through continuous calibration; invest in AI-driven signal sharing and predictive alignment |

## Common Pitfalls in Assessment

- **Single-side assessment bias**: When only one team participates, scores inflate by 1.0-1.5 points. Both sales and marketing must provide input independently, then discrepancies become the most valuable diagnostic data. [src1]
- **Process-existence fallacy**: Having an SLA document or attribution model does not mean it is used or trusted. Ask "when was this last updated?" and "who looks at this weekly?" — stale artifacts score 2 regardless of sophistication.
- **Attribution credit wars masking the real problem**: Teams that fight over attribution credit often have deeper misalignment in definitions and strategy. Fix definitions and shared planning first — attribution fights resolve naturally when teams share goals.
- **Confusing meetings with alignment**: Regular meetings without structured agendas, shared data, and action items are activity, not alignment. Score Strategic Planning based on outcomes (shared plans, coordinated actions) not inputs (meeting frequency).
