# Channel Partner Sales Readiness Assessment

## Purpose

This assessment evaluates whether an organization is ready to launch or expand channel sales — measuring product-market fit for indirect sales, operational readiness, economic viability, and enablement infrastructure. It helps revenue leaders make an informed go/no-go decision on channel investment and identifies prerequisite gaps to address before partner recruitment begins. The output is a readiness score that maps to specific actions: proceed, proceed with caveats, or defer. [src1]

## Constraints
<!-- Agents: read before running this assessment with a user. -->

- Requires established direct sales motion with documented sales process, win rates, and cycle times — channel cannot fix a broken product-market fit
- Most relevant for B2B SaaS and technology companies; physical product distribution follows different channel economics
- Should be run by revenue leadership (CRO, VP Sales, VP Partnerships) with finance input for margin analysis
- Evaluates readiness to launch or scale channel — not the performance of an existing partner program
- Re-run when considering significant partner program changes, new market entry, or product line expansion

## Assessment Dimensions

<!-- Each dimension is scored independently. The structured format lets agents
     walk through this conversationally with a user, one dimension at a time. -->

### Dimension 1: Product-Channel Fit

**What this measures**: Whether the product and market characteristics favor indirect sales — complexity, price point, buyer behavior, and competitive landscape.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | Product requires heavy customization or founder-led sales; no standardized sales process exists | Every deal is bespoke; no two implementations look alike; sales cycle requires deep technical discovery |
| 2 | Emerging | Product is somewhat standardized but still requires significant hand-holding; ACV under $10K makes partner economics marginal | Some repeatable patterns but partners would struggle to sell or implement without direct support |
| 3 | Defined | Product has clear use cases, documented sales process, and ACV of $15K-75K that supports partner margins; competitive landscape has active channel players | Partners could learn to sell and deliver with structured enablement; market has existing partner buying patterns |
| 4 | Managed | Product has strong product-led adoption signals that partners can capitalize on; clear integration ecosystem; ACV $25K-200K with upsell paths | Product is "channel-friendly" — partners see attachment revenue opportunity; buyers expect to purchase through partners |
| 5 | Optimized | Platform play with robust API/integration ecosystem; partners can build differentiated solutions on top; market dominated by channel sales | Buyers prefer to purchase through trusted partners; product is a platform that enables partner IP and services revenue |

**Red flags**: Product has < 70% retention rate (partners won't invest in a leaky bucket); average deal requires > 3 demos with engineering present; no competitive products sold through channel in the market. [src2]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "Could a well-trained partner rep give a product demo and handle the first 80% of buyer questions without calling your team?"

### Dimension 2: Economic Viability

**What this measures**: Whether the financial model supports profitable channel operations — margins, partner economics, and CAC comparison.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | No channel margin analysis done; direct sales CAC not established; no understanding of partner economics | Cannot answer "what margin could you offer partners?" or "what's your blended CAC?" |
| 2 | Emerging | Basic margin analysis shows channel could work theoretically but margins are tight; gross margin < 65% limits partner discount room | Margins calculated on paper but 20% partner discount would eliminate profitability at current ACV |
| 3 | Defined | Gross margin > 70% supports 20-30% partner discounts; channel CAC modeled at < 70% of direct CAC; partner economics documented | Partner business case shows positive ROI for a partner doing 5+ deals/year; tier structure drafted |
| 4 | Managed | Multi-tier margin structure tested and validated; deal registration provides 5-10% additional margin protection; MDF budget allocated at 3-5% of partner revenue | Partner economics validated with pilot cohort; partners achieving target margins; clear path from Silver to Gold tier |
| 5 | Optimized | Channel CAC is < 50% of direct CAC; partner lifetime value tracked; channel profitability measured by partner tier and segment; growth accelerators add 3-8% for top performers | Channel is demonstrably more profitable than direct at scale; partner unit economics drive program investment decisions |

**Red flags**: Gross margin below 60%; direct sales team views partners as margin-eroding competitors; no deal registration process to protect partner investments. [src3]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "If you gave a partner 25% off your list price, would the deal still be profitable for you — and would 25% be enough for the partner to invest their sales effort?"

### Dimension 3: Operational Readiness

**What this measures**: The internal infrastructure, processes, and team capacity to recruit, onboard, enable, and manage channel partners.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | No dedicated channel resources; partner inquiries handled ad hoc by direct sales team | Partners are treated as leads in the direct sales pipeline; no partner portal, agreement, or onboarding process |
| 2 | Emerging | One person manages partnerships part-time; basic partner agreement exists; onboarding is informal | Partner agreement is a modified customer contract; onboarding is "here's our website and price list" |
| 3 | Defined | Dedicated channel manager hired; structured partner portal with deal registration, pricing, and training materials; partner agreement covers terms, territories, and conflict resolution | Partner portal operational; deal registration SLA defined (24-48 hour approval); channel conflict rules documented |
| 4 | Managed | Channel team of 2-5 with regional or vertical specialization; PRM system deployed; joint business planning with top partners; quarterly business reviews | PRM tracks partner pipeline and activity; top 20% of partners have joint business plans; MBR/QBR cadence established |
| 5 | Optimized | Scaled channel organization with partner success managers; automated partner lifecycle management; partner advisory council; ecosystem orchestration capabilities | Partner experience is productized; self-service onboarding < 2 weeks to first deal; partner NPS tracked and actioned |

**Red flags**: Direct sales team incentivized to compete with partners rather than collaborate; no channel conflict resolution process; partner onboarding takes > 90 days. [src1]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "If a partner brought you a qualified deal tomorrow, what would happen — do you have a process, or would people scramble to figure out what to do?"

### Dimension 4: Enablement Infrastructure

**What this measures**: The quality and completeness of training, content, and tools provided to partners to sell and deliver effectively.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | No partner-specific sales or technical training; partners use whatever direct sales materials they can find | Partners rely on public website content and whatever they pick up from joint calls; no certification program |
| 2 | Emerging | Basic product training available (usually recorded webinars); partner sales deck exists but is a copy of the direct sales deck | Training exists but is not partner-contextualized; no role-based learning paths or competency validation |
| 3 | Defined | Partner-specific sales playbook with ICP, messaging, objection handling, and competitive positioning; technical certification program; co-sellable demo environment | Partners complete certification before selling; partner-specific battle cards and case studies available |
| 4 | Managed | Role-based enablement paths (sales, technical, executive); regular enablement webinars and deal clinics; co-marketing content library; partner sandbox for POCs | Partners report high confidence in selling; certification completion > 70% of active partners; deal clinic attendance strong |
| 5 | Optimized | Continuous enablement with just-in-time content delivery; AI-assisted partner selling tools; partner community for peer learning; enablement effectiveness measured and optimized | Partner-sourced win rates within 10% of direct; time-to-first-deal after onboarding < 90 days; enablement NPS > 50 |

**Red flags**: Partners consistently ask for direct SE support on every deal; partner certification completion rate < 30%; no competitive intelligence shared with partners. [src4]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "How long does it take from partner signing to their first independently closed deal — and do you track that number?"

### Dimension 5: Market Timing and Competitive Positioning

**What this measures**: Whether market conditions, competitive dynamics, and buyer preferences favor a channel strategy at this time.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | No competitive channel intelligence; unclear whether target buyers purchase through partners | Cannot identify which competitors sell through channel or whether target buyers prefer direct or indirect purchasing |
| 2 | Emerging | Some awareness of competitive channel presence but no systematic analysis; market may be too early for established partner ecosystem | Competitors have partners but unclear whether their channel is material (>10% of revenue) |
| 3 | Defined | Competitive channel landscape mapped; target market segments identified where buyers prefer partner-assisted purchasing; timing analysis complete | Clear evidence that 30%+ of target market buys through partners; specific partner types identified (VARs, MSPs, SIs, ISVs) |
| 4 | Managed | Channel strategy differentiated from competitors; unique partner value proposition documented; market entry timing validated with early partner interest | Partners express interest because your offering fills a gap in their portfolio; clear differentiation from competitive channel programs |
| 5 | Optimized | Market-leading channel position with strong partner brand preference; ecosystem effects driving organic partner recruitment; platform status attracting partner-built solutions | Partners actively seek to join your program; ecosystem flywheel generating deal flow without heavy recruitment investment |

**Red flags**: Market is consolidating around 1-2 vendors with locked-in partner ecosystems; potential partners show no interest or enthusiasm; target market segment is too small for partners to prioritize. [src5]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "Have any partners or resellers proactively asked to sell your product — and if so, how many in the last 12 months?"

## Scoring & Interpretation

### Overall Score Calculation

Weighted scoring — Product-Channel Fit and Economic Viability are prerequisites that gate the decision; operational and enablement gaps can be built.

```
Overall Score = (Product-Channel Fit x 1.5 + Economic Viability x 1.5 + Operational Readiness x 1.0 + Enablement Infrastructure x 1.0 + Market Timing x 1.0) / 6
```

### Score Interpretation

| Overall Score | Maturity Level | Interpretation | Recommended Next Step |
|---------------|---------------|----------------|----------------------|
| 1.0 - 1.9 | Critical | Not ready for channel — fundamental prerequisites missing; premature channel launch will burn partner goodwill and internal resources | Focus on direct sales fundamentals; revisit channel readiness in 6-12 months |
| 2.0 - 2.9 | Developing | Early signals of channel potential but significant gaps; some prerequisites met but operational infrastructure insufficient | Address top 2-3 gaps identified; consider a structured pilot with 3-5 design partners before scaling |
| 3.0 - 3.9 | Competent | Ready for structured channel launch; product-market and economics validated; operational gaps are buildable | Launch partner program with initial cohort of 10-20 partners; invest in enablement before scaling recruitment |
| 4.0 - 4.5 | Advanced | Strong channel readiness across dimensions; ready to scale partner recruitment and investment | Scale program with tiered structure; invest in PRM and partner marketing; target 20-30% of revenue through channel within 18 months |
| 4.6 - 5.0 | Best-in-class | Channel-ready organization with platform economics; partner ecosystem can become a strategic moat | Pursue ecosystem-led growth strategy; invest in partner-built solutions and marketplace |

## Common Pitfalls in Assessment

- **Premature channel investment**: Launching a partner program before direct sales motion is repeatable is the most common and expensive channel mistake. If direct win rate is below 20% or sales cycle is undefined, channel will not fix it — it will amplify the dysfunction. [src2]
- **Margin optimism**: Teams often model channel economics with aspirational ACV and ideal partner volume. Model with median ACV and assume only 20% of recruited partners will be "active" (generating 2+ deals/year).
- **Confusing partner interest with partner commitment**: Partners expressing interest at conferences or in emails is not validation. Track partners who complete certification and register their first deal within 90 days as the real signal.
- **Underestimating channel conflict**: If direct reps and partners compete for the same accounts without clear rules, both channels suffer. Address conflict resolution before launching — not after the first blowup.
