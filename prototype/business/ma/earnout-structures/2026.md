# Earnout Structures in M&A

## Definition

An earnout is a contractual provision in an M&A transaction where a portion of the purchase price is contingent on the target company achieving specified financial or operational milestones post-close. Earnouts bridge valuation gaps between buyer and seller, incentivize seller retention, and allocate risk for uncertain future performance. In 2024, earnouts appeared in 22% of all M&A deals, with the median earnout size at 31% of closing payments outside life sciences. [src4]

## Key Properties

- **Prevalence**: 22% of M&A deals in 2024 include earnout provisions [src4]
- **Median earnout size**: 31% of closing payment value (non-life-sciences); higher in biotech/pharma [src4]
- **Most common metric**: Revenue (62% of earnouts) vs. EBITDA/earnings (22%) [src4]
- **Typical duration**: 1-3 years post-close; life sciences may extend to 5+ years for milestone-based earnouts
- **Realization rate**: Earnouts achieve approximately 21 cents on the dollar on average; 28% are contested [src4]

## Constraints

     These are hard boundaries on when and how it applies. -->

- Ambiguous metric definitions (especially EBITDA) are the leading cause of earnout disputes — use accounting policy schedules attached to the purchase agreement [src2]
- Buyer operational control post-close creates inherent conflicts — the buyer can starve the target of resources, undermining earnout targets [src3]
- Revenue-based earnouts incentivize top-line growth but may sacrifice profitability if not paired with margin floors [src4]
- ASC 805 (IFRS 3) requires fair value measurement at close, with subsequent revaluations hitting P&L — this creates earnings volatility for buyers [src5]
- Earnout periods beyond 3 years significantly increase litigation risk and seller disengagement [src1]

## Application Checklist

### Step 1: Define earnout metrics precisely
- **Inputs needed**: Proposed metric (revenue, EBITDA, milestones), target company's historical financials, buyer's post-close operating plan
- **Output**: Metric definition schedule with explicit accounting policy elections, adjustments, and exclusions
- **Constraint**: Every metric must reference a specific accounting standard (GAAP/IFRS) and list items included/excluded — "EBITDA" without definition is litigable [src2]

### Step 2: Set targets and payment structure
- **Inputs needed**: Financial projections (base, upside, downside), comparable earnout terms from recent deals
- **Output**: Tiered payment schedule (threshold, target, stretch) with linear or step-function interpolation
- **Constraint**: Earnout cap should not exceed 40-50% of total deal value; above this, the structure creates excessive risk concentration [src3]

### Step 3: Negotiate operational covenants
- **Inputs needed**: Buyer's integration plan, seller's concerns about resource allocation
- **Output**: Covenant package covering minimum investment, headcount commitments, and business conduct standards
- **Constraint**: Buyer must commit to operating the target "in the ordinary course consistent with past practice" — without this, earnout targets can be manipulated [src1]

### Step 4: Establish dispute resolution mechanism
- **Inputs needed**: Preferred dispute forum, independent accountant process, timeline requirements
- **Output**: Dispute resolution clause specifying notice period, independent accountant review, and final arbitration
- **Constraint**: Must include a binding mechanism (not just negotiation) — 28% of earnouts are contested, so dispute resolution must be predetermined [src4]

## Anti-Patterns

### Wrong: Using "EBITDA" without a detailed definition schedule
Parties agree to "EBITDA as calculated consistent with GAAP" without specifying treatment of restructuring charges, management fees, intercompany allocations, or one-time items. This produces disputes in nearly every contested earnout. [src2]

### Correct: Attach an accounting policy schedule to the purchase agreement
Define EBITDA with a line-by-line schedule specifying every adjustment, referencing the target's historical accounting practices and explicitly listing items that are included vs. excluded. [src3]

### Wrong: Giving the buyer full operational discretion during the earnout period
Buyers who can redirect resources, change pricing, or integrate the target's operations freely can inadvertently (or deliberately) undermine earnout metrics. [src1]

### Correct: Include operational covenants protecting earnout achievability
Negotiate minimum investment thresholds, headcount maintenance, pricing authority limits, and "ordinary course" operating requirements to prevent buyer manipulation. [src3]

### Wrong: Using only financial metrics when the value driver is a regulatory approval
For biotech or tech deals where value depends on FDA approval or patent issuance, revenue or EBITDA earnouts are meaningless during the pre-revenue phase. [src4]

### Correct: Use binary milestone payments for regulatory or technical targets
Structure earnout as fixed payments upon achievement of specific, objectively verifiable milestones (e.g., FDA Phase 3 approval, patent grant). [src4]

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: Earnouts primarily benefit the seller by allowing them to capture upside.
  **Reality**: Earnouts achieve only ~21 cents on the dollar on average. They primarily benefit the buyer by deferring risk and reducing upfront capital outlay. [src4]

- **Misconception**: Revenue-based earnouts are simple and dispute-free.
  **Reality**: While easier to measure than EBITDA, revenue earnouts still generate disputes around revenue recognition timing, customer allocation in multi-entity deals, and product bundling effects. [src2]

- **Misconception**: The earnout period should be as long as possible to capture full value.
  **Reality**: Periods beyond 3 years dramatically increase dispute risk, seller disengagement, and accounting complexity. Shorter, more targeted earnouts outperform. [src1]

## Comparison with Similar Concepts

| Concept | Key Difference | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| Earnout | Contingent payment based on post-close performance metrics | When buyer and seller disagree on future value |
| Escrow/holdback | Fixed amount withheld for indemnification claims | When protecting against known risks or reps & warranties breaches |
| Contingent Value Rights (CVRs) | Tradeable securities tied to milestones | When milestone is binary and public market liquidity desired |
| Seller note | Deferred fixed payment regardless of performance | When buyer needs to manage cash flow, not allocate risk |
