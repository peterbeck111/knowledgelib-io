# Business Insurance Decision Framework

## Summary

This framework helps US business owners determine which insurance policies they need, appropriate coverage amounts, and how to optimize deductibles based on their industry, size, and risk profile. Workers' compensation is the only universally required policy (all states except Texas), but general liability, professional liability, and cyber insurance are effectively mandatory for most businesses depending on industry and contracts. For small businesses with under $1M revenue, a Business Owner's Policy (BOP) bundling general liability and property insurance at $700-3,000/year is typically the most cost-effective starting point. [src1, src4]

## Constraints
<!-- Agents: read before walking through this decision with a user. -->

- Workers' compensation requirements are state-specific — exemption thresholds, rates, and monopolistic state funds vary; always check the specific state [src6]
- General liability is not legally required but is contractually required by nearly all commercial leases, client contracts, and government work [src4]
- Professional liability (E&O) is required by law for some licensed professions (attorneys, accountants, insurance agents, medical professionals) in many states [src6]
- Insurance costs vary 3-5x by industry — construction GL costs $2,000-5,000/year while consulting GL costs $400-800/year for the same coverage limits [src3]
- Deductible optimization requires understanding cash flow — higher deductibles ($2,500-$5,000) reduce premiums 10-25% but expose the business to more per-claim cost [src1]

## Decision Tree

```
START — Which insurance policies does my business need?
├── Do you have employees (W-2)?
│   ├── YES → Workers' Compensation (REQUIRED in 49 states)
│   │   Cost: $1,000-10,000/yr depending on payroll and industry
│   │   Note: Construction/manufacturing pay highest rates
│   └── NO (solo or 1099 only) → Workers' comp may not be required
│       But: Check state-specific rules; some states count owners
├── Do you have a physical location (lease/own)?
│   ├── YES → Business Owner's Policy (BOP) recommended
│   │   Bundles: General liability + commercial property
│   │   Cost: $700-3,000/yr for small business
│   │   Note: Most leases require proof of GL + property coverage
│   └── NO (home-based/remote) → General Liability standalone
│       Cost: $400-2,000/yr
├── Do you provide professional advice or services?
│   ├── YES → Professional Liability / E&O (often required)
│   │   Cost: $1,200-2,200/yr
│   │   Required by law: Attorneys, CPAs, insurance agents, doctors
│   │   Required by contracts: Consultants, IT services, architects
│   └── NO → Skip professional liability
├── Do you store customer data or process payments?
│   ├── YES → Cyber Liability Insurance (strongly recommended)
│   │   Cost: $1,200-3,000/yr for small business
│   │   Covers: Breach notification, forensics, legal, regulatory fines
│   └── NO → Evaluate based on risk tolerance
├── Do you use vehicles for business?
│   ├── YES → Commercial Auto Insurance (REQUIRED by law)
│   │   Cost: $1,200-3,000/yr per vehicle
│   └── NO → Personal auto may suffice (check with carrier)
├── OVERRIDE CONDITIONS:
│   ├── Government contracts → Typically require GL ($1M/$2M), workers' comp, and often D&O
│   ├── Construction/trades → GL, workers' comp, surety bonds, and umbrella (often $5M+)
│   └── Board of directors exists → Directors & Officers (D&O) insurance: $1,200-7,000/yr
└── DEFAULT (if inputs are ambiguous):
    └── RECOMMEND: BOP ($700-3K/yr) + Workers' Comp (if employees)
        Reason: Covers the two most common claim types at lowest cost
```

## Decision Logic

<!-- If/then rules for agents to evaluate programmatically. -->

### If business has W-2 employees in any US state except Texas
→ **Workers' compensation is legally required**. Purchase immediately. Penalties for non-compliance include fines of $100-100,000+ depending on state, and personal liability for the business owner. [src6]

### If business has a commercial lease OR client contracts requiring insurance
→ **Business Owner's Policy (BOP)**. Bundles general liability ($1M/$2M) and commercial property at 10-15% less than purchasing separately. Most leases and contracts specify minimum GL limits. [src4]

### If business provides professional advice, consulting, or technology services
→ **Professional Liability (E&O)**. $1M/$1M coverage at $1,200-2,200/year. Check state licensing requirements — many professions legally require this coverage. Even when not legally required, client contracts typically mandate it. [src3]

### If business stores PII, processes payments, or operates any digital platform
→ **Cyber Liability Insurance**. Data breach costs average $150-300 per affected record (notification, credit monitoring, legal). A breach affecting 1,000 records costs $150K-300K — far exceeding the $1,200-3,000 annual premium. [src2]

### Default recommendation
→ **Start with BOP + Workers' Comp**. These two policies cover the most common claims (third-party injuries, property damage, workplace injuries) and satisfy most lease and contract requirements. Add professional liability if you provide advisory services, and cyber liability if you handle any customer data. [src1]

## Anti-Patterns

### Wrong: Buying minimum coverage to save on premiums
Business owner selects $300K GL limit to save $200/year on premiums. A single slip-and-fall lawsuit settles for $500K, and the $200K gap comes directly from business assets. The premium savings over 10 years ($2,000) doesn't cover 1% of the exposure. [src4]

### Correct: Match coverage to realistic claim scenarios
Standard $1M/$2M GL limits are the industry minimum for a reason — they cover 95%+ of typical claims. Only reduce limits after analyzing actual claim data for your specific industry. Higher limits add surprisingly little cost: $2M/$4M is typically only 15-25% more than $1M/$2M.

### Wrong: Skipping cyber insurance because "we're too small to be hacked"
43% of cyberattacks target small businesses. The average data breach cost for small businesses is $120K-150K, which exceeds the business savings of many small companies. Cyber insurance at $1,200-3,000/year is the most underpriced coverage relative to actual risk. [src2]

### Correct: Evaluate cyber risk based on data holdings, not company size
If you store any customer names, emails, payment info, or health data, you have cyber exposure. Purchase at least $1M in cyber coverage. The underwriting questionnaire itself often identifies security gaps worth fixing.

### Wrong: Setting deductibles as high as possible to minimize premiums
A $10,000 deductible reduces GL premiums by 20-30% but creates cash flow risk for small businesses. If two claims occur in one year, the $20,000 out-of-pocket expense can threaten operations. [src3]

### Correct: Set deductibles based on cash reserves, not premium savings
Deductible should not exceed what the business can comfortably pay within 30 days. For businesses with under $100K in liquid reserves, $500-$1,000 deductibles are appropriate. For $100K-$500K reserves, $1,000-$2,500. Only businesses with $500K+ reserves should consider $5,000+ deductibles.

**Hidden cost multipliers**: Premiums increase 5-15% annually in hard market cycles (current 2025-2026). Claims history adds 10-40% to renewal premiums. Multi-state operations add complexity and cost. Industry classification changes can dramatically shift rates. [src5]
