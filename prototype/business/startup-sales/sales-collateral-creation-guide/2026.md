# Sales Collateral Creation Guide

## Purpose

This recipe creates a prioritized sales collateral library — one-pager, pitch deck, case study, ROI calculator, and battle card — mapped to pipeline stages, so every prospect receives the right material at the right moment. The output is a complete set of sales-ready documents that reduce deal cycle time by giving buyers the information they need to advance internally. [src1]

## Prerequisites

- [ ] **Value proposition and positioning** — messaging foundation from [Positioning Framework](/business/brand-strategy/positioning-statement-framework/2026)
- [ ] **Sales process definition** — pipeline stages with buyer questions at each stage from [Sales Process Design](/business/startup-sales/sales-process-design/2026)
- [ ] **At least 1 customer story** — before/after metrics and a customer willing to be referenced (for case study)
- [ ] **Product screenshots or demo** — for visual content in one-pager and pitch deck
- [ ] **Pricing information** — for ROI calculator inputs from [Pricing Strategy](/business/startup-sales/pricing-presentation-strategy/2026)

## Constraints

- Create in priority order — build the one-pager first, not the entire library. Each piece should be validated with 3-5 prospects before creating the next. [src5]
- Case studies require written customer permission — never publish customer names, logos, or metrics without explicit approval. [src6]
- ROI calculators must use conservative, defensible numbers — overstating ROI destroys trust when buyers validate internally. [src1]
- Design quality matters less than message clarity at early stage — invest in copy before design. [src3]
- Every collateral piece must map to a specific pipeline stage and buyer question — do not create materials that do not directly support a deal advancing.

## Execution Flow

### Step 1: Create the One-Pager (Priority 1)

**Duration**: 2-3 hours
**Tool**: Canva, Google Slides, or Figma

The one-pager is your highest-priority collateral — it is the single document prospects forward to internal stakeholders. [src1] [src2]

```
One-Pager Template Structure (single page, front only):
────────────────────────────────────────────────

TOP THIRD: Hook
  - Headline: State the problem you solve (not your company name)
    Example: "Cut manual data entry by 80% in 30 days"
  - Subheadline: Who it's for and the outcome
    Example: "For finance teams at $10M-$100M companies tired of spreadsheet chaos"

MIDDLE THIRD: Proof
  - 3 key benefits (icon + 1 sentence each)
    Benefit 1: [Primary value proposition]
    Benefit 2: [Secondary differentiator]
    Benefit 3: [Speed/ease/cost advantage]
  - 1-2 customer proof points
    "[Company name] reduced processing time from 5 days to 4 hours"
    Logo bar: 3-6 customer logos (if you have them)

BOTTOM THIRD: Action
  - How it works (3 steps, 1 sentence each)
    Step 1: Connect → Step 2: Configure → Step 3: Launch
  - CTA: "Book a 15-minute demo" with calendar link or QR code
  - Contact info: name, email, phone

Design rules:
  - Maximum 200 words on the entire page
  - Leave 30%+ white space
  - Use your brand colors (2-3 max)
  - Export as PDF (not editable by recipient)
  - File size under 2MB (for email attachment)
```

**Verify**: Show the one-pager to 3 people outside your company. Can they explain what you do and who it is for within 10 seconds of reading?
**If failed**: If the answer is no, the headline is too vague or too jargon-heavy. Rewrite it to state the outcome, not the product category.

### Step 2: Create the Sales Pitch Deck (Priority 2)

**Duration**: 3-5 hours
**Tool**: Google Slides, Pitch, Beautiful.ai, or Canva

The pitch deck supports your demo conversation — it is not a standalone document, but a visual guide for live presentations. [src3] [src4]

```
Sales Pitch Deck Structure (10-15 slides):
────────────────────────────────────────────────

Slide 1:  TITLE — Company name, one-line value prop, your name
Slide 2:  THE PROBLEM — State the problem using the prospect's language
           Use data: "X% of companies struggle with Y" (cite source)
Slide 3:  WHY NOW — What has changed that makes this urgent?
           Market shift, regulation change, new technology, competitor move
Slide 4:  THE SOLUTION — What you do in 1 sentence + screenshot/visual
           Focus on outcomes, not features
Slide 5:  HOW IT WORKS — 3-step process (simple visual)
Slide 6:  KEY FEATURES — 3-4 features mapped to benefits
           Feature → Benefit → Proof point
Slide 7:  DEMO PLACEHOLDER — "Live Demo" slide (transition to product)
Slide 8:  CASE STUDY — Before/after with specific metrics
           "[Company] went from [state A] to [state B] in [timeframe]"
Slide 9:  SOCIAL PROOF — Customer logos, testimonials, analyst quotes
Slide 10: ROI / VALUE — Expected return, payback period, cost comparison
Slide 11: PRICING — Tiers or packages (only if presenting pricing)
Slide 12: IMPLEMENTATION — Timeline, onboarding process, what to expect
Slide 13: FAQ / OBJECTION HANDLING — 3-4 common concerns with answers
Slide 14: NEXT STEPS — Specific proposed next action with date
Slide 15: APPENDIX — Technical details, integrations, security (backup slides)

Presentation rules:
  - 1 idea per slide, 6 words per bullet max
  - More visuals than text (screenshots, charts, icons)
  - Presenter notes contain the talk track (what you say, not what's on screen)
  - Create a "leave-behind" version: add context to slides so they work without you
```

**Verify**: Present the deck to a friendly prospect or advisor. They should be able to explain your solution, differentiation, and expected ROI after the presentation.
**If failed**: If they cannot articulate these three things, the deck has too many features and not enough outcomes. Rewrite slides 4-6 to focus on "so what?" for the buyer.

### Step 3: Write the First Case Study (Priority 3)

**Duration**: 2-4 hours (plus customer interview)
**Tool**: Google Docs or Notion, then Canva for PDF design

A case study is the most persuasive collateral for mid-pipeline deals — it answers the prospect's core question at the evaluation stage. [src5] [src6]

```
Case Study Template:
────────────────────────────────────────────────

LENGTH: 1-2 pages (500-800 words)
FORMAT: PDF with branded design

HEADLINE: "[Result] for [Company Name]"
  Example: "80% Faster Invoice Processing for Acme Corp"

SECTION 1: The Company (2-3 sentences)
  Who they are, size, industry, relevant context

SECTION 2: The Challenge (1 paragraph)
  What problem they faced, in their words
  Include a direct quote from the customer

SECTION 3: The Solution (1 paragraph)
  What you implemented and how
  Include timeline and integration details

SECTION 4: The Results (bullets with numbers)
  - Primary metric: "80% reduction in processing time"
  - Secondary metric: "Saved 40 hours/month per team member"
  - Tertiary metric: "$120K annual cost savings"
  - Time to value: "Results achieved in 30 days"

SECTION 5: Customer Quote (pull quote, highlighted)
  "[Full quote about the experience and results]"
  — Name, Title, Company

SIDEBAR: At a Glance
  Industry: [industry]
  Company size: [employees/revenue]
  Product used: [your product/tier]
  Time to value: [weeks/months]
  Key result: [top metric]

Customer interview questions (20-minute call):
  1. What was the situation before our solution?
  2. What made you start looking for a solution?
  3. Why did you choose us over alternatives?
  4. What was the implementation process like?
  5. What results have you seen? (get specific numbers)
  6. What would you say to someone considering us?
  7. Can we use your name, title, and company in the case study?
```

**Verify**: Customer has approved the final version in writing. All metrics are accurate and attributable. The case study answers "how is this relevant to me?" for your target ICP.
**If failed**: If you cannot get customer approval to use their name, create an anonymized version: "A Series B SaaS Company" with metrics but without the company identity. Still valuable, but less persuasive.

### Step 4: Build an ROI Calculator (Priority 4)

**Duration**: 2-3 hours
**Tool**: Google Sheets

An interactive ROI calculator helps champions justify the purchase internally — it translates your value proposition into their CFO's language. [src1]

```
ROI Calculator Structure (Google Sheets):
────────────────────────────────────────────────

TAB 1: INPUTS (prospect fills in)
  Cell B3: Number of [relevant unit] (e.g., employees, invoices, leads)
  Cell B4: Current time spent on [process] per [unit] (hours)
  Cell B5: Average hourly cost of team member ($)
  Cell B6: Current error/failure rate (%)
  Cell B7: Cost per error/failure ($)
  Cell B8: Number of [units] processed per month

TAB 1: CALCULATED OUTPUTS (auto-calculated)
  Current annual cost:        =B3 * B4 * B5 * 12
  Cost of errors:             =B8 * B6 * B7 * 12
  Total current cost:         =SUM(above)
  Projected cost with solution: =Total * (1 - improvement_rate)
  Annual savings:             =Current - Projected
  Your investment:            [your pricing, pre-filled]
  Net ROI:                    =(Savings - Investment) / Investment * 100
  Payback period:             =Investment / (Savings / 12) (in months)

TAB 2: ASSUMPTIONS (transparent)
  List all assumptions with sources:
  - "80% time reduction based on average across 15 customers"
  - "Error rate improvement based on [customer name] case study"
  - "Hourly rate default based on BLS data for [role]"

TAB 3: COMPARISON (optional)
  Side-by-side: current state vs. with your solution vs. with competitor
  Include: cost, time, quality, risk dimensions

Design rules:
  - Lock all formula cells (protect sheets)
  - Highlight input cells in yellow, output cells in green
  - Include a "reset to defaults" button
  - Add conditional formatting (green for positive ROI, red for negative)
  - Export-friendly: looks good when printed to PDF
```

**Verify**: Enter realistic customer numbers. The ROI output should be defensible and match your case study results. Test with 3 different prospect profiles.
**If failed**: If ROI comes out negative for a typical prospect, either your pricing is too high or the calculator inputs need adjustment. Do not inflate assumptions to force positive ROI.

### Step 5: Create Internal Battle Cards (Priority 5)

**Duration**: 1-2 hours
**Tool**: Google Docs or Notion

Battle cards are internal-only documents that help reps handle competitive situations and objections. [src6]

```
Battle Card Template (per competitor):
────────────────────────────────────────────────

COMPETITOR: [Name]
LAST UPDATED: [Date]

OVERVIEW
  What they do (1 sentence)
  Pricing: [their pricing tiers]
  Target customer: [who they sell to]
  Key strength: [their main advantage]
  Key weakness: [their main limitation]

WE WIN WHEN:
  - [Scenario where you have clear advantage]
  - [Customer profile that favors your solution]
  - [Feature or capability they lack]

WE LOSE WHEN:
  - [Scenario where they have advantage]
  - [Customer profile that favors them]
  - [Feature or capability you lack]

LANDMINES (questions to ask the prospect):
  - "Have you evaluated how [competitor] handles [your strength area]?"
  - "What is their approach to [area where they are weak]?"
  - "How important is [your differentiator] to your decision?"

OBJECTION HANDLING:
  Objection: "[Competitor] is cheaper"
  Response: "[Frame value, not price. Example: 'Our customers find that
  the [specific feature] saves them [X hours/dollars], which more than
  offsets the price difference.']"

  Objection: "[Competitor] has more features"
  Response: "[Reframe complexity as a cost. Example: 'More features often
  means more time to implement and learn. Our customers go live in
  [X days] versus [their typical timeframe].']"
```

**Verify**: Review with your sales team. They should be able to handle the top 3 competitive objections using the battle card without additional coaching.
**If failed**: If reps still struggle with competitive situations, schedule a 30-minute role-play session using the battle card scenarios.

## Collateral-to-Stage Mapping

```
Pipeline Stage          → Collateral to Send          → Purpose
─────────────────────     ──────────────────────────     ───────────────────
Lead Identified         → One-pager (attached to email) → Quick value overview
Qualified               → Pitch deck (pre-meeting)      → Set context for demo
Discovery Complete      → Case study (post-discovery)    → Build credibility
Demo / Solution         → ROI calculator (during demo)   → Quantify value
Proposal Sent           → Case study + ROI summary       → Support internal champion
Negotiation             → Battle card (internal only)     → Handle competitive objections
```

## Quality Benchmarks

| Quality Metric | Minimum Acceptable | Good | Excellent |
|---------------|-------------------|------|-----------|
| One-pager readability | Explains product in 10 seconds | Prospect forwards to colleague | Champion uses it in internal meetings |
| Pitch deck effectiveness | Supports demo conversation | Prospect requests leave-behind | Deal advances after every presentation |
| Case study persuasiveness | Customer approved with metrics | Prospect asks for reference call | Cited by champion in internal business case |

## Error Handling

| Error | Likely Cause | Recovery Action |
|-------|-------------|----------------|
| Prospects do not read one-pager | Too long, too much jargon, no clear CTA | Reduce to 200 words max; lead with outcome, not features |
| Pitch deck feels like a lecture | Too many text-heavy slides | Apply 6-word rule per bullet; add screenshots and visuals |
| No customers for case study | Pre-revenue or no reference customers | Create anonymized case study or "expected results" based on pilot data |
| ROI calculator shows negative ROI | Pricing too high or assumptions wrong | Adjust assumptions to match real customer data; test with actual metrics |
| Battle cards not used by reps | Too long or hard to find | Keep to 1 page per competitor; store in CRM deal record as attachment |
| Collateral is outdated | No review schedule | Set quarterly review calendar; assign ownership per piece |

## Anti-Patterns

### Wrong: Building a 20-piece collateral library before closing your first 10 deals
Early-stage founders spend weeks creating materials that miss the mark because they have not validated their messaging with real buyers. Most collateral created pre-product-market-fit gets thrown away. [src5]

### Correct: Build one piece, test with 3-5 prospects, iterate, then build the next
Start with the one-pager. Use it in 5 conversations. Revise based on feedback. Then create the pitch deck. This iterative approach ensures every piece actually accelerates deals.

### Wrong: Creating a pitch deck that works as a leave-behind document
A deck designed for live presentation has minimal text and relies on the presenter. A leave-behind needs full context to stand alone. Using the same deck for both means it fails at both. [src3]

### Correct: Create two versions — presentation and leave-behind
The presentation version has big visuals and presenter notes. The leave-behind version adds context to each slide so it makes sense without narration.

### Wrong: Writing case studies in your voice instead of the customer's
Case studies that read like marketing copy are dismissed by skeptical buyers. The most persuasive element is the customer's own words and specific metrics. [src6]

### Correct: Use direct quotes and specific numbers
Interview the customer, use their exact language, and highlight their specific metrics. Let the customer tell the story.
