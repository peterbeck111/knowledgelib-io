# Sales Process Design

## Purpose

This recipe produces a complete, documented B2B sales process with named pipeline stages, qualification criteria, discovery call frameworks, demo structure, proposal templates, and closing playbooks — configured in your CRM and ready for your first (or next) sales hire to execute from day one. The output is a repeatable system that converts leads to customers through a predictable sequence of verifiable steps. [src1]

## Prerequisites

- [ ] **ICP definition** — documented ideal customer profile with firmographics, pain points, and buying triggers from [ICP Definition Framework](/business/customer-research/icp-definition-framework/2026)
- [ ] **Product/service with defined pricing** — at minimum, know your price point and packaging tiers
- [ ] **CRM account** — free account at [HubSpot](https://app.hubspot.com/signup), [Pipedrive](https://www.pipedrive.com/register), or [Close](https://app.close.com/signup/) (free tier: HubSpot 2 users, 1 pipeline)
- [ ] **Meeting scheduler** — [Calendly](https://calendly.com) free tier or [Cal.com](https://cal.com) for discovery call booking
- [ ] **5+ closed deals or 10+ prospect conversations** — needed to design stages based on real buyer behavior, not theory

## Constraints

- Pipeline stages must mirror your actual buyer journey — copying a generic template without adaptation produces a process nobody follows. [src2]
- Qualification framework must match deal complexity: BANT for transactional (<$25K ACV), SPICED for mid-market ($25-75K), MEDDIC/MEDDPICC for enterprise (>$75K). [src3]
- Every stage requires explicit exit criteria — deals cannot advance without meeting documented conditions. [src4]
- Discovery calls should target 25-45 minutes; demos 30-45 minutes. Longer sessions reduce prospect commitment to next steps.
- Do not build more than 5-7 pipeline stages for early-stage companies — complexity kills adoption. [src1]

## Execution Flow

### Step 1: Map Your Buyer Journey

**Duration**: 1-2 hours
**Tool**: Whiteboard or document

Review your last 5-10 closed deals (or prospect conversations if pre-revenue) and identify the actual steps buyers took from first contact to signed contract. [src2]

```
Buyer Journey Mapping Exercise:
────────────────────────────────────────────────
For each closed deal, answer:
1. How did the prospect first engage? (inbound form, cold email, referral, event)
2. What happened in the first conversation? (discovery, qualification, or both)
3. How many meetings occurred before a proposal?
4. Who else got involved in the decision? (titles and roles)
5. What triggered the final buying decision?
6. How long from first contact to signed contract?
7. What nearly killed the deal?

Pattern Recognition:
- Group similar journeys together
- Identify the 4-6 key inflection points where deals advanced or stalled
- These inflection points become your pipeline stages
```

**Verify**: You have 4-6 distinct phases documented with the key activities and decisions in each.
**If failed**: If you have fewer than 5 conversations to draw from, use the standard B2B template in Step 2 and refine as you close your first 5-10 deals.

### Step 2: Define Pipeline Stages and Exit Criteria

**Duration**: 1-2 hours
**Tool**: Document or spreadsheet

Map your buyer journey phases to named pipeline stages with explicit exit criteria that determine when a deal can advance. [src4]

```
Standard B2B Startup Pipeline (adapt to your journey):
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Stage 1: LEAD IDENTIFIED
  Activities: Inbound form submitted, outbound target identified, referral received
  Exit criteria: Contact info verified, matches ICP firmographics, initial research done
  Probability: 10%
  Owner: SDR or founder

Stage 2: QUALIFIED
  Activities: Qualification call completed, framework criteria assessed
  Exit criteria: Meets BANT/SPICED/MEDDIC thresholds (see Step 3), pain confirmed
  Probability: 25%
  Owner: SDR hands to AE (or founder continues)

Stage 3: DISCOVERY COMPLETE
  Activities: Deep discovery call, pain quantified, stakeholders mapped
  Exit criteria: Pain quantified in dollars or time, decision-maker identified,
                 timeline confirmed, next step scheduled
  Probability: 40%
  Owner: AE or founder

Stage 4: DEMO / SOLUTION PRESENTED
  Activities: Tailored demo or solution presentation, technical validation
  Exit criteria: Champion confirms solution fits, no blocking technical objections,
                 budget range discussed
  Probability: 60%
  Owner: AE (+ SE for technical demos)

Stage 5: PROPOSAL SENT
  Activities: Formal proposal or quote delivered, pricing presented
  Exit criteria: Proposal received and reviewed, decision timeline confirmed,
                 procurement/legal contact identified (if applicable)
  Probability: 75%
  Owner: AE

Stage 6: NEGOTIATION
  Activities: Contract redlines, discount requests, legal review
  Exit criteria: Mutual agreement on terms, contract sent for signature
  Probability: 85%
  Owner: AE + management for discount approval

Stage 7: CLOSED WON / CLOSED LOST
  Activities: Contract signed (won) or deal dispositioned with loss reason (lost)
  Exit criteria: Payment received or loss reason documented
  Probability: 100% / 0%
  Owner: AE → CS handoff for won deals
```

**Verify**: Each stage has a clear name, 2-3 exit criteria, an assigned owner, and a probability percentage. Total stages between 5-7.
**If failed**: If you have more than 7 stages, combine stages that have similar activities. If fewer than 5, you are likely skipping critical qualification or discovery steps.

### Step 3: Select and Configure Qualification Framework

**Duration**: 1 hour
**Tool**: Document

Choose a qualification framework based on your deal complexity and train your team on using it consistently. [src3] [src5]

```
Qualification Framework Selection:
────────────────────────────────────────────────

BANT — Best for: ACV < $25K, single decision-maker, < 30 day cycles
  B - Budget:    "Do you have budget allocated for this?"
  A - Authority: "Are you the decision-maker, or who else is involved?"
  N - Need:      "What problem are you trying to solve?"
  T - Timing:    "When do you need this implemented?"
  Scoring: All 4 = qualified. Missing 1 = conditionally qualified. Missing 2+ = nurture.

SPICED — Best for: ACV $25K-$75K, 2-4 stakeholders, 1-3 month cycles
  S - Situation:      "Tell me about your current setup and team."
  P - Pain:           "What is not working today?"
  I - Impact:         "What happens if this problem continues for 6 months?"
  C - Critical Event: "Is there a deadline or trigger driving this decision?"
  E - Decision:       "Walk me through how your team makes purchasing decisions."
  Scoring: Strong impact + critical event = high priority. No critical event = lower urgency.

MEDDIC — Best for: ACV > $75K, 4+ stakeholders, 3+ month cycles
  M - Metrics:         "What KPIs will this project impact?"
  E - Economic Buyer:  "Who signs the contract and controls budget?"
  D - Decision Criteria: "What are the top 3 things you are evaluating vendors on?"
  D - Decision Process:  "What steps remain between now and a signed contract?"
  I - Identify Pain:     "What is the cost of the current problem?"
  C - Champion:          "Who internally is advocating for this change?"
  Scoring: Must identify champion + economic buyer. No champion = deal at risk.
```

**Verify**: Framework selected, scoring criteria defined, and 5-8 qualification questions written for your specific product.
**If failed**: If unsure which framework to use, start with BANT — it is the simplest and covers the basics. Upgrade to SPICED once you consistently see multi-stakeholder deals.

### Step 4: Build Discovery Call and Demo Frameworks

**Duration**: 1-2 hours
**Tool**: Document

Structure your discovery call around your qualification framework and your demo around the prospect's stated pain points. [src1]

```
Discovery Call Structure (30-minute call):
────────────────────────────────────────────────

0:00-2:00  OPENER
  - Thank them for their time
  - Set the agenda: "I'd like to learn about [their situation], share how we've
    helped similar companies, and if there's a fit, schedule a deeper dive."
  - Confirm time: "Does that work for our 30 minutes?"

2:00-15:00  DISCOVERY (qualification framework questions)
  - Ask framework questions in natural conversation, not interrogation
  - Use "Tell me about..." and "Walk me through..." vs yes/no questions
  - Take notes on exact words the prospect uses (mirror language in demo)

15:00-20:00  BRIDGE
  - Summarize what you heard: "So if I understand correctly, [pain] is costing
    you [impact], and you need [solution] by [timeline]."
  - Confirm accuracy: "Did I get that right?"

20:00-25:00  POSITION
  - Share 1-2 relevant customer stories (not a product pitch)
  - Connect stories to their specific pain points

25:00-30:00  NEXT STEP
  - If qualified: Schedule demo/deep-dive with decision-makers
  - If not qualified: Offer resources and set follow-up for when timing improves
  - Always confirm next step before hanging up: "I'll send a calendar invite
    for [date] at [time] with [attendees]. Sound good?"

Demo Structure (45-minute demo):
────────────────────────────────────────────────

0:00-5:00   RECAP & AGENDA
  - Recap discovery findings (shows you listened)
  - Set agenda: "I'll show you three things based on what you told me..."

5:00-30:00  TAILORED DEMO (3 sections, 8 min each)
  - Section 1: Address primary pain point
  - Section 2: Address secondary pain point
  - Section 3: Differentiator / "wow" moment
  - After each section: "How does that compare to what you're doing today?"

30:00-40:00  Q&A AND OBJECTION HANDLING
  - Address concerns directly
  - Use "feel, felt, found" for common objections

40:00-45:00  NEXT STEPS
  - Propose specific next step: proposal, technical validation, or exec meeting
  - Confirm timeline and attendees
```

**Verify**: You have a documented discovery call script with your qualification questions embedded and a demo structure with 3 tailored sections.
**If failed**: If you cannot fill in the demo sections, you need more prospect conversations to understand which features address which pain points.

### Step 5: Design Proposal and Closing Process

**Duration**: 1 hour
**Tool**: Document + proposal tool (Google Docs, PandaDoc, or Qwilr)

Create a proposal template and define your closing checklist. [src4]

```
Proposal Template Structure:
────────────────────────────────────────────────

1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (1 paragraph)
   - Restate their challenge in their words
   - State your solution and expected outcome
   - Include 1 proof point (customer result or metric)

2. RECOMMENDED SOLUTION (1 page)
   - What you will deliver
   - Implementation timeline
   - What is included vs. not included

3. INVESTMENT (1 page)
   - Pricing table with tiers if applicable
   - Payment terms (annual vs. monthly, net-30)
   - What happens after they sign (onboarding timeline)

4. SOCIAL PROOF (half page)
   - 2-3 relevant customer logos
   - 1 short testimonial quote
   - Key metric: "X% improvement in Y for Z company"

5. NEXT STEPS (3 bullet points)
   - Review and provide feedback by [date]
   - Contract execution by [date]
   - Kick-off meeting on [date]

Closing Checklist:
────────────────────────────────────────────────
□ All decision-makers have seen the demo or proposal
□ Budget confirmed and approved
□ Procurement/legal requirements identified
□ Implementation timeline agreed
□ Discount (if any) approved by sales management
□ Contract sent via e-signature tool
□ Follow-up scheduled within 48 hours of sending
□ If no response in 5 business days, call champion directly
```

**Verify**: Proposal template created with all 5 sections. Closing checklist has 6+ items covering budget, legal, and timeline.
**If failed**: If you cannot create the social proof section, use industry statistics or analyst quotes until you have your first 2-3 case studies.

### Step 6: Configure CRM Pipeline and Test

**Duration**: 1-2 hours
**Tool**: CRM platform

Translate your designed process into your CRM by creating pipeline stages, required fields, and automation. [src1]

```
CRM Configuration Checklist:
────────────────────────────────────────────────

Pipeline Stages (create in exact order from Step 2):
  1. Lead Identified      → 10%
  2. Qualified            → 25%
  3. Discovery Complete   → 40%
  4. Demo / Solution      → 60%
  5. Proposal Sent        → 75%
  6. Negotiation          → 85%
  7. Closed Won           → 100%
  8. Closed Lost          → 0%

Required Deal Properties:
  - Deal name (company + use case)
  - Deal amount (ACV)
  - Close date (expected)
  - Deal owner
  - Loss reason (required on Closed Lost)
  - Qualification score (from framework)
  - Next step (free text, required on every save)
  - Decision-maker identified (yes/no)

Automation Rules:
  - When deal moves to "Qualified" → create task: "Schedule discovery call"
  - When deal moves to "Proposal Sent" → create task: "Follow up in 3 days"
  - When deal is "Closed Won" → notify CS team for handoff
  - When deal has no activity for 14 days → alert deal owner

HubSpot Free Tier Setup:
  Settings → Objects → Deals → Pipelines → Edit stages
  Drag stages to reorder, set probability for each
  Add required properties under "Conditional stage properties"
```

**Verify**: Create a test deal and move it through all stages. Confirm required fields block progression, automations fire, and reporting shows accurate pipeline value.
**If failed**: If CRM does not support required fields per stage (HubSpot free limitation), use a naming convention or manual checklist until you upgrade.

## Error Handling

| Error | Likely Cause | Recovery Action |
|-------|-------------|----------------|
| Reps skip qualification stage | Framework too complex or not enforced | Simplify to 3-4 mandatory questions; make qualification fields required in CRM |
| Deals stall at demo stage | Demo not tailored to prospect pain; wrong stakeholders in the room | Review discovery notes before every demo; require decision-maker attendance |
| High proposal-to-close drop-off | Proposal sent to non-decision-makers or pricing misaligned | Confirm budget and authority before sending proposal; use verbal pricing before written |
| Pipeline shows inflated value | Probability percentages too high or stale deals not cleaned | Run weekly pipeline hygiene: remove deals with no activity > 30 days |
| Long sales cycles with no progress | Missing critical event or champion | Add "critical event" question to discovery; require champion identification before Stage 4 |

## Anti-Patterns

### Wrong: Copying a sales process template without adapting to your buyer journey
Generic templates (e.g., "7-stage enterprise pipeline") applied to a $5K ACV product with single decision-makers create unnecessary complexity. Reps will shortcut stages, data quality degrades, and pipeline reporting becomes meaningless. [src2]

### Correct: Start from your closed deals and work backward
Map 5-10 real deals to identify the actual inflection points. Your pipeline stages should reflect how your buyers actually buy, not how a sales methodology textbook says they should.

### Wrong: Using MEDDIC for a $3K/month SMB product
MEDDIC was designed for $100K+ enterprise deals with complex buying committees. Applying it to SMB deals over-qualifies prospects and adds weeks to a cycle that should take days. [src3]

### Correct: Match qualification depth to deal value
Use BANT for quick-cycle transactional deals. Reserve MEDDIC for deals where the cost of losing a late-stage opportunity justifies the upfront qualification investment.

### Wrong: Advancing deals without verifiable next steps
Moving a deal from "Discovery" to "Demo Scheduled" based on a vague "they seemed interested" is the single largest source of pipeline inaccuracy. [src4]

### Correct: Require a calendar-confirmed next step before stage advancement
No next step = deal stays in current stage. This single rule eliminates 60-70% of stale pipeline.
