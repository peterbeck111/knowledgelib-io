# Pitch Deck Slide-by-Slide Guide

## Purpose

This recipe walks through every standard pitch deck slide with exact content requirements, word limits, common failure modes, and design rules — producing a fully populated pitch deck where each slide passes the 60-second comprehension test. The output is a complete, investor-ready presentation with every slide meeting quality benchmarks for clarity, data density, and visual design.

## Prerequisites

- [ ] **Deck structure outline** — slide order and count from [Pitch Deck Structure by Stage](/business/fundraising/pitch-deck-structure-by-stage/2026) or your own framework
- [ ] **Business data** — revenue, users, growth rates, CAC, LTV, retention metrics (whatever is available)
- [ ] **Product screenshots or mockups** — at least 2-3 high-resolution images of the product in use
- [ ] **Market research** — TAM/SAM/SOM figures with sources
- [ ] **Team headshots and bios** — professional photos and 1-2 sentence achievement summaries per co-founder
- [ ] **Competitive landscape analysis** — list of 4-8 competitors with differentiation points
- [ ] **Presentation tool** — [Google Slides](https://slides.google.com), [Canva](https://www.canva.com), [Figma](https://www.figma.com), PowerPoint, or Keynote

## Constraints

- The 10/20/30 Rule: 10 slides, 20 minutes, 30-point minimum font. This is the baseline. [src1]
- One idea per slide. Mixing messages halves retention. [src7]
- Average investor deck review: 3.2 minutes. If a slide cannot communicate its point in 15-30 seconds, it is too complex. [src6]
- Problem and Solution must be separate slides. Combining them confuses the narrative. [src3]
- Traction metrics must include timeframes. "$50K MRR" means nothing without "growing 25% MoM over 6 months." [src5]

## Execution Flow

### Step 1: Title Slide — The 10-Second First Impression

**Duration**: 10 minutes
**Tool**: Presentation software

The title slide determines whether the investor reads further. It must communicate what you do instantly.

```
Required Content:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. Company name (prominent, top or center)
2. Logo (if available — skip if no professional logo yet)
3. One-line description: ≤10 words explaining what you do
   Format: "We [verb] [thing] for [audience]"
   Example: "We automate compliance reporting for fintech startups"
4. Founder name and title
5. Contact info: email + phone (investors lose decks; make it easy to reach you)
6. Round info (optional): "Raising $X Seed Round"

Common Failures:
- Tagline is a vision statement, not a description ("Making the world better" tells nothing)
- Missing contact info (investor cannot follow up)
- Logo takes up half the slide with no explanation of the business

Design Rules:
- Max 3 visual elements: logo, tagline, contact
- White/dark background, no stock photos
- Company name in ≥48pt font
```

**Verify**: Show the slide to someone outside your industry for 5 seconds. Can they tell what your company does?
**If failed**: Rewrite the one-liner. If it needs explanation, it is not clear enough.

### Step 2: Problem Slide — Make the Pain Visceral

**Duration**: 20 minutes
**Tool**: Presentation software

The problem slide must make the investor feel the pain before seeing the solution. [src3]

```
Required Content:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. Problem statement: 1 clear sentence describing the specific pain
2. Who feels it: Named persona or customer segment
3. Current alternatives: How people cope today (and why those solutions fail)
4. Quantification: 1-2 data points showing scale or cost of the problem
   Examples: "70% of SMBs struggle with X" or "$X billion lost annually to Y"

Content Framework (pick one):
A) Story: "Meet Sarah. She spends 15 hours/week on X because..."
B) Data: "X million people face [problem], costing $Y annually"
C) Quote: Real customer quote describing the pain

Common Failures:
- Too vague: "Communication is broken" (who? where? how?)
- Too broad: Describing an entire industry problem instead of specific pain
- No data: Assertions without numbers lack credibility
- Multiple problems: One slide, one problem. Save secondary pains for appendix

Design Rules:
- 3 bullet points maximum
- ≤40 words total body text
- One supporting visual: icon, chart, or customer photo
- Use red/warm tones subtly to convey urgency
```

**Verify**: The investor can state the problem in their own words after seeing the slide for 15 seconds.
**If failed**: Your problem is too abstract. Add a specific example or customer quote.

### Step 3: Solution Slide — Show, Don't Tell

**Duration**: 20 minutes
**Tool**: Presentation software + product screenshots

The solution slide links directly to the problem slide. Investors should understand how you fix the pain in under 15 seconds. [src4]

```
Required Content:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. Solution statement: 1-2 sentences linking to the problem
   Format: "[Problem] exists because [root cause]. We solve this by [mechanism]."
2. Product visual: Screenshot, mockup, or demo GIF (occupies 50%+ of slide)
3. 3 key benefits: Not features. Benefits = outcomes for the customer
   Example: "Save 15 hours/week" not "Automated reporting engine"
4. Differentiation hook: One sentence on what makes this uniquely effective

Common Failures:
- All text, no product visual (investors need to SEE the solution)
- Feature list instead of benefits (investors don't care about your tech stack)
- No connection to problem slide (feels like a different presentation)
- Jargon: "AI-powered ML pipeline" → "Automated analysis in 10 seconds"

Design Rules:
- Product screenshot or mockup takes ≥50% of slide area
- Benefits listed in 3 short bullets beside or below the visual
- ≤30 words of text (the product visual does the talking)
```

**Verify**: Cover the solution text and show only the screenshot. Can someone guess what the product does?
**If failed**: Your screenshot is not self-explanatory enough. Add UI annotations or choose a different view.

### Step 4: Market Size Slide — Bottoms-Up, Not Fantasy

**Duration**: 25 minutes
**Tool**: Presentation software + market data

Market sizing must be credible. Investors immediately discount "top-down only" TAM claims. [src6]

```
Required Content:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. TAM (Total Addressable Market): Top-down reference only
   Source from industry reports (Gartner, Statista, government data)
2. SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market): Your segment of the TAM
   Define by geography, customer type, price point
3. SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market): What you can realistically capture in 2-3 years
   Bottoms-up: (Target customers) × (Annual revenue per customer) = SOM
4. Sources: Cite every number. Uncited market claims = instant credibility loss

Calculation Template:
  TAM: Global [industry] market = $X billion (Source: Gartner 2025)
  SAM: [Segment] in [geography] = $X billion
  SOM: [N target customers] × [$X ARPU] = $X million Y1-Y3

Common Failures:
- "It's a $100B market" with no source (every market is large if you define it broadly)
- TAM only, no SAM/SOM (signals the founder hasn't thought about actual addressability)
- Top-down only ("if we get 1% of the market...") — use bottoms-up always
- Conflating TAM with revenue opportunity (TAM ≠ your revenue potential)

Design Rules:
- Concentric circles diagram (TAM > SAM > SOM) is the industry standard
- Dollar amounts in ≥36pt font
- Source citations in small text at bottom of slide
- ≤25 words of body text — let the numbers speak
```

**Verify**: Can you defend every number if challenged? Is each figure sourced?
**If failed**: Replace uncited numbers with bottoms-up calculations from your own customer data.

### Step 5: Traction Slide — Proof Over Promises

**Duration**: 20 minutes
**Tool**: Presentation software + analytics data

Traction is the most important slide for seed and the opening slide for Series A. Order metrics from most impressive to least. [src5]

```
Required Content:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. Primary metric: The single strongest number (MRR, users, growth rate)
   ALWAYS include timeframe: "$50K MRR, growing 25% MoM over 6 months"
2. Supporting metrics: 2-3 additional data points
   Revenue, users, retention, CAC, LTV, NPS — pick what's strongest
3. Visual: Line chart showing growth over time (the "hockey stick" if you have it)
4. Qualitative proof (if early): Customer logos, testimonials, LOIs, partnerships

If You Have No Revenue Yet:
- Waitlist size + conversion rate
- Beta users + engagement metrics
- Letters of intent (LOIs) with dollar amounts
- Pilot results with named customers
- Pre-orders or signed contracts

Stage-Specific Metrics:
  Pre-seed: Problem validation interviews (50+), waitlist, LOIs
  Seed: Users, revenue run rate, growth rate, retention
  Series A: MRR/ARR, cohort retention, CAC/LTV ratio, payback period

Common Failures:
- Vanity metrics: "10,000 signups" (how many active? how many paying?)
- No timeframe: "$100K revenue" (this month? this year? lifetime?)
- Wrong metric for stage: Showing downloads when investors want retention
- Flat or declining chart presented as "growth" — better to acknowledge and explain

Design Rules:
- Primary metric in ≥48pt font, prominently placed
- Growth chart occupies 50%+ of slide
- ≤4 metrics total — more than 4 dilutes focus
- Green color for growth charts (psychological association)
```

**Verify**: The investor can name your top metric and growth rate after 10 seconds.
**If failed**: Your metrics are buried in text. Pull the key number to ≥48pt font and add the growth chart.

### Step 6: Team Slide — Achievements, Not Titles

**Duration**: 15 minutes
**Tool**: Presentation software

Investors fund people, not ideas. The team slide must communicate why THIS team can execute THIS vision. [src6]

```
Required Content:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. Co-founders: Photo + name + ONE standout achievement per person
   NOT: "CEO, 10 years experience in tech"
   YES: "Previously built X product to $5M ARR, acquired by Y"
2. Key hires (if notable): CTO, VP Engineering, etc. — only include if impressive
3. Domain expertise signal: Why this team understands this problem uniquely
4. Advisory board (optional): Only if names are recognizable to target investors

Common Failures:
- Job titles without achievements (investors don't care about titles at startups)
- Too many people (4+ team members dilutes focus — show 2-3 key people)
- No photos (slides without faces feel impersonal, reduce trust)
- Missing founder-market fit narrative (why YOU specifically?)

Design Rules:
- Professional headshots (consistent style, not vacation photos)
- 2-3 people maximum on main slide (others in appendix)
- Achievement text in ≤15 words per person
- LinkedIn icons linking to profiles (for emailed decks)
```

**Verify**: The investor understands what each person is uniquely great at in 10 seconds.
**If failed**: Replace generic titles with specific, verifiable achievements.

### Step 7: Ask Slide — Specific, Justified, Milestone-Linked

**Duration**: 15 minutes
**Tool**: Presentation software

The closing slide must make the investment case concrete. [src1]

```
Required Content:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. Amount: Exact dollar figure — "$2M Seed Round" not "$1-3M"
2. Use of funds: 3-4 categories with percentages
   Example: Product (40%), Sales & Marketing (30%), Team (20%), Operations (10%)
3. Milestones: What this raise unlocks (3 specific outcomes, 12-18 month horizon)
   Example: "Reach $100K MRR, hire 3 engineers, launch in 2 new markets"
4. Contact info: Email, phone, calendly link (make it frictionless to follow up)

Common Failures:
- Range instead of number: "$1-3M" signals uncertainty about the business
- No use of funds: Investor cannot evaluate whether the amount is right
- Missing milestones: Money without outcomes = unclear ROI thesis
- Forgetting contact info: The most basic mistake, yet extremely common

Design Rules:
- Dollar amount in ≥48pt font, prominently placed
- Pie chart or simple bar for use-of-funds allocation
- Milestones as 3 bullet points with timeline
- ≤30 words total (this is not a detailed financial plan)
```

**Verify**: An investor knows exactly how much you need, what you will do with it, and what you will achieve.
**If failed**: If you cannot specify an amount, you are not ready to fundraise. Model your costs first.

## Quality Benchmarks

| Quality Metric | Minimum Acceptable | Good | Excellent |
|---------------|-------------------|------|-----------|
| Word count per slide | < 50 words | < 30 words | < 20 words + visual |
| 60-second comprehension | Key idea understood in 60s | Understood in 30s | Understood in 15s |
| Data citations | 1 per data slide | 2+ per data slide | Primary + secondary sources |
| Product visuals | 1 screenshot | 2-3 screenshots with annotations | Demo GIF or interactive prototype link |
| Traction metrics with timeframes | 1 metric with timeframe | 3+ metrics with timeframes | Cohort charts + growth curves |

**If below minimum**: The most common fix is removing text, not adding it. Delete every word that does not directly support the slide's one key message. Replace text with visuals wherever possible.

## Error Handling

| Error | Likely Cause | Recovery Action |
|-------|-------------|----------------|
| Slide has > 50 words | Trying to explain too much on one slide | Split into two slides or move detail to appendix |
| Investor confused after 60 seconds | Multiple ideas competing on one slide | Apply one-idea-per-slide rule; remove secondary message |
| No product visual on solution slide | Product is not built yet or no screenshots | Create mockup in Figma (free) or use wireframe with annotation |
| Traction slide looks empty | Pre-revenue, pre-user startup | Show alternative traction: waitlist, LOIs, pilot agreements, interviews conducted |
| Market size feels arbitrary | Top-down TAM only with no bottoms-up | Calculate SOM from (target customers x ARPU); cite every number |
| Team slide feels generic | Job titles without achievements | Replace every title with a specific, verifiable accomplishment |

## Anti-Patterns

### Wrong: Walls of text explaining the solution
Investors skim, they do not read. A solution slide with 100+ words and no product visual gets skipped entirely. The average review time of 3.2 minutes means each slide gets 15-20 seconds of attention. [src7]

### Correct: Product screenshot takes 50%+ of the slide
Let the visual communicate. Use 3 short bullet points for benefits (not features), and let the screenshot demonstrate the product in action.

### Wrong: Vanity metrics without engagement context
"100,000 downloads" means nothing if daily active users are 500. Investors immediately ask about activation, retention, and revenue — presenting vanity metrics without context signals either ignorance or deception. [src5]

### Correct: Pair every acquisition metric with an engagement metric
"100,000 downloads → 15,000 MAU → 8% paid conversion → $45K MRR" tells a complete story. Always include the funnel, not just the top.

### Wrong: Generic team bios with job titles
"CEO — 10 years in tech" tells investors nothing about execution capability. Every startup CEO has "experience in tech." This adds no differentiation signal. [src2]

### Correct: Specific, verifiable achievements
"Built Acme's ML pipeline from 0 to 2M predictions/day; previously co-founded X (acquired by Y for $Z)." Achievements signal capability far more than titles.
