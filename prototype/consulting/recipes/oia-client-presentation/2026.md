# OIA Client Presentation

## Purpose

This recipe produces a complete client-facing presentation of Organizational Immune Analysis findings, including interactive health score dashboards, risk heat maps by organizational dimension, before/after network topology comparisons, a phased remediation roadmap, and an outcome-based retainer proposal. The presentation replaces the traditional 50-page slide deck with a living diagnostic that shows the organization as a biological system — visualizing dysfunction as structural disease rather than individual blame. [src1]

## Prerequisites

- [ ] **Organizational health scores** available from `consulting/oia/organizational-health-scoring/2026` — [Health Scoring Framework](/consulting/oia/organizational-health-scoring/2026)
- [ ] **Network topology data** — anonymized ONA graph export from Microsoft Viva Insights or Slack analytics
- [ ] **Risk assessment results** — structured risk register from OIA diagnostic phase
- [ ] **30-day baseline measurement** — minimum data collection period before presenting before/after comparisons
- [ ] **Audience profile** — confirmed primary audience (C-suite, IT leadership, HR, or mixed) to calibrate language and depth

## Constraints

- Anonymization: Never present individual employee names in network visualizations to non-HR audiences. Use role-based identifiers ("Engineering Lead #3") or team-level aggregation. [src2]
- Baseline requirement: Before/after topology comparisons require minimum 30 calendar days of baseline data. Presenting without a baseline is speculation, not diagnosis — it undermines the entire scientific credibility of the OIA methodology.
- Pricing model: Retainer proposals must use outcome-based (metabolic recovery) pricing, never time-and-materials. The entire value proposition of OIA is "pay for the cure, not the clock." [src3]
- Visual clarity: Follow Stephen Few's dashboard design principles — no 3D charts, no pie charts for more than 3 categories, no dual-axis charts. Cognitive load kills executive attention. [src4]
- Narrative structure: Every data visualization must answer a specific question. Uncontextualized charts create the "so what?" problem that kills consulting engagements. [src5]

## Execution Flow

### Step 1: Structure the Narrative Arc

**Duration**: 1-2 hours
**Tool**: Outline document

Build the presentation around a four-act diagnostic narrative:

1. **The Patient** — Current organizational state (health scores, vital signs)
2. **The Diagnosis** — Where the structural defects are (risk heat maps, network topology gaps)
3. **The Treatment Plan** — Remediation roadmap with phased interventions
4. **The Prognosis** — Expected outcomes with metabolic recovery milestones and retainer proposal

For each act, define the specific question being answered and the visualization type that answers it. Never present a chart without a preceding question. [src5]

**Verify**: Each section has a clear "question --> answer --> implication" structure. No orphan visualizations.
**If failed**: Review with a colleague — if they cannot state the takeaway of each section in one sentence, the narrative needs restructuring.

### Step 2: Build Health Score Dashboard

**Duration**: 3-5 hours
**Tool**: D3.js or Chart.js

Create the organizational health score visualization with these components:

- **Radar chart**: 6-8 dimensions (communication flow, decision velocity, knowledge distribution, structural resilience, compliance health, innovation capacity)
- **Benchmark overlay**: Industry benchmarks shown as a gray polygon behind the client's scores
- **Trend arrows**: Direction indicators showing improvement or degradation since baseline

```javascript
// Health score radar chart configuration
const dimensions = [
  { axis: "Communication Flow", value: 0.72, benchmark: 0.65 },
  { axis: "Decision Velocity", value: 0.45, benchmark: 0.60 },
  { axis: "Knowledge Distribution", value: 0.58, benchmark: 0.55 },
  { axis: "Structural Resilience", value: 0.38, benchmark: 0.50 },
  { axis: "Compliance Health", value: 0.81, benchmark: 0.70 },
  { axis: "Innovation Capacity", value: 0.52, benchmark: 0.55 }
];
// Red zone: < benchmark - 0.15 | Yellow: within 0.15 of benchmark | Green: > benchmark
```

**Verify**: All dimensions use 0-1 scale. Benchmark data is from validated industry source, not fabricated. Color coding follows red/yellow/green with accessible color choices.
**If failed**: If benchmark data unavailable, show absolute scores with percentile annotations from OIA historical database.

### Step 3: Generate Risk Heat Maps

**Duration**: 2-4 hours
**Tool**: D3.js heatmap or Plotly

Build a risk heat map matrix with organizational dimensions on one axis and risk categories on the other:

| | Communication | Decision-Making | Knowledge | Compliance | Innovation |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Single Point of Failure** | Low | Critical | High | Low | Medium |
| **Information Bottleneck** | High | Medium | Critical | Low | High |
| **Complexity Collapse** | Medium | High | Medium | Medium | Low |
| **Immune Response (change resistance)** | Low | Critical | Low | High | Critical |

Each cell shows severity (critical/high/medium/low) with supporting evidence from the ONA data. Critical cells link to specific structural findings.

**Verify**: Every "Critical" cell has supporting evidence from diagnostic data — no severity ratings based on intuition alone.
**If failed**: Downgrade any unsupported cells to "Insufficient Data" rather than guessing severity.

### Step 4: Create Before/After Network Topology Comparison

**Duration**: 2-3 hours
**Tool**: D3.js force-directed graph

Visualize the organizational communication network as a force-directed graph showing:

- **Before state**: Current topology with bottlenecks highlighted (oversized nodes = single points of failure, red edges = broken pathways, gray clusters = isolated silos)
- **After state (projected)**: Expected topology after remediation — distributed load, connected clusters, eliminated bottlenecks

Use the Swiss Cheese Model framing: show structural "holes" in the communication layers and how remediation closes them. [src2]

**Verify**: Node sizing correlates with actual communication centrality metrics (betweenness centrality). Not artistic interpretation.
**If failed**: If ONA data lacks sufficient granularity for individual node analysis, aggregate to team-level topology.

### Step 5: Build Remediation Roadmap

**Duration**: 1-2 hours
**Tool**: Mermaid.js or timeline library

Create a phased remediation timeline with:

- **Phase 1 (0-30 days)**: Quick wins — immediate structural fixes (communication channel redesign, bottleneck relief)
- **Phase 2 (30-90 days)**: Core remediation — process redesign, WBC deployment, escalation workflow activation
- **Phase 3 (90-180 days)**: Hardening — graduated autonomy expansion, measurement calibration, culture shift monitoring
- **Phase 4 (180+ days)**: Continuous immunity — ongoing monitoring, periodic re-diagnosis, retainer-based health maintenance

Each phase includes specific health score targets and measurable success criteria.

**Verify**: Each phase has quantified success criteria (e.g., "Decision velocity score improves from 0.45 to 0.55"). No vague milestones.
**If failed**: If health score targets cannot be quantified, use directional indicators ("improve by 15-20%") with confidence intervals.

### Step 6: Draft Retainer Proposal with Metabolic Recovery Pricing

**Duration**: 2-3 hours
**Tool**: Document template + pricing calculator

Build the retainer proposal using outcome-based pricing:

- **Recovery fee**: Percentage of quantified operational savings (typically 15-25% of documented waste reduction)
- **Monitoring retainer**: Fixed monthly fee for ongoing WBC operation and quarterly re-diagnosis
- **Success milestones**: Health score improvement targets that trigger retainer renewal
- **Risk sharing**: If health scores do not improve by agreed thresholds within 180 days, monitoring retainer is waived until improvement is demonstrated

This pricing model directly counters the "billable hour" incentive misalignment that kills traditional consulting engagements. [src3]

**Verify**: Pricing proposal includes explicit risk-sharing clause. Client should never feel they are paying for time — only for measurable health improvement.
**If failed**: If client procurement requires time-based billing, structure as "time with performance bonus" — base rate covers costs, bonus tied to outcomes.

## Quality Benchmarks

| Quality Metric | Minimum Acceptable | Good | Excellent |
|---------------|-------------------|------|-----------|
| Narrative clarity (can audience state takeaway per section?) | 60% of sections clear | 80% | 100% |
| Data backing (% of claims with quantified evidence) | > 70% | > 85% | > 95% |
| Visual accessibility (WCAG 2.1 AA compliance) | Color contrast passes | + alt text | + screen reader tested |
| Remediation specificity (% of phases with quantified targets) | > 50% | > 75% | 100% |
| Client engagement (questions asked during presentation) | > 3 questions | > 6 | > 10 (deep engagement) |

**If below minimum**: If narrative clarity is below 60%, restructure around the four-act framework before presenting. Never present with unclear narrative — it is worse than delaying.

## Error Handling

| Error | Likely Cause | Recovery Action |
|-------|-------------|----------------|
| Health scores seem implausible (all dimensions above 0.8) | Sampling bias or insufficient data collection period | Extend baseline measurement to 60 days. Cross-validate with qualitative interviews. |
| Network topology shows no clear bottlenecks | Organization may be genuinely healthy, or ONA data lacks granularity | Verify data source captures all communication channels (not just email). If confirmed healthy, present as strength. |
| Client rejects outcome-based pricing | Procurement requires time-based contracts | Offer "time + performance bonus" hybrid. Base rate at 60% of normal T&M, 40% tied to health score improvement. |
| Executive audience disengages during technical network visualizations | Too much technical detail for audience level | Switch to team-level aggregated view. Replace force-directed graph with simplified department connectivity matrix. |
| Before/after comparison shows regression | Intervention may have caused unintended disruption | Present honestly. Diagnose which intervention caused regression and propose correction. Transparency builds trust. |

## Anti-Patterns

### Wrong: Presenting raw data without narrative structure
Dumping health scores, heat maps, and network graphs on executives without a connecting story is the digital equivalent of the 50-page slide deck — technically thorough but functionally useless. Pfeffer and Sutton's research shows this creates a "knowing-doing gap" where the client nods but never acts. [src1]

### Correct: Build every presentation around the four-act diagnostic narrative (Patient, Diagnosis, Treatment, Prognosis)
Each visualization answers a specific question. The audience should never wonder "so what?" — every chart flows into an actionable implication. [src5]

### Wrong: Using individual employee names in network topology visualizations
Presenting "Bob is a bottleneck" to a room of executives triggers defensive political reactions and kills trust. It turns a structural diagnosis into a personal attack. [src2]

### Correct: Use anonymized role-based identifiers or team-level aggregation
Show "Engineering Team Lead (Node #7) has 4.2x average centrality" rather than naming individuals. The structural insight is identical; the political risk is eliminated.

### Wrong: Proposing time-and-materials retainers after presenting OIA findings
If the diagnosis shows the organization is "bleeding" from structural defects and the remedy is continuous monitoring, billing by the hour creates the exact perverse incentive the OIA methodology was designed to eliminate. [src3]

### Correct: Always propose outcome-based (metabolic recovery) pricing tied to health score improvement
The consultant's revenue should depend on solving the problem, not studying it. This aligns incentives and differentiates OIA from traditional consulting.
