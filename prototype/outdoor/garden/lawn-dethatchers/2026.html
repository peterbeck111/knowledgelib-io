What are the best lawn dethatchers in 2026?

Summary

The 2026 dethatcher market splits cleanly into four product categories. Corded electric walk-behinds (Sun Joe AJ801E, Greenworks 27022, Worx WG850, Earthwise DT71613) dominate suburban yards under 10,000 sq ft — Bob Vila, Reviewed.com, and Family Handyman all converge on the Sun Joe AJ801E (~$160) as the consensus pick for affordability and effectiveness, with over 3,200 Amazon reviews making it the most-purchased model in the category [src1, src2, src7]. Cordless 40V models like the Greenworks DHF410 (15 in., ~$200 tool only) are gaining ground but reviewers still warn they lack torque for heavy thatch loads — Bob Vila lists Greenworks as an "Upgrade Pick" rather than the overall winner, and Reviewed.com still recommends sticking with corded for serious work [src1, src2]. Tow-behind dethatchers are the only sane choice above 1/2 acre: the Agri-Fab 45-0295 (48 in., 24 tines, ~$190) wins on durability while the Brinly DT-480BH-A (48 in., 24 tines, inline-wheel design) is the only top-tier tow-behind compatible with zero-turn mowers [src3, src5]. Manual thatch rakes (Ames 2915100, Walensee 15-inch) remain the right answer for spot work and lawns under ~1,500 sq ft [src1, src4].

The single most important spec is working width — 13–16 in. for walk-behind electric, 40–48 in. for tow-behind — because dethatching is a 3-pass job (two perpendicular passes plus a cleanup pass) and time scales linearly with width. Tine count (typically 18–24 spring-steel tines for tow-behinds, ~45 zinc-plated tines for the widest electric Earthwise DT71613) and depth adjustment (3 positions on most models, 5 on the Greenworks DHF410) are secondary [src1, src3]. Dethatching is seasonal: cool-season grasses in early spring or early fall, warm-season in late spring after green-up. Dethatching during dormancy or summer heat damages turf [src3, src8].

Best for Each Use Case

Best Overall: Sun Joe AJ801E (~$160) — Check price

The consensus pick across Reviewed.com, Family Handyman, and BestReviews. 12-amp motor, 13-in. cutting path, 5-position depth control, included 8-gallon collection bag. Over 3,200 Amazon reviews make it the most widely purchased dethatcher in the category — universally praised for combining effectiveness with a price point that undercuts most competitors. Reviewed.com calls it the best for users "looking to scarify their lawn" thanks to its detachable scarifier blade. [src2, src6, src7]

Best for Small Lawns / Under 5,000 sq ft: Worx WG850 (~$130) — Check price

Lightweight at 18.6 lb (10+ lb less than the Sun Joe), 14-in. cutting width, 3-depth adjust (-9 mm, -3 mm, +3 mm), 12-amp motor, folding handle for storage. Reviewed.com's pick for medium-sized yards where maneuverability matters more than raw power. [src2]

Best for Large Lawns (Tow-Behind): Agri-Fab 45-0295 48-Inch (~$190) — Check price

Top pick across Reviewed.com, Lawn Care Gurus, and Family Handyman for half-acre and larger lawns. 48-in. working width, 24 heat-treated spring-steel tines (rust-proof and replaceable), 70-lb weight tray for variable depth, universal hitch fits any garden tractor. 3-year limited warranty. Made in USA. [src2, src3, src5]

Best Electric for Heavy Thatch: Earthwise DT71613 (~$190) — Check price

13-amp motor with 16-in. working width — the widest corded electric in this list. 45 zinc-plated piano-wire spring pins reach -0.4 in. to +0.4 in. depth (deeper than the Sun Joe's range). Family Handyman highlights it for users who want gas-like cutting power without emissions. [src3]

Best Cordless: Greenworks 40V 15-Inch DHF410 (~$200 tool only) — Check price

Bob Vila's "Upgrade Pick." 15-in. brushless dethatcher/scarifier with quick-swap tine reels, 5-position depth control. Bob Vila's testers were able to "pull up armfuls of thatch with each step" — performing as well as or better than the corded models tested. Battery shared across 75+ Greenworks 40V tools. Caveat: roughly 2× the price of comparable corded models, and battery runtime limits coverage to ~5,000–8,000 sq ft per charge. [src1]

Best Manual / Small Lawn: Ames 2915100 15-Inch Adjustable Thatch Rake (~$45) — Check price

Bob Vila's overall pick for manual rakes. 15-in. head with 19 curved steel tines, hardwood handle, adjustable head angle. Effective on light-to-moderate thatch in small yards (under ~1,500 sq ft) where buying a powered tool would be overkill. Built to last per Bob Vila testing. [src4]

Best Budget Tow-Behind: Craftsman CMXGZBF7124315 40-Inch (~$140) — Check price

40-in. working width, 20 heat-treated spring tines, 31 lb. Lawn Care Gurus' budget pick — the cheapest top-tier tow-behind ($-tier) for small lawns and occasional use. [src5]

Best for Zero-Turn Mowers: Brinly DT-480BH-A 48-Inch (~$220) — Check price

The only top-tier tow-behind designed for ZTR compatibility, thanks to inline transport wheels that minimize footprint and allow tight turns without binding. 48-in. working width, two rows of 12 independently flexing spring-steel tines, single universal hitch pin attaches to any rider, tractor, ZTR, or ATV. Tractor Supply and Lowe's both stock it. Choose this over the Agri-Fab 45-0295 if you mow with a zero-turn. [src5]

Best Value Manual: Walensee 15-Inch Thatch Rake (~$30) — Check price

Bob Vila lists it as "Also Consider." 19 curved stainless-steel tines, anti-rust coating, adjustable handle length. Lighter than the Ames at ~3 lb and roughly 30% cheaper. [src4]

Decision Logic

If lawn is under 1,500 sq ft or thatch is patchy

→ Ames 2915100 (~$45) or Walensee 15-Inch (~$30). A powered dethatcher's setup, storage, and noise overhead aren't worth it for occasional spot work. [src1, src4]

If lawn is 1,500–10,000 sq ft and you have an outdoor outlet

→ Sun Joe AJ801E (~$160). The category default — most-purchased, consensus pick across Reviewed.com, Family Handyman, and BestReviews. Step up to the Earthwise DT71613 (~$190, 16-in. width) only if thatch is severe (over 3/4 in.). [src1, src2, src6, src7]

If lawn is 10,000 sq ft – 1/2 acre and you want cordless freedom

→ Greenworks 40V 15-Inch DHF410 (~$200 tool only). Bob Vila's upgrade pick. Caveat: roughly 2× the cost of corded equivalents and battery runtime tops out around 5,000–8,000 sq ft per charge — buy a second 40V battery if covering more. [src1]

If lawn is over 1/2 acre and you own a riding mower / lawn tractor

→ Agri-Fab 45-0295 48-Inch (~$190). Top tow-behind pick across 3 of 4 reviewed sources. If you mow with a zero-turn, swap to the Brinly DT-480BH-A instead — Agri-Fab's tow-behinds are not designed for ZTR turn radii. [src2, src3, src5]

If you mow with a zero-turn mower

→ Brinly DT-480BH-A (48 in.) or Brinly DT-40BH (40 in.). The inline transport-wheel design is the only top-tier tow-behind that handles ZTR turns without binding. Agri-Fab explicitly does not warrant their dethatchers for ZTR use. [src5]

If thatch layer is under 1/2 in.

→ Skip a dedicated dethatcher entirely. Routine mowing at the right height + a vigorous pass with a regular leaf rake is sufficient. Dethatching unnecessarily damages healthy turf. [src3, src8]

Default recommendation (unknown lawn size)

→ Sun Joe AJ801E (~$160). Safest pick when you don't know the user's lawn size — covers ~80% of suburban use cases (under 10,000 sq ft, walk-behind, corded). [src2, src7]

Key Market Trends (2026)

  • Cordless 40V dethatchers are catching up but not winning yet: Bob Vila lists the Greenworks 40V 15-in. DHF410 as an "Upgrade Pick," not the overall winner. Reviewed.com still explicitly recommends sticking to corded models for serious thatch removal because battery torque drops under heavy load. Expect this gap to close by 2027 as 60V/80V platforms (e.g., Greenworks Pro, EGO) move into the category. [src1, src2]
  • 16-inch corded class establishes itself: The Earthwise DT71613 (16 in., 13 amp, 45 wire tines) and similar 16-in. models are pushing past the long-standing 13–14 in. corded standard set by the Sun Joe AJ801E and Greenworks 27022. Wider cuts cut a 5,000-sq-ft job from ~3 hours to ~2.25 hours. [src3]
  • Brinly's inline-wheel design owns the ZTR niche: With zero-turn mowers now ~40% of new ride-on sales, Brinly's tow-behind line — designed around inline transport wheels for tight turns — is the category's only ZTR-compatible top-tier option. Agri-Fab still leads 48-in. tow-behinds for traditional lawn tractors but explicitly does not warrant ZTR use. [src5]
  • Combo lawn sweeper + dethatcher attachments expanding: Brinly's STS-42BHDK-A (42-in. lawn sweeper with integrated dethatcher) and the new 54-in. LSDT-54BH combine two passes into one — appealing for autumn fescue/Kentucky-bluegrass overseeding workflows where you dethatch, sweep, then seed. [src8]
  • Manual thatch rakes still hold the under-1,500-sq-ft segment: Despite the rise of $130 electrics, Bob Vila's small-lawn pick is still a $45 Ames manual rake. Setup/storage/noise overhead of a powered tool isn't worth it for spot work. [src1, src4]

Important Caveats

  • Prices are approximate U.S. street prices as of April 2026. Sun Joe and Greenworks corded models in particular fluctuate $20–$40 around major lawn-care holidays (Memorial Day, July 4, Labor Day) on Amazon.
  • Tow-behind dethatchers (Agri-Fab, Brinly, Craftsman) are only useful if you already own a riding mower, lawn tractor, or ZTR with a hitch. They have no value for push-mower-only households.
  • The "scarifier" function on combo dethatcher/scarifier units (Sun Joe AJ801E-SCRF blade, Greenworks DHF410, Worx WG850) cuts deeper into living turf than a tine reel and should only be used on heavily compacted lawns at the start of an overseeding cycle. Misused, it kills healthy grass. [src8]
  • Manufacturer "amp" ratings vs. real cutting torque don't always correlate — the 12-amp Sun Joe AJ801E outperforms several 13-amp competitors in side-by-side testing per Reviewed.com and Family Handyman, mostly because of better tine geometry and tine count. [src2, src7]
  • Dethatching season is narrow: cool-season grasses (Kentucky bluegrass, fescue, ryegrass) in early spring or early fall; warm-season grasses (Bermuda, Zoysia) in late spring after green-up. Dethatching during dormancy or summer heat causes lasting turf damage. [src3]
