# Best 1440p Gaming Monitors (2026)

## Summary

The 1440p gaming monitor market in April 2026 is dominated by 4th-generation Tandem OLED panels and an unprecedented OLED price collapse. The best overall 1440p gaming monitor is now the ASUS ROG Swift OLED PG27AQWP-W (~$1,100), a 540Hz 4th-gen Tandem WOLED with 0.02ms response time, DisplayPort 2.1a UHBR20, DisplayHDR True Black 500, and 380 nits sustained full-screen brightness -- a 49% increase over its predecessor. For 480Hz at a lower price, the LG UltraGear 27GX790B-B (~$800) delivers 540Hz native via 4th-gen Tandem WOLED, while the LG 27GX790A (~$680) offers 480Hz WOLED at the most affordable high-refresh OLED price point yet. [src1, src4, src5]

The biggest shift since Q1 2026 is the arrival of sub-$350 OLED monitors. The Alienware AW2726DM (~$350), launched April 2026, delivers 240Hz QD-OLED with 0.03ms response time, 111% DCI-P3, and a 3-year burn-in warranty at the lowest OLED price ever -- undercutting even budget IPS panels. The AOC Q27G4ZD (~$449) adds another budget QD-OLED option at 240Hz with HDR True Black 400 and dual DisplayPort. On the high end, the MSI MPG 271QR X50 (~$700) pushes QD-OLED to 500Hz with USB-C 98W PD and DisplayPort 2.1a. Budget non-OLED options remain strong: the Gigabyte M27Q3 (~$270) delivers 320Hz IPS, while the AOC Q27G40XMN (~$280) pairs Mini-LED with 1,152 dimming zones for genuine HDR at a fraction of OLED pricing. [src2, src3, src6]

## Best for Each Use Case

### Best Overall: ASUS ROG Swift OLED PG27AQWP-W (~$1,100) -- [Check price](https://knowledgelib.io/go/asus-rog-swift-pg27aqwp-w)
The PG27AQWP-W is the new flagship 1440p gaming monitor, succeeding the PG27AQDP. Its 4th-gen Tandem WOLED panel delivers 540Hz at native 1440p (720Hz at 720p) with a 0.02ms response time, 109% DCI-P3, and DisplayHDR True Black 500. Full-screen brightness reaches 380 nits sustained -- a 49% increase over its predecessor -- with 1,500 nits HDR peak. DisplayPort 2.1a UHBR20 provides 80Gbps bandwidth for uncompressed 540Hz. Three USB 3.2 downstream ports, ASUS OLED Care Pro with Neo Proximity Sensor, and a premium semi-transparent chassis complete the package. [src1, src5, src8]

### Best for Competitive FPS: LG UltraGear 27GX790B-B (~$800) -- [Check price](https://knowledgelib.io/go/lg-27gx790b)
LG's 4th-gen Primary RGB Tandem WOLED delivers 540Hz at native 1440p with a 0.02ms response time and dual-mode 720Hz at 720p. DisplayPort 2.1 UHBR20 with full 80Gbps bandwidth supports 1440p at 540Hz without compression. At 335 nits sustained SDR brightness with DisplayHDR True Black 500, it is among the brightest 1440p OLEDs available. The TrueBlack glossy coating enhances contrast and eliminates the purple-tint issue seen on older panels. Originally $1,000, now ~$800 at retailers. [src1, src4]

### Best Premium Value: ASUS ROG Swift PG27AQDP (~$730) -- [Check price](https://knowledgelib.io/go/asus-rog-swift-pg27aqdp)
The PG27AQDP has dropped from $1,000 to ~$730, making it the best value 480Hz OLED. Its WOLED panel delivers perfect blacks with a 1,500,000:1 contrast ratio, 99% DCI-P3 coverage, and true 10-bit color depth. The 480Hz refresh rate combined with 0.03ms response time results in virtually no motion blur. ASUS calibrates each unit individually, and the custom heatsink design maintains performance without fan noise. At its current price, it undercuts the LG 27GX790A while offering superior calibration. [src1, src3]

### Best 4th-Gen OLED Value: ASUS ROG Strix XG27AQWMG (~$650) -- [Check price](https://knowledgelib.io/go/asus-rog-strix-xg27aqwmg)
The first affordable 4th-gen Tandem OLED, the XG27AQWMG delivers 280Hz, 0.03ms, DisplayHDR True Black 500, and ASUS TrueBlack Glossy coating at $650. Its Tandem OLED technology provides 15% higher peak brightness (up to 1,600 nits HDR highlight), 25% wider color volume, and 60% longer lifespan vs. prior WOLED. RGB stripe subpixel layout ensures sharp text for mixed gaming and productivity. G-Sync compatible with Neo Proximity Sensor for automatic standby. [src4, src8]

### Best Brightness OLED: Gigabyte MO27Q28G (~$550) -- [Check price](https://knowledgelib.io/go/gigabyte-mo27q28g)
The MO27Q28G records the highest SDR brightness of any OLED gaming monitor at 341 nits sustained, with 1,500 nits peak HDR brightness and DisplayHDR True Black 500 certification. Its 4th-gen LG Tandem WOLED panel at 280Hz with 0.03ms delivers 99.5% DCI-P3 and 84% BT.2020 coverage. USB-C with KVM, HDMI 2.1, and G-Sync compatibility round out the feature set. At $550, it undercuts the ASUS XG27AQWMG while matching its panel generation. [src3, src4]

### Best Feature-Rich QD-OLED: MSI MPG 271QR X50 (~$700) -- [Check price](https://knowledgelib.io/go/msi-mpg-271qr-x50)
MSI's flagship pushes QD-OLED to 500Hz with DisplayPort 2.1a UHBR20, USB-C 98W PD, KVM switch, and DisplayHDR True Black 500. Color coverage reaches 108% DCI-P3, and the built-in NPU powers OLED Care 3.0 for intelligent burn-in prevention. HDMI 2.1 supports 4K 120Hz for console gaming. At ~$700 (down from $899 MSRP), it offers the most connectivity of any QD-OLED gaming monitor. 3-year burn-in warranty included. [src4, src7]

### Best Budget QD-OLED: AOC Q27G4ZD (~$449) -- [Check price](https://knowledgelib.io/go/aoc-q27g4zd)
The Q27G4ZD delivers genuine QD-OLED picture quality at $449 -- significantly less than competing OLEDs. Its 240Hz refresh rate with 0.03ms response time provides excellent motion clarity, and color coverage reaches 110% DCI-P3. Dual DisplayPort and dual HDMI 2.0 ports offer flexible connectivity. The main trade-offs vs. pricier OLEDs are limited to HDMI 2.0 (no 4K 120Hz console support) and slightly lower brightness. 3-year zero-bright-dot warranty included. [src3, src6]

### Cheapest OLED: Alienware AW2726DM (~$350) -- [Check price](https://knowledgelib.io/go/alienware-aw2726dm)
Launched April 2026, the AW2726DM is the cheapest 1440p OLED monitor ever made. Its 3rd-gen QD-OLED panel delivers 240Hz with 0.03ms response time and 111% DCI-P3 color coverage at a price that undercuts most IPS monitors. The updated subpixel layout improves text clarity over older QD-OLEDs. Trade-offs are significant: 200 nits full-screen brightness, no USB ports, no speakers, and HDMI limited to 120Hz. Best for dark-room gamers who want OLED blacks at the absolute lowest cost. 3-year burn-in warranty. [src6]

### Best Budget High-Refresh: Gigabyte M27Q3 (~$270) -- [Check price](https://knowledgelib.io/go/gigabyte-m27q3)
At $270, the M27Q3 is the most affordable 320Hz 1440p monitor available, making ultra-high refresh rate gaming accessible to budget-conscious buyers. Its SuperSpeed IPS panel delivers 1ms response time with well-optimized overdrive, while HDMI 2.1 ports support VRR for console gaming. G-Sync and FreeSync Premium Pro compatibility, a USB-C port with KVM, and a fully adjustable stand add meaningful value. Contrast and HDR are average for IPS, but motion clarity rivals monitors costing three times as much. [src2, src3]

### Best Budget HDR: AOC Q27G40XMN (~$280) -- [Check price](https://knowledgelib.io/go/aoc-q27g40xmn)
The AOC Q27G40XMN is the only sub-$300 monitor delivering genuine HDR performance, thanks to its 1,152-zone Mini-LED backlight with Quantum Dot film. The VA panel achieves a native contrast ratio of ~4,800:1, which climbs to nearly 34,000:1 with local dimming enabled. Color volume exceeds 104% DCI-P3, and peak brightness surpasses 1,300 nits in HDR mode. At 180Hz with Adaptive-Sync, it is not the fastest option, but its HDR picture quality at this price point is unmatched. Best for bright rooms where OLED brightness falls short. [src2, src3]

## Important Caveats

- Prices listed are approximate US street prices as of April 2026. Prices fluctuate frequently; the ASUS PG27AQDP has been seen as low as ~$685, the Alienware AW2725D at ~$400, and the LG 27GX790A at ~$680 (down from $999 MSRP). Verify current pricing before purchase.
- OLED monitors carry a burn-in risk with static content (taskbars, HUDs). All models listed include manufacturer-level burn-in mitigation (pixel shift, logo dimming, screen savers) and 3-year burn-in warranties. 4th-gen Tandem OLED panels claim 60% longer lifespan, but long-term burn-in data is still limited.
- QD-OLED panels (Samsung, MSI, Alienware, AOC) use a triangle subpixel layout that can cause color fringing on small text. WOLED panels (ASUS ROG Swift, LG UltraGear, Gigabyte MO27Q28G) have a traditional RGB stripe layout with better text clarity -- important for mixed gaming and productivity use. Newer 3rd-gen QD-OLED (AW2726DM, Q27G4ZD) use an updated subpixel structure that reduces this issue.
- Refresh rates above 360Hz require a high-end GPU (NVIDIA RTX 4080+ or AMD RX 9070 XT+) to drive competitive titles at 1440p. Most single-player games will not reach 480Hz+ even on flagship hardware.
- Response time figures (0.02ms-0.03ms GtG) are manufacturer-specified for OLED panels. Independent lab measurements confirm these are accurate, as OLED pixel transitions are near-instantaneous.
- HDR brightness varies significantly: 4th-gen Tandem OLEDs reach 341-380 nits sustained vs. 200 nits for budget OLEDs, while the AOC Q27G40XMN Mini-LED hits 1,300+ nits. In bright rooms, Mini-LED remains the superior choice for visible HDR impact.
- The Alienware AW2726DM at $350 is a genuine QD-OLED but limited to 200 nits full-screen, no USB ports, and HDMI capped at 120Hz -- buyers needing connectivity or brightness should budget $430+ for the Alienware AW2725D or $449+ for the AOC Q27G4ZD.
