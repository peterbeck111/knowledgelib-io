# Leveraged Buyout (LBO) Framework

## Definition

A leveraged buyout (LBO) is the acquisition of a company using a significant amount of borrowed money (debt) to meet the purchase price, with the target company's assets and cash flows serving as collateral and repayment source. The LBO model is the financial framework used to evaluate whether such a transaction can achieve the private equity sponsor's target returns (typically 20-25% IRR) over a 3-7 year holding period. [src1] The core mechanic is financial leverage: by funding 60-80% of the purchase price with debt and only 20-40% with equity, the PE firm amplifies returns on its equity investment as the company's cash flows pay down the debt. [src2]

## Key Properties

- **Capital structure**: Typically 60-80% debt, 20-40% equity. Modern deals trend toward 60/40 split (vs 80/20 in 1980s). [src4]
- **Target IRR**: 20-25% annualized return over 3-7 year holding period. [src1]
- **Return metric**: Multiple of Money (MoM) — typically 2.0-3.0x for a successful deal over 5 years. [src3]
- **Three return levers**: (1) EBITDA growth (revenue growth + margin expansion), (2) Debt paydown (deleveraging), (3) Multiple expansion (selling at a higher multiple than entry). [src3]
- **Ideal target profile**: Stable cash flows, low capex requirements, defensible market position, potential for operational improvement, and strong management team. [src2]
- **Debt capacity benchmark**: Senior Debt/EBITDA typically 3-5x; Total Debt/EBITDA 4-6x; Interest coverage ratio >2x. [src4]

## Constraints

- LBO targets must generate stable, predictable free cash flows to service mandatory debt payments — companies with high revenue volatility, heavy cyclicality, or significant capex needs are poor candidates. [src1]
- The model assumes a definite exit within 3-7 years, meaning the investment thesis must include a credible path to sale, IPO, or recapitalization. [src1]
- Leverage ratios are constrained by lender appetite and credit markets — in tight credit environments, maximum leverage drops from 6x to 4x EBITDA, fundamentally changing deal economics. [src4]
- LBO returns are asymmetric: if the business underperforms, equity is wiped out first (due to debt priority), making downside scenarios far more severe than in an unlevered acquisition. [src2]
- The framework assumes debt can be refinanced or repaid — interest rate spikes or covenant violations can force restructuring even if the underlying business is performing. [src3]

## Application Checklist

### Step 1: Determine entry valuation and sources & uses
- **Inputs needed**: Target EBITDA, comparable transaction multiples, debt market terms (rates, covenants), equity check size
- **Output**: Purchase price, sources of funds (senior debt, mezzanine, equity), uses of funds (equity purchase, fees, working capital)
- **Constraint**: Entry multiple must be supportable by comparable transactions — paying a premium above market comps requires explicit justification for operational improvement or synergies [src1]

### Step 2: Build operating projections (3-7 year forecast)
- **Inputs needed**: Historical financials (3-5 years), revenue growth assumptions, margin expansion thesis, capex requirements, working capital needs
- **Output**: Projected income statement, balance sheet, and free cash flow statement
- **Constraint**: Growth assumptions must be grounded in the investment thesis — the base case should not require heroic assumptions. Stress-test with downside scenario at -20% EBITDA [src2]

### Step 3: Model debt schedule and cash flow waterfall
- **Inputs needed**: Debt tranches (senior, mezzanine, subordinated), interest rates, amortization schedules, mandatory vs optional repayment
- **Output**: Year-by-year debt balance, interest expense, and free cash flow available for debt repayment
- **Constraint**: Maintain interest coverage ratio >2x and Debt/EBITDA declining trajectory throughout hold period. If any year breaches covenants, restructure the capital structure [src3]

### Step 4: Calculate exit valuation and returns
- **Inputs needed**: Exit year EBITDA, exit multiple assumption, remaining debt at exit
- **Output**: IRR and MoM for the equity investment
- **Constraint**: Exit multiple should equal or be slightly below entry multiple as base case — multiple expansion is upside, not the core thesis. If the deal only works with multiple expansion, the risk is too high [src1, src3]

## Anti-Patterns

### Wrong: Banking on multiple expansion as the primary return driver
Modeling a deal where the entry multiple is 10x EBITDA and the exit multiple is 13x, with most returns coming from the multiple increase rather than EBITDA growth or deleveraging. This approach is speculative and has a poor track record. [src1]

### Correct: Building returns primarily from EBITDA growth and deleveraging
Model the base case with a flat or slightly declining exit multiple. Target at least two-thirds of returns from operational improvement and debt paydown. Multiple expansion should be treated as upside optionality. [src1]

### Wrong: Ignoring downside scenarios and covenant analysis
Building only a base case and upside case without testing what happens if EBITDA declines 20-30%. In LBO structures, the debt magnifies downside risk and covenant breaches can trigger accelerated repayment. [src2]

### Correct: Stress-testing with realistic downside scenarios
Run at minimum three scenarios (base, upside, downside). The downside case should assume a 20-30% EBITDA decline and test whether covenants hold and interest can be serviced. If the downside case results in covenant breach, reduce leverage. [src2, src3]

### Wrong: Using LBO valuation for growth-stage or pre-profit companies
Attempting to apply LBO analysis to a company with negative or highly volatile EBITDA, where the fundamental premise of debt-funded acquisition financed by stable cash flows does not hold. [src3]

### Correct: Restricting LBO analysis to appropriate targets
Apply LBO analysis only to companies with stable, positive EBITDA (typically >$10M), predictable cash flows, limited cyclicality, and reasonable capex requirements. For growth-stage companies, use venture capital or growth equity valuation methods instead. [src1]

## Common Misconceptions

- **Misconception**: LBOs are primarily about financial engineering and cost-cutting.
  **Reality**: Modern PE firms generate the majority of returns through revenue growth and operational improvement, not leverage alone. The 1980s model of pure financial engineering has largely given way to operational value creation as the primary return driver. [src1]

- **Misconception**: Higher leverage always means higher returns.
  **Reality**: While leverage amplifies returns, it also amplifies risk. The optimal leverage level balances return enhancement against default probability. Overleveraged deals (>6x Debt/EBITDA) have significantly higher failure rates and do not reliably deliver superior returns. [src4]

- **Misconception**: The exit multiple should be higher than the entry multiple.
  **Reality**: Conservative LBO modeling assumes a flat or declining exit multiple. Returns should be driven by EBITDA growth and debt paydown. Multiple expansion is possible but should not be the thesis — it represents market timing, not value creation. [src3]
