# FP&A Maturity Assessment

## Purpose

This assessment evaluates the maturity of a company's Financial Planning & Analysis function across five critical dimensions: budgeting process, forecasting accuracy, scenario modeling, management reporting, and decision support integration. The output is a composite maturity score (1-5) that identifies the weakest capabilities and routes to specific improvement actions. Use this when diagnosing why financial planning is reactive rather than strategic, preparing for a CFO transition, or benchmarking the FP&A function against industry standards. [src1]

## Constraints
<!-- Agents: read before running this assessment with a user. -->

- Requires access to budget-vs-actual variance reports and at least 12 months of forecasting accuracy history for reliable scoring
- Not meaningful for pre-revenue startups or companies with fewer than $2M annual revenue
- Self-assessment bias: finance teams consistently over-score by 0.5-1.0 points — always require documented evidence, not opinions
- Assessment is diagnostic only — it identifies the current state but does not prescribe solutions
- Re-run semi-annually; FP&A maturity shifts significantly with new planning tools, leadership changes, or growth stages

## Assessment Dimensions

<!-- Each dimension is scored independently. The structured format lets agents
     walk through this conversationally with a user, one dimension at a time. -->

### Dimension 1: Budgeting Process

**What this measures**: How effectively the organization creates, manages, and iterates on its annual budget and periodic reforecasts.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | Budget is a spreadsheet exercise done annually with no process ownership; takes 3+ months to complete | Budget exists only as disconnected spreadsheets; no version control; budget owner unclear |
| 2 | Emerging | Structured annual budget process with department input, but still primarily spreadsheet-based; takes 8-12 weeks | Templates exist; departments submit inputs; consolidation is manual; one reforecast per year |
| 3 | Defined | Formalized budgeting process with driver-based inputs, clear timeline, and 2-3 reforecasts per year; takes 4-8 weeks | Documented budget calendar; driver-based assumptions; planning tool in use; quarterly reforecasts |
| 4 | Managed | Rolling budget or continuous planning with monthly reforecasts; cycle time under 4 weeks; variance analysis automated | Rolling 12-18 month budget; automated variance reporting; department owners self-serve budget updates |
| 5 | Optimized | Zero-based or continuous planning with real-time budget updates; AI-assisted allocation; cycle time under 2 weeks | Continuous planning platform; predictive budget recommendations; real-time spend tracking vs. plan |

**Red flags**: Budget process takes more than 12 weeks; no reforecasts during the year; budget is never referenced after approval. [src1]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "How many times per year do you reforecast, and how long does the process take?"

### Dimension 2: Forecasting Accuracy

**What this measures**: The accuracy, speed, and methodology of financial forecasts across revenue, expenses, and cash flow.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | No formal forecasting; plans are static annual budgets; variance is measured only at year-end | No forecast documents exist; variance analysis is annual; accuracy is not tracked |
| 2 | Emerging | Quarterly forecasts based on historical run rates; accuracy tracked but not reported; MAPE above 20% | Quarterly forecast exists; formula-based projections; no rolling horizon; accuracy > 20% error |
| 3 | Defined | Monthly or rolling forecasts using driver-based models; MAPE 10-20%; forecasts extend 6+ months; formal accuracy tracking | Driver-based models; rolling 6-12 month horizon; accuracy reports distributed; MAPE 10-20% |
| 4 | Managed | Rolling 12-18 month forecasts with multiple scenarios; MAPE 5-10%; probabilistic ranges provided; 63% of teams struggle beyond 6 months | Multi-scenario models; probabilistic forecasts; MAPE 5-10%; automated data feeds from source systems |
| 5 | Optimized | Continuous forecasting with AI/ML augmentation; MAPE under 5%; real-time scenario simulation; forecast finalization under 3 days | AI-augmented models; real-time data integration; MAPE < 5%; forecast-to-close cycle under 72 hours |

**Red flags**: Forecasts are never compared to actuals; forecast is a single point estimate with no range; revenue forecast consistently misses by more than 15%. [src2]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "What is your forecast accuracy (MAPE) over the last 4 quarters, and do you track it formally?"

### Dimension 3: Scenario Modeling

**What this measures**: The organization's ability to model different business scenarios, stress tests, and what-if analyses to support decision-making.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | No scenario modeling; only a single plan exists; stress testing is done informally if at all | No scenario documents; "what-if" questions answered ad hoc via manual spreadsheet changes |
| 2 | Emerging | Basic best/worst/base case models exist but are maintained manually; used only during annual planning | Three-scenario model exists but is static; updated only during budget season; limited variables |
| 3 | Defined | Multiple scenario models with linked assumptions; updated quarterly; cover revenue, cost, and cash flow | Linked scenario models; 5-10 key variables; quarterly updates; used in board presentations |
| 4 | Managed | Dynamic scenario models with real-time assumption updates; Monte Carlo or probabilistic approaches; used in strategic decisions | Dynamic models in planning tools; probability-weighted scenarios; used for M&A, hiring, capex decisions |
| 5 | Optimized | Real-time scenario simulation with AI-driven sensitivity analysis; embedded in operational decision-making; executive decision-making time reduced by 20%+ | Real-time scenario dashboards; AI-driven insights; decision-support workflow integration; <24hr turnaround |

**Red flags**: Only one version of the plan exists; leadership cannot answer "what happens if revenue drops 20%?" within 24 hours. [src4]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "How many active scenarios do you maintain, and how quickly can you model the impact of a major assumption change?"

### Dimension 4: Management Reporting

**What this measures**: The quality, timeliness, and actionability of financial reports delivered to management and the board.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | Financial reports are produced manually, often late, inconsistent format; no standard reporting package | Reports are ad hoc spreadsheets; format changes each month; delivered 3+ weeks after close |
| 2 | Emerging | Standardized monthly reporting package; delivered within 15 business days of close; primarily backward-looking | Monthly financial package exists; standard template; delivery 10-15 business days; mostly P&L focus |
| 3 | Defined | Comprehensive reporting within 10 business days; includes KPIs, variance analysis, and commentary; board-ready | Board deck with financials + KPIs; 7-10 day close-to-report cycle; variance commentary included |
| 4 | Managed | Automated dashboards with real-time or near-real-time data; self-service access for executives; 5-day close-to-report cycle | BI dashboards (Tableau/Power BI); automated data pipelines; self-service analytics; 5-day delivery |
| 5 | Optimized | Predictive analytics embedded in reporting; automated narrative generation; continuous monitoring with exception alerts | AI-generated commentary; predictive metrics; automated alerts for threshold breaches; 2-3 day cycle |

**Red flags**: Monthly close takes more than 15 business days; board receives only a P&L with no operational KPIs; reports are not comparable period-over-period. [src6]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "How many business days after month-end do stakeholders receive the management reporting package?"

### Dimension 5: Decision Support & Business Partnership

**What this measures**: How effectively FP&A partners with business units to drive data-informed decisions rather than just producing reports.

| Score | Level | Description | Evidence |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Ad hoc | FP&A is purely a reporting function; no involvement in business decisions; reactive to data requests | FP&A team only produces reports; not invited to strategy meetings; no business unit alignment |
| 2 | Emerging | FP&A provides ad hoc analyses when requested; beginning to attend business reviews; limited proactive insight | Some ad hoc analyses completed; occasional business review attendance; insights are reactive |
| 3 | Defined | FP&A business partners assigned to major BUs; regular cadence of review meetings; proactive variance explanations | Named FP&A business partners; monthly BU reviews; proactive commentary on trends and variances |
| 4 | Managed | FP&A drives the strategic planning cycle; provides ROI analysis for major investments; influences capital allocation | FP&A leads strategic planning; investment committee involvement; unit economics analysis capabilities |
| 5 | Optimized | FP&A is a strategic partner embedded in all major decisions; predictive insights drive strategy; CEO/board rely on FP&A for scenario-based recommendations | FP&A at executive committee; predictive decision support; strategy co-creation; measurable value-add |

**Red flags**: FP&A is not invited to business review meetings; no analysis has influenced a major decision in the past 6 months; FP&A team spends more than 70% of time on report production. [src2, src5]
**Quick diagnostic question**: "When was the last time an FP&A analysis directly influenced a significant business decision?"

## Scoring & Interpretation

### Overall Score Calculation

All five dimensions are weighted equally, reflecting that world-class FP&A requires strength across all areas — a team cannot compensate for poor forecasting with excellent reporting.

```
Overall Score = (Budgeting + Forecasting + Scenario Modeling + Reporting + Decision Support) / 5
```

### Score Interpretation

| Overall Score | Maturity Level | Interpretation | Recommended Next Step |
|---------------|---------------|----------------|----------------------|
| 1.0 - 1.9 | Critical | FP&A function barely exists — organization is flying blind on financial planning | Build foundational budgeting process and hire dedicated FP&A resource |
| 2.0 - 2.9 | Developing | Basic processes exist but are manual, slow, and primarily backward-looking | Implement planning tool, establish rolling forecasts, track forecast accuracy |
| 3.0 - 3.9 | Competent | Solid FP&A foundation with room for automation and strategic partnership | Automate reporting, develop scenario capabilities, embed FP&A in business decisions |
| 4.0 - 4.5 | Advanced | Strong FP&A function driving strategic value; optimization opportunities exist | Deploy AI augmentation, reduce cycle times, build predictive capabilities |
| 4.6 - 5.0 | Best-in-class | FP&A is a strategic differentiator; continuous improvement focus | Maintain edge through innovation, benchmark externally, share best practices |

## Common Pitfalls in Assessment

- **Tool-maturity conflation**: Teams with expensive planning tools (Anaplan, Adaptive) often score themselves higher, but tool adoption does not equal process maturity. A team using Anaplan at 20% capacity scores lower than a team with excellent Excel-based processes. [src3]
- **Forecasting accuracy gaming**: Some teams narrow forecast windows to artificially improve accuracy metrics. A 3-month forecast with 5% MAPE is less valuable than a 12-month forecast with 12% MAPE. Always assess forecast horizon alongside accuracy. [src2]
- **Reporting quantity vs. quality**: Producing 50 reports monthly does not indicate maturity. Assess whether reports drive decisions, not whether they exist. The key question is "what decision did this report inform?" [src5]
- **Snapshot fallacy**: A single assessment is a point-in-time snapshot. Track semi-annually for trend analysis — a declining score may indicate leadership turnover or tool migration disruption.
